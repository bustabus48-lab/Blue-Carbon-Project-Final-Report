Executive Summary
This Inception Report presents the foundation for the effective implementation of the Blue
Carbon Ecosystem Assessment Consultancy in Ghana. The consultancy is designed to
support national climate goals by generating high-quality data on the carbon sequestration
potential of mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems, whiles strengthening local capacities
and stakeholder engagement in ecosystem monitoring and management.
The report outlines a clear project charter, methodology aligned with Intergovernmental
Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and Plan Vivo standards, and a robust framework for
monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV).
It presents a well-structured Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) integrated into a realistic
implementation schedule over an 8-month period, with a total contract sum of USD
497,552.50.
Key highlights of the report include:
 Methods and Standards: Scientifically sound approaches combining Earth
Observation applications, field surveys, joint community-based and Government
Agencies data collection aligned with IPCC best practices and Plan Vivo standards.
 Stakeholder Engagement and Capacity-Building: An inclusive plan that outlines the
roles of national institutions, local government authorities, CREMAs, traditional
authorities, and NGOs. It includes a detailed schedule of consultations, workshops,
and training programs designed to empower stakeholders and ensure local
ownership.
 Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) Framework: A clear system for
baseline data gathering and continuous ecosystem monitoring, with defined roles for
CREMAs and government agencies beyond the consultancy period.
 Risk Management Plan: Identification of potential environmental, technical, social,
and institutional risks with appropriate mitigation strategies and a detailed risk
register.
 Cost Management Plan: A transparent budget of USD 497,552.50 covering
personnel, data collection, training, logistics, stakeholder engagement, MRV
activities, and a contingency provision.
 Schedule Management Plan: A fully integrated timeline and work structure guiding
implementation, monitoring, and reporting responsibilities.
 Quality and Procurement Management Plans: Clearly defined quality
assurance/control measures, and procurement standards to ensure efficient, ethical,
and timely acquisition of goods and services necessary for the completion of the
assignment.

Plan Vivo and national standards, ensuring timely, transparent communication of

 Conclusion and Next Steps: A detailed roadmap for implementation beginning with

1.0 Project Charter
1.1 Project Objectives
The main goals of this consultancy are to:
1. Conduct thorough evaluations of Ghana's mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems
to understand their current condition and potential.
2. Measure and map how much carbon these ecosystems currently store, using globally
recognized standards such as Plan Vivo and IPCC guidelines.
3. Develop a clear, community-involved system for monitoring, reporting, and verifying
(MRV) the state of these ecosystems and their carbon storage.
4. Provide a platform that enables real-time monitoring of the various ecosystems for
prompt decision-making and actions
5. Identify and prioritize areas needing restoration and recommend appropriate
restoration methods.
6. Develop clear terms of reference to recruit an expert consultant to create a detailed
restoration strategy.
7. Build the capacity of local communities and government agencies, enabling them to
actively participate in managing and protecting these ecosystems.
1.2 Project Justification
Mangroves and tidal salt marshes are critical ecosystems along Ghana's coast, offering vital
benefits such as storing carbon to combat climate change, protecting biodiversity, and
supporting local livelihoods. However, these ecosystems face ongoing threats from human
activities, climate change impacts, and habitat loss, leading to significant environmental,
social and economic issues.
This project addresses these challenges by gathering comprehensive data and developing
practical strategies to restore and sustainably manage these ecosystems. This initiative
supports Ghana’s climate goals under the Paris Agreement and aligns with international
standards for carbon markets, ensuring that local communities benefit socially,
economically, and environmentally.

2.0 Methods and Standards
2.1 Introduction to the Methods
This project will use a combination of modern scientific methods, local knowledge, and
community involvement to assess the potential of mangroves and tidal salt marsh
ecosystems in Ghana to store carbon and support local livelihoods. The methods align with
globally recognized standards, specifically Plan Vivo and IPCC, to ensure accuracy,
transparency, and community participation.
2.2 Defining the Project Scope
The assessment will cover the entire coast of the country with specific interest in Songor
Ramsar Site (Volta Estuary), Keta Lagoon Complex, Densu Delta, Greater Amanzule
Wetlands, and Muni-Pumadze Ramsar Site in Winneba.
Detailed maps will be created to show exactly which areas are included and their
environmental status. Ecosystem areas considered will typically be 2 hectares or larger, but
smaller areas with high restoration potential (at least 5 hectares of potential restored area)
will also be included.
2.3 Baseline Scenario Establishment
Ecological Nest will first establish what the condition of these ecosystems would likely be if
no conservation or restoration actions were undertaken. This scenario provides a clear
benchmark against which the positive impacts of future interventions can be measured. The
baseline scenario will detail current ecosystem health, rate of loss, carbon storage levels,
and ongoing environmental pressures.
2.4 Mapping and Ecological Surveys
Earth observation technology will be used to collect and analyze detailed condition of
mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems. These tools will help map exact ecosystem
boundaries, highlight areas of degradation, and identify locations ideal for restoration.
Ground teams will also visit these areas to validate satellite data and conduct detailed
surveys of the vegetation, wildlife, soil quality, and other important ecological features.
2.5 Carbon Measurement and Accounting
Field teams will take soil and vegetation samples from the ecosystems to measure the
amount of carbon they store. The carbon measurement process will strictly follow
guidelines from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) 2019 refinement to
2006 Guidelines for National Green House Gas (GHG) Inventories (https://www.ipcc-
nggip.iges.or.jp/public/2019rf/index.html) and Plan Vivo standards
(https://www.planvivo.org/methodologies)to ensure accuracy and global recognition. The
data collected will then be used to calculate how much carbon is currently stored and how
much additional carbon could potentially be stored through effective conservation and
restoration actions.

2.6 Community and Socio-economic Involvement
Local communities will actively participate in the assessment process. Interviews, surveys,
and workshops will be conducted to understand local livelihoods, resource use, cultural
values, and economic opportunities related to these ecosystems. The goal is to ensure that
conservation actions also support local economic development, such as through sustainable
fishing, eco-tourism, and other ecosystem-friendly activities.
2.7 Restoration Recommendations
The assessment will identify specific methods for restoring degraded mangrove and salt
marsh areas, such as replanting native mangrove species, restoring natural water flows, and
promoting natural ecosystem recovery. These recommendations will be clearly documented
and will serve as the basis for detailed planning in a follow-up consultancy dedicated to
developing a comprehensive restoration strategy.
2.8 Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV)
A clear and simple monitoring system will be set up to regularly check the progress and
success of restoration and conservation actions. This system will involve regular satellite
monitoring, drone surveys, and joint Government Agencies/community-led field
assessments. Regular reports will document progress, ensuring transparency and allowing
stakeholders to verify the effectiveness of conservation efforts.
2.9 Risk Management
Potential risks, such as climate change impacts, ecosystem degradation, or community
participation challenges, will be clearly identified and managed. Strategies such as
establishing protected buffer areas, continuous community engagement, and contingency
plans will be put in place to minimize these risks.
2.10 Compliance with International Standards
The assessment methods and project activities will strictly comply with Plan Vivo and IPCC
standards. Plan Vivo focuses on community participation, transparency, and biodiversity
protection, while IPCC provides scientifically credible carbon accounting methods.
Comprehensive documentation and independent validation will ensure the project meets
these international standards.
2.11 Conclusion
This clear and accessible methodological approach ensures that the Blue Carbon Ecosystem
Assessment Consultancy is effective, inclusive, scientifically credible, and easily understood
by all stakeholders.

10.0 Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV)
Framework
10.1 Introduction
The MRV Framework outlines systematic procedures to monitor and report ecosystem
conditions, measure carbon sequestration, and ensure transparency and credibility. The
consultant's role over the 8-month contract will establish baseline data, protocols, and
capacity-building, while CREMAs and government agencies will continue ongoing monitoring
and reporting beyond the consultancy.
10.2 Objectives of MRV Framework
 Establish baseline conditions and standardized monitoring protocols during the
consultancy.
 Equip CREMAs and Government Agencies with practical skills for continuous monitoring.
 Ensure transparent, reliable data collection and reporting consistent with Plan Vivo
requirements.
10.3 Consultant’s Role (8-month Contract Period)
Months 1-2: Baseline Establishment
 Conduct satellite and drone-based ecological surveys.
 Perform initial field assessments for carbon stocks and ecosystem health.
Months 3-4: Development of Monitoring Protocols
 Develop standardized, ecosystem-specific monitoring protocols for mangrove and tidal
salt marsh ecosystems.
 Provide Platform for Real-time monitoring of mapped ecosystems
 Provide practical training workshops for CREMA and government staff.
Months 5-6: Capacity-Building and Field Testing
 Conduct intensive hands-on training sessions for CREMAs and relevant agencies.
 Test and refine protocols through field exercises with local stakeholders.
Months 7-8: Finalization and Transition Planning
 Finalize the MRV protocols and produce clear, accessible documentation.
 Transition responsibilities to CREMAs and government agencies, ensuring readiness for
ongoing monitoring.
