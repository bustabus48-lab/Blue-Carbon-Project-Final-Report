A Carbon-Ready Baseline for Improved Cookstove Interventions in Ghana's Coastal Ramsar Sites

## 

## Executive Summary

This report establishes the comprehensive socio-economic baseline for an improved cookstove (ICS) intervention designed as a core component of the West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project (WACA ResIP 2) Blue Carbon initiative. The analysis covers 37 communities across Ghana's Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze Ramsar sites, providing the foundational data required for a high-integrity carbon finance project. The findings confirm that over 70% of households are dependent on mangrove-linked activities for their livelihoods, with a staggering 82% of coastal households relying on firewood as their primary cooking fuel. This creates a direct, quantifiable, and unsustainable link between daily cooking practices and the degradation of critical blue carbon ecosystems.1

The strategic rationale for the proposed ICS intervention is to decouple community well-being from ecosystem destruction. By significantly reducing the demand for fuelwood from both domestic cooking and the culturally and economically vital fish smoking industry, the project will achieve three synergistic objectives: directly alleviate anthropogenic pressure on mangrove forests, generate verifiable greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reductions, and deliver profound health and economic co-benefits to local communities, particularly women and children who bear the disproportionate burden of traditional cooking methods.1

To ensure financial sustainability and global recognition, the baseline data presented herein has been meticulously structured to meet the rigorous requirements of leading voluntary carbon market standards, specifically the Verra and Gold Standard programs. Key parameters essential for carbon accounting including baseline fuel consumption patterns, stove usage dynamics ("stacking"), the fraction of non-renewable biomass (fNRB), and community readiness for technology adoption have been documented to inform the development of a robust Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) plan.5 It is explicitly noted that the Plan Vivo standard, while a credible framework for land-use projects, is not applicable to energy-efficiency interventions such as cookstoves and is therefore not considered further in this analysis.7

Key findings from the baseline assessment are summarized as follows:

Pervasive Fuelwood Dependence: There is a deeply entrenched reliance on biomass, primarily mangrove wood and charcoal, for both domestic cooking and commercial-scale fish smoking. This dependency is the central driver of mangrove degradation across all three project sites.1

Intense Seasonal Demand Peaks: The demand for fuelwood, particularly for fish smoking, is not uniform throughout the year. It experiences a dramatic peak between November and February, a dynamic directly driven by the need to process the surge in fish landings that follows the government-mandated closed fishing season.1 This seasonal pressure point represents the period of most acute mangrove destruction.

Profound Gendered Burdens: Women and girls are overwhelmingly responsible for securing household energy. They bear the primary burden of fuelwood collection, spending an average of 2-4 hours daily on this task and traveling distances of 2-5 kilometers into increasingly degraded landscapes. This labor-intensive activity not only exposes them to significant health risks from smoke inhalation and physical strain but also represents a major opportunity cost, limiting their engagement in education and income-generating activities.1

High Community Readiness Tempered by Key Barriers: There is a high and clearly articulated willingness within the communities to adopt ICS. This interest is primarily driven by the tangible desires for fuel savings, reduced exposure to smoke, and greater cooking convenience. However, this readiness is constrained by significant and predictable barriers that have historically hindered the success of similar projects. These include the prohibitive upfront cost of stoves, a lack of awareness of the full spectrum of non-financial benefits (such as long-term health improvements), and the critical need for stove technologies to be compatible with traditional cooking practices, especially the preparation of staple foods.1

Based on this comprehensive baseline, this report puts forth a set of strategic recommendations. A phased rollout strategy is advised, prioritizing communities in high-degradation zones with strong social cohesion to build early momentum. The project must utilize innovative financing and distribution models to overcome affordability barriers. Crucially, the selection of stove technologies must be user-centric, ensuring they are robust, durable, and fully compatible with local cooking needs for both domestic meals and the commercial smoking of fish.1 By adhering to these evidence-based recommendations, the WACA Blue Carbon initiative can implement a cookstove project that achieves lasting environmental impact and transformative social change.

## The Nexus of Cooking, Livelihoods, and Mangrove Degradation in Coastal Ghana

### Introduction

To design an effective blue carbon intervention in coastal Ghana, it is imperative to first understand the profound socio-ecological context in which communities operate. In the wetland landscapes of the Songor, Keta Lagoon complex, and Muni-Pomadze Ramsar sites, the act of cooking is not a simple domestic chore; it is the central node connecting livelihoods, cultural identity, economic activity, and the health of the surrounding environment. The choice of cooking fuel is not merely a household decision but a reflection of a complex system of resource dependency that has sustained communities for generations, even as it now threatens the very ecosystems upon which they rely. This section establishes this intricate context, detailing the deep-seated dependence of local populations on mangrove ecosystems and illustrating how the cooking fire serves as a direct and powerful conduit for mangrove degradation.

### The Mangrove Ecosystem as a Livelihood Engine

The socio-economic baseline study validates that the relationship between communities and their coastal ecosystems is one of profound and multifaceted dependence. Across the three Ramsar sites, over 70% of households are directly engaged in mangrove-linked activities.1 These are not peripheral or supplemental income streams; they are the core pillars of the local economy. The primary activities include fish smoking, an industry almost entirely fueled by mangrove wood; salt production, which often involves clearing mangrove areas for evaporation pans; and the direct harvesting and sale of fuelwood and charcoal.1

This deep-rooted dependency creates a challenging paradox that lies at the heart of the blue carbon initiative. The very ecosystems that provide the foundation for community livelihoods are being progressively degraded by the extractive nature of these same activities. This establishes a dangerous feedback loop: as mangrove resources are depleted, the services they provide such as acting as nurseries for fish populations diminish. This leads to lower productivity in linked sectors like fisheries, which in turn increases economic pressure on households, potentially driving them to intensify their exploitation of the remaining mangrove resources to compensate for lost income. Breaking this cycle is the central challenge that the improved cookstove intervention is designed to address.1

### Fish Smoking: A Cultural and Economic Cornerstone

Within the array of mangrove-dependent livelihoods, fish smoking stands out for its cultural significance and economic scale. It is the most prevalent method of fish preservation in Ghana, with national estimates suggesting that 70-80% of the domestic marine and freshwater catch is consumed in smoked form.12 This practice is essential for food security. It transforms a highly perishable product into one with a shelf life of up to nine months, allowing fish caught during periods of abundance to be stored and consumed during the lean season.12 Beyond preservation, smoking is integral to the national cuisine, enhancing the flavor of fish and making it a staple ingredient in soups and sauces.12

Crucially, the fish smoking industry is a domain overwhelmingly dominated by women. From processing the raw fish to managing the smoking process and marketing the final product, women are the primary actors and beneficiaries. In many of the surveyed communities, women have organized into powerful local institutions, such as the Midakpenam Fish Processors Association, to coordinate their activities.1 The industry is therefore a critical source of female economic empowerment, providing women with an independent income and a significant role in the household and community economy. Any intervention in this sector must recognize and work within these established social and economic structures.

### The Direct Line from the Cooking Fire to Mangrove Loss

The connection between community energy needs and environmental degradation is direct and unambiguous. The baseline report confirms that the unsustainable harvesting of mangroves for fuelwood is a primary and relentless driver of ecosystem degradation in all three Ramsar sites.1 This demand is overwhelmingly driven by two sources: domestic cooking and, on a much larger scale, the commercial fish smoking industry.

The impact of this pressure is spatially evident. In the Keta Lagoon Complex, communities situated at the heart of the fish processing industry such as Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, and Salo exhibit some of the most severe and visible mangrove loss. Community members in areas like Galotse report the near-total disappearance of entire mangrove stands that existed within living memory.1 A similar pattern is observed in the Songor Ramsar site, where degradation is highest in and around the salt production zones of Elavanyo and Obane, where mangroves have been cleared for salt pans and harvested for fuel.1

This dynamic reveals an interlocking system of environmental and economic pressures. The well-documented decline in Ghana's marine fish stocks places immense economic strain on fishing households.1 This heightened economic vulnerability can make households more reliant on other accessible natural resources, such as mangrove wood, which can be harvested and sold as fuel or converted into charcoal for additional income. This creates a vicious cycle. The degradation of mangrove forests, which serve as critical nursery habitats for countless fish and crustacean species, further harms the health and productivity of the very fisheries that communities depend on.1 Consequently, pressure on the marine ecosystem amplifies pressure on the coastal forest ecosystem, and vice versa.

This understanding reframes the improved cookstove project. It is not merely an "energy" or "health" intervention; it is a critical intervention in the fisheries value chain and a powerful tool for ecosystem restoration. By drastically reducing the demand for mangrove wood, the project can help break the feedback loop of degradation. Its success will have positive ripple effects, potentially easing pressure on beleaguered fish stocks by allowing their vital nursery habitats to recover. This capacity to deliver multi-system benefits is a key element of the project's value proposition for both sustainable development financing and the voluntary carbon market.

## A Granular Baseline of Household and Commercial Energy Consumption

A robust, data-driven understanding of existing energy consumption is the technical bedrock upon which a credible carbon project is built. To meet the stringent accounting requirements of standards like Verra and Gold Standard, it is essential to move beyond general statements of dependency to a granular, quantified baseline of fuel use. This section presents the detailed findings on household and commercial energy consumption patterns across the project's geography, providing the specific data points needed to define the baseline scenario, calculate potential emissions reductions, and design a targeted and effective intervention.

### Fuel Mix and Domestic Consumption Patterns

The baseline study confirms a near-total reliance on solid biomass for domestic cooking, with clean alternatives like Liquefied Petroleum Gas (LPG) remaining largely inaccessible.

Dominance of Firewood: Across the lagoon and coastal communities, an overwhelming 82% of households use firewood as their primary cooking fuel.1 A significant and often preferred component of this firewood supply is mangrove wood, prized for its burning characteristics.

Role of Charcoal: In line with national trends, charcoal is the dominant fuel in more urbanized households and in upland communities further from the coast.15 The project area reflects this rural-urban energy divide, with charcoal being a key fuel in peri-urban centers like Sankor and Nsuekyir within the Muni-Pomadze site.1

Minimal LPG Penetration: Despite long-standing national policies aimed at promoting a transition to LPG, its adoption in the project areas is negligible. Only 13% of all respondents reported ever using LPG, and this use was almost exclusively confined to the most accessible peri-urban settings.1 This finding is critical for the carbon project's legitimacy, as it demonstrates "additionality” the principle that the emissions reductions achieved by the project would not have occurred in the absence of the intervention. A widespread, organic transition to cleaner cooking is demonstrably not underway.16

Consumption Volume Estimates: While the socio-economic report did not conduct quantitative fuel weighing studies, national-level data provides a reliable proxy for initial project modeling. A 2021 World Bank analysis estimates Ghana's total annual demand for cooking firewood at approximately 6.8 million tonnes, with an additional demand for charcoal equivalent to 5.6 million tonnes of wood.17 For the purposes of project design under carbon standards, methodologies often provide conservative default values. The Gold Standard's simplified methodology, for example, allows for the use of a default baseline biomass consumption figure of 0.5 tonnes per capita per year, which can be used for initial feasibility calculations before project-specific measurements are taken.6

### The Fuelwood Economy of the Fish Smoking Industry

The fish smoking industry represents a concentrated, commercial-scale demand for fuelwood that dwarfs domestic consumption on a per-unit basis and is a primary target for intervention.

Scale of Consumption: Fish smoking is an intensely energy-hungry process. The baseline study found that individual fish smoking businesses consume between 15 and 50 bundles of firewood each day during peak operations, with this fuel being sourced almost entirely from surrounding mangrove forests.1 At a national level, a World Bank study estimates the total annual woodfuel demand specifically for fish smoking in Ghana to be 225,000 tonnes.17

Seasonal Demand Dynamics: The pressure on mangrove ecosystems from this industry is not constant. While some level of harvesting occurs year-round, the study confirmed a distinct and dramatic peak in activity between November and February.1 This seasonality is not random; it is a direct consequence of national fisheries management policy. The Ghanaian government implements an annual "closed season" for marine fishing, typically spanning July and August, to protect fish stocks during their peak spawning period. This conservation measure halts fishing activities for artisanal and semi-industrial fleets, effectively creating a bottleneck in the supply of fresh fish.8

The result of this policy is a massive, synchronized surge in fish landings in the months immediately following the reopening of the season, from August to October. Faced with this glut of highly perishable catch, fish processors must work intensively to smoke and preserve the product for later sale. This creates the intense, concentrated demand for fuelwood that drives the peak mangrove harvesting observed from November through February. This reveals that a national fisheries conservation policy, while well-intentioned, has the unintended consequence of creating acute, seasonal deforestation pressure in coastal ecosystems.

This dynamic has profound implications for the cookstove project. The intervention must be designed to be robust enough to handle this intense seasonal peak in demand. It also presents a strategic opportunity: targeting the fish smoking industry with highly efficient ovens, such as the improved Chorkor smoker 12, and ensuring they are distributed and operational before the peak processing season begins in November, could yield the most significant and rapid reduction in mangrove degradation.

### The Human Cost of Traditional Fuel Collection

The reliance on traditional biomass fuels carries a heavy, often hidden, cost that is borne disproportionately by women and children. This human toll encompasses a significant time and labor burden, as well as severe and well-documented health impacts from exposure to household air pollution.

The Time and Labor Burden: The baseline report quantifies the immense time poverty imposed by fuelwood collection. In the project communities, women and girls spend an average of 2 to 4 hours every day on this task.1 This finding is consistent with broader research in regions like South Asia, where women can spend up to 20 hours per week securing fuel for their families.10 This time is a direct opportunity cost, preventing them from engaging in more productive income-generating activities, pursuing education, or providing childcare. It is a daily tax on their potential.1

The Physical Burden and Risk: As nearby forests become depleted, the journey to find fuel becomes longer and more arduous. The study found that women and girls now travel distances of 2 to 5 kilometers daily to gather firewood.1 This daily trek, often carrying heavy loads, can lead to chronic musculoskeletal injuries. Furthermore, traveling to remote and isolated areas can expose them to other risks, including violence.3

The Health Impacts of Indoor Air Pollution (IAP): The use of open fires and inefficient traditional stoves in poorly ventilated spaces results in dangerously high levels of indoor air pollution. Women in the project communities consistently reported suffering from respiratory ailments, including persistent coughing and eye irritation, which they directly attribute to smoke exposure.1 This local experience is reflective of a global health crisis. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that IAP from cooking with solid fuels is responsible for 3.2 million premature deaths annually, with women and young children being the most severely affected.3 The toxic cocktail of particulate matter and carbon monoxide emitted by these fires is a leading cause of pneumonia in children under five, and is strongly linked to stroke, ischemic heart disease, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and lung cancer in adults.2 In ecological zones like northern Ghana, studies have shown that exposure to cookstove smoke can increase the risk of contracting bacterial meningitis by a factor of nine.2 The daily act of preparing a meal becomes a significant threat to life and well-being.

## Community Readiness for a Clean Cooking Transition

The technical efficiency of an improved cookstove is meaningless if it is not adopted, used correctly, and sustained over time. The history of cookstove programs is littered with projects that failed because they overlooked the complex social, cultural, and economic factors that govern household behavior.23 Therefore, a critical component of this baseline is a thorough assessment of the social landscape: understanding community awareness of clean cooking options, their perceptions of new technologies, their willingness to change, and the critical barriers that must be overcome. This section analyzes the community readiness for a clean cooking transition, moving from what fuels people use to why they use them and whether they are truly prepared to embrace an alternative.

### Awareness, Perceptions, and Willingness to Adopt Improved Cookstoves (ICS)

The baseline study reveals a promising social foundation for an ICS intervention, characterized by high interest but constrained by practical and perceptual barriers.

High Willingness to Adopt: Across all three Ramsar sites, there is a strong and clearly articulated interest in adopting improved cookstoves. During focus group discussions, women in particular expressed a high level of enthusiasm for any technology that could save fuel, reduce their daily exposure to smoke, and improve the efficiency of their cooking and fish processing activities.1 This indicates that communities are not resistant to change but are actively seeking solutions to the problems they face with their current cooking methods.

Perceived Benefits as Primary Drivers: The motivation to adopt is rooted in tangible, immediate, and practical benefits. A World Bank behavioral study conducted in Ghana confirms that the most powerful driver for adoption is the prospect of reduced fuel consumption, which translates directly into monetary savings or reduced time spent on collection.11 Other highly valued benefits include faster cooking times, reduced hassle in starting and maintaining a fire, and, critically, improved safety for children by reducing the risk of burns from open flames.11

Low Salience of Long-Term Health Benefits: While the health risks of IAP are severe, the long-term health benefits of cleaner cooking are often not a primary driver for adoption among non-users.11 The connection between daily smoke exposure and the future onset of chronic diseases like COPD or lung cancer is less tangible and immediate than the daily reality of high fuel costs or the time spent gathering wood. Therefore, marketing and awareness campaigns that focus solely on abstract health warnings are likely to be less effective than those that emphasize immediate economic, convenience, and safety benefits.

The Power of Demonstration: The study found a high degree of openness to "try-before-you-buy" models.1 This suggests that community members are pragmatic and evidence-driven. They are more likely to be convinced by seeing a stove in action; witnessing its fuel efficiency, speed, and reduced smoke output firsthand than by abstract promises. Live demonstrations, peer testimonials, and short-term trial periods are therefore likely to be highly effective tools for driving adoption.

### Critical Barriers and Drivers for Adoption and Sustained Use

Despite the high level of interest, several critical barriers must be systematically addressed to ensure the project achieves high rates of adoption and, crucially, sustained use over the long term.

Economic Barrier: The Primacy of Affordability: The single most significant barrier cited in every community is the upfront cost of an improved stove.1 Even for locally produced, relatively low-cost models like the Gyapa stove, which retails for under $10, the initial cash outlay can be prohibitive for households with limited and irregular income.24 The baseline study found that even when flexible installment plans were offered for other stove models, the requirement for an initial deposit and the discipline of making consistent monthly repayments remained a major challenge for many families.11 This highlights that the core issue is not just the total price, but a lack of access to household liquidity and financing mechanisms that align with rural cash flows.

Behavioral Barrier: The Challenge of "Stove Stacking": A common phenomenon that severely undermines the environmental and health benefits of ICS projects is "stove stacking." This is where a household acquires a new, improved stove but continues to use their traditional stove alongside it, rather than replacing it entirely.11 This practice drastically reduces the actual fuel savings and emissions reductions achieved. The baseline study identified several key reasons for stove stacking in the Ghanaian context:

Incompatibility with Staple Foods: Traditional Ghanaian dishes like banku, fufu, and tuo zaafi require large pots and vigorous, forceful stirring. Many improved cookstoves are perceived as not being sturdy enough to handle this, leading users to revert to their three-stone fire for preparing these essential meals.11

Insufficient Cooking Volume: Households with large families often need to cook large quantities of food at once. The smaller capacity of many improved stoves makes the traditional, larger stove more practical for this purpose.11

Need for Multiple Burners: In households where time is a critical constraint, using multiple stoves simultaneously for example, a traditional stove for the main dish and an improved stove for a sauce is a common time-saving strategy.11

Technical and Logistical Barriers: Several other practical concerns influence long-term use:

Trust in Durability: Having experienced or heard stories of poorly made products in the past, community members are naturally skeptical about the durability of new technologies.1

Maintenance and Spare Parts: The long-term sustainability of the project depends on the local availability of spare parts and the knowledge to perform basic maintenance. If a stove breaks and cannot be easily repaired, users will quickly revert to their traditional methods.1

Lack of Awareness of Options: The study revealed that many households were unaware that improved stoves come in different sizes and models. A lack of information prevents them from selecting a stove that might be better suited to their specific family size and cooking needs.11

This analysis reveals a critical reality for the project design. The data presents a seeming contradiction: stoves like the Gyapa are often marketed as "affordable," yet cost consistently emerges as the primary barrier. The issue is that affordability is not determined by the final price tag alone, but by the alignment of payment structures with household cash flow. The true barrier is often a lack of liquidity and accessible micro-finance. Furthermore, the prevalence of stove stacking demonstrates that even a stove provided for free will not achieve its intended impact if it does not meet the user's fundamental cultural and practical cooking requirements. Technical efficiency is irrelevant if the stove cannot be used to cook the local staple food.

This leads to two clear imperatives for the project design. First, it must prioritize the development of innovative financing models that go beyond simple subsidies. This could include partnerships with local Village Savings and Loan Associations (VSLAs), the introduction of pay-as-you-go systems using mobile money or rent-to-own schemes. Second, it must be relentlessly user-centric in its technology selection. The chosen stoves, for both domestic use and fish smoking, must be rigorously tested in the local context and proven to be effective for local recipes and the required scales of cooking. The widespread and sustained success of the Chorkor smoker is a powerful testament to this principle of cultural and practical fit.12

## Aligning Project Design with Carbon Crediting Standards

To unlock the potential of carbon finance, a project must be designed from its inception to meet the exacting technical requirements of international carbon crediting standards. The socio-economic baseline is not merely a contextual study; it is the source of the essential data needed to build a credible and verifiable carbon project. This section translates the baseline findings into the specific language of carbon finance, demonstrating how the collected data will be used to develop an intervention that is compliant with the leading standards of Verra and Gold Standard, and how it establishes the foundation for quantifying valuable co-benefits.

### Establishing a Verifiable Baseline under Verra and Gold Standard Frameworks

The project's eligibility for carbon credits hinges on its ability to demonstrate that it causes real, measurable, and additional emissions reductions against a credible baseline.

Clarification on Inapplicability of Plan Vivo: A review of the Plan Vivo standard confirms that it exclusively certifies land-use, land-use change, and forestry (LULUCF) projects, such as afforestation and REDD+. The standard explicitly does not certify energy-efficiency or energy-production projects, which includes improved cookstoves.7 Therefore, this analysis focuses exclusively on the two leading and applicable standards for cookstove projects: Verra and Gold Standard.

Applicable Methodologies: The project will be designed in accordance with the most current and robust methodologies available. These are Verra's VCS Methodology VM0050, Energy Efficiency and Fuel-Switch Measures in Cookstoves, and Gold Standard's Simplified Methodology for Clean and Efficient Cookstoves.5 Both frameworks provide detailed procedures for quantifying emissions reductions from projects that replace traditional, inefficient cooking methods.

Defining the Baseline Scenario: Both standards require a clear and verifiable definition of the "business-as-usual" or baseline scenario. The data presented in Section 3 of this report provides this definition unequivocally. The baseline scenario is the continued use of traditional three-stone fires or inefficient, locally made metal stoves, fueled predominantly by firewood and charcoal derived from non-renewable biomass, particularly mangrove wood.1

Key Baseline Parameters for Carbon Accounting: The socio-economic data directly informs several critical parameters required by these methodologies:

Baseline Fuel Consumption: This is the quantity of fuel that would have been consumed in the absence of the project. The baseline report's data on the fuel mix (82% firewood in coastal areas) and qualitative descriptions of high consumption provide the initial justification.1 To meet the standards' requirements for quantification, this baseline must be precisely measured through a representative sample of households using a Kitchen Performance Test (KPT), which involves weighing fuel over several days. Alternatively, methodologies allow for the use of conservative, pre-approved default values (e.g., 0.5 tonnes of biomass per capita per year under the Gold Standard simplified methodology) for initial calculations.6

Fraction of Non-Renewable Biomass (fNRB): This is one of the most sensitive variables in cookstove carbon accounting. Credits can only be claimed for saving biomass that is "non-renewable," meaning it is harvested at a rate faster than it can regrow. The baseline report's detailed accounts of intense, unmanaged mangrove harvesting, leading to visible ecosystem degradation and fuelwood scarcity, provide strong qualitative evidence for a high fNRB in the project areas.1 To quantify this, the methodologies require the use of sophisticated, officially recognized tools, such as the UN's Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) Tool 30 or Tool 33, or advanced spatial models like the MoFuSS model, which analyze wood supply and demand dynamics in the project region.25

Stove Usage and Stacking: Carbon credits can only be generated when the improved stoves are actually being used. The qualitative findings on the prevalence and causes of stove stacking are therefore of paramount importance.11 Both Verra and Gold Standard methodologies require that projects implement a robust monitoring plan to track stove usage rates and the extent to which baseline stoves remain in use. This is typically accomplished through annual usage surveys administered to a random sample of project households.6 The baseline report's insights into why stacking occurs will allow the project to design more effective surveys and develop targeted strategies to encourage the complete replacement of old stoves.

### Framework for Quantifying Co-Benefits: Health, Gender, and Economic Impacts

In the modern voluntary carbon market, high-quality projects that can verifiably demonstrate strong, positive social and environmental co-benefits command premium prices. Buyers are increasingly seeking to invest in projects that not only reduce emissions but also contribute to the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Both Gold Standard, which was founded on the principle of integrating climate and development outcomes, and Verra, through its rigorous Climate, Community & Biodiversity (CCB) Standards, provide frameworks for certifying these additional impacts.29

The richness of the data collected in this socio-economic baseline goes far beyond the minimum required for a simple carbon calculation. It provides a robust, pre-packaged baseline for a wide array of valuable co-benefits, positioning this project to be marketed as a premium carbon-plus initiative. The MRV system should be designed from the outset to track both carbon metrics and these critical social indicators.

Establishing the Co-benefit Baseline: This report provides the necessary starting point for measuring progress against key SDGs:

SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-being): The baseline documents widespread self-reported respiratory and eye ailments due to IAP and highlights the high risk of burns to children from open fires.1 This allows for future monitoring of health improvements through tracking reductions in self-reported symptoms, changes in clinic visitation rates for respiratory infections, or reductions in reported burn incidents.

SDG 5 (Gender Equality): The baseline precisely quantifies the time and distance burden on women and girls for fuel collection (2-4 hours and 2-5 km per day).1 This creates a clear and powerful metric, hours saved per household per day to track female empowerment and the re-allocation of their time to other priorities.

SDG 1 (No Poverty) & SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth): The baseline establishes household reliance on purchasing fuel and quantifies the immense opportunity cost of time spent on fuel collection.30 This allows the project to monitor direct household savings from reduced fuel expenditure and to track how saved time is re-allocated to income-generating activities, such as farming, trading, or small business development.4

SDG 15 (Life on Land): The project's core environmental benefit is the reduction of pressure on terrestrial ecosystems, specifically mangrove forests. The baseline has identified high-degradation zones and documented the primary driver of that degradation.1 This allows for the establishment of ecological monitoring plots within these zones to track indicators of forest regeneration over the project's lifetime.

## Strategic Recommendations for a High-Impact, Carbon-Ready Cookstove Intervention

The comprehensive baseline analysis presented in this report provides a clear and evidence-based foundation for action. To translate these findings into a successful, scalable, and financially sustainable intervention, the following strategic recommendations are proposed. They are designed to create a high-impact project that not only meets the technical rigors of carbon crediting standards but also delivers transformative and lasting benefits to the communities and ecosystems of coastal Ghana.

### Technology Selection and Distribution Models

The success of the project hinges on providing the right technology through the right channels. A one-size-fits-all approach is destined to fail.

Fit-for-Purpose Technology Selection:

Domestic Cookstoves: The primary criterion for selection must be proven compatibility with local cooking practices, particularly the preparation of staple foods that require large pots and vigorous stirring.11 The project should offer a curated range of stove sizes to accommodate different family needs. Priority should be given to durable, locally produced models with an established track record, such as the Gyapa charcoal stove, which has demonstrated success in the Ghanaian market.4 All selected models must undergo rigorous field testing within the project communities before any large-scale rollout.

Fish Smoking Ovens: For the commercial fish smoking industry, the ideal technology is the FAO-developed Chorkor smoker. Its advantages are well-documented: it is significantly more fuel-efficient than traditional ovens, it produces a higher-quality and more uniformly smoked product, it dramatically increases the volume of fish that can be processed at one time, and it can be constructed using locally available materials.12 The project's strategy should focus on training and certifying local artisans in the construction and maintenance of Chorkor ovens, creating a sustainable local industry to support their dissemination.

Innovative and Sustainable Business Models:

The project must move beyond a reliance on free handouts or simple subsidies, which can undermine local markets and long-term ownership. Instead, it should foster sustainable business models that make stoves affordable.

Micro-finance Partnerships: The project should actively partner with the numerous Village Savings and Loan Associations (VSLAs) and women's groups identified in the baseline study. These groups can serve as trusted local channels for providing micro-loans to their members for stove purchases, with repayment schedules tailored to local cash flow patterns.1

Pay-As-You-Go (PAYG) Models: For higher-cost stoves, the project should explore implementing PAYG systems, which have been highly successful for solar home systems and other energy products across Africa. These models, often leveraging mobile money platforms, allow users to make small, regular payments to unlock the stove for use, eventually owning it outright.

Leveraging Social Networks for Distribution: The project should empower existing social networks such as CREMAs, women's groups, church congregations, and the established network of Community Change Agents (CCAs) to act as sales and distribution agents. This approach not only leverages trusted community relationships but also creates local employment and entrepreneurial opportunities.1

### A Phased Rollout Strategy for Priority Communities

A strategic, phased rollout will allow the project to build momentum, learn from experience, and maximize its impact over time.

Phase 1: Establish Early Action Hubs: The intervention should begin in a select number of communities identified as having a high degree of "readiness." These are communities that exhibit strong social cohesion, have active and organized local institutions (such as engaged CCAs or women's groups), and are located in areas of significant mangrove degradation. Based on the baseline assessment, the communities of Sankor (Muni-Pomadze), Pute (Songor), and Dzita (Keta Lagoon) are strong candidates for these initial hubs.1 A successful pilot in these communities will generate valuable lessons and create powerful success stories that can be used to drive adoption in subsequent phases.

Phase 2: Scale to High-Impact Zones: Following the pilot phase, the project should expand its focus to the communities where the intervention can have the greatest and most immediate environmental impact. This means targeting the epicenters of the fish smoking industry, such as Agbatsivi, Galotse, and Bomigo in the Keta Lagoon Complex, where fuelwood consumption is most intense and mangrove degradation is most severe.1 Intervening in these high-pressure zones will yield the largest reductions in mangrove harvesting.

Phase 3: Broader Dissemination and Saturation: In the final phase, the project will roll out to the remaining communities. The distribution, financing, and marketing strategies for this phase will be refined and optimized based on the data and lessons learned from the first two phases, ensuring a more efficient and effective large-scale deployment.

### A Robust Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) Framework

A credible MRV system is the non-negotiable backbone of any carbon finance project. It is the mechanism through which performance is measured, and it provides the data that auditors will use to verify the project's claims and approve the issuance of carbon credits.

Design for an Integrated MRV System: The project's MRV system must be designed from the outset to be fully compliant with the chosen carbon standard (Verra VM0050 or the relevant Gold Standard methodology). This system must be capable of tracking both the core carbon metrics (e.g., number of stoves distributed, usage rates, fuel savings) and the key co-benefit indicators (e.g., time saved in fuel collection, household expenditure on fuel, self-reported health improvements).

Champion Community-Led Monitoring: To enhance accuracy, reduce costs, and build local ownership, the project must empower and equip the network of Community Change Agents (CCAs) to play a central role in local data collection.1 With appropriate training and simple tools, CCAs can be responsible for conducting annual usage surveys, tracking stove functionality, documenting any grievances, and collecting testimonials and stories related to the project's co-benefits. This participatory approach transforms monitoring from an extractive exercise into a tool for community engagement and adaptive management.

Incorporate Appropriate Technology: The MRV system should leverage technology to improve efficiency and data quality. This can include using digital survey tools on mobile phones or tablets (e.g., KoboToolbox or ODK) for streamlined data collection and real-time dashboarding. The project should also consider piloting the use of Stove Use Monitors (SUMs) in a small, representative sample of households. These small, heat-sensing devices provide precise, objective data on when and for how long stoves are used, which can significantly strengthen the credibility of the project's carbon claims and potentially reduce reliance on more subjective survey data.5

Plan for Regular Third-Party Verification: The project plan and budget must include provisions for regular verification audits by an accredited, independent Validation and Verification Body (VVB). The comprehensive baseline data presented in this report will serve as the essential foundation for the first of these critical audits, setting the stage for the successful issuance of carbon credits and ensuring the long-term financial viability of the intervention.
