# 1.0 Executive Summary

## 1.1 Context and Purpose

Ghana’s WACA (West Africa Coastal Areas) Blue Carbon initiative seeks to restore mangrove ecosystems in the Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze Ramsar sites while empowering local communities through carbon finance, improved governance, and climate-resilient livelihoods. As part of this effort, the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR), in collaboration with the Ministry of Environment, Science and Technology (MEST), commissioned this socio-economic baseline study as part of the larger Blue Carbon Assessment potential of Ghana to provide a comprehensive understanding of local contexts, inform restoration planning, and establish readiness for future carbon market participation using the WACA project areas as pilot.

## 1.2 Methodology Overview

The baseline was conducted between June and August 2025 across 37 communities spanning three Ramsar zones. It employed a mix of qualitative and participatory tools, including:

Focus Group Discussions (FGDs)

Key Informant Interviews (KIIs)

Community Profiling by MLNR-WACA Site Specific Ramsar Working Groups

Community mapping and transect walks

Institutional profiling and policy analysis

Desk Review of Secondary Data 

The study employed safeguards-compliant processes aligned with FPIC (Free, Prior, and Informed Consent), the Project Environmental and Social Safeguards Framework, while ensuring gender and social inclusion.

## 1.3 Key Findings

### 1. Livelihood Systems and Ecosystem Pressure

Over 70% of households depend on mangrove-linked activities: fish smoking, salt production, and fuelwood harvesting.

Unsustainable harvesting practices and lack of alternatives continue to degrade mangrove patches in all three Ramsar sites.

Women bear disproportionate labor burdens but remain underrepresented in governance structures.

### 2. CREMA Readiness and Governance

CREMA (Community Resource Management Area) processes have been initiated but are at varying stages:
• High Interest and Energy in Muni-Pomadze
• Emerging in Songor
• Fragmented in Keta Lagoon

Community Change Agents are present in all the profiled communities, and Community Resource Management Committees (CRMCs) exist in some of the areas but require training, legal finalization, and operational support.

### 3. Safeguards and Inclusion

FPIC was adequately achieved during the execution of the assignment but needs renewal, especially in Keta and Songor.

Persons with disabilities (PWDs) and migrants remain largely excluded from planning processes, though efforts are being rigorously pursued under the larger WACA project to address this gap.

Grievance systems are informal in over 80% of communities that were profiled.

### 4. Environmental Conditions

Mangrove degradation is highest near salt production zones (e.g., Elavanyo, Obane).

Solid and plastic waste is a major challenge in all three Ramsar Sites.

Communities observe coastal erosion, declining fisheries, and seasonal flooding.

Restoration success has been observed in pilot communities (e.g., Sankor, Pute).

### 5. Opportunities and High-Potential Areas

Sankor, Pute, and Dzita were identified as early action hubs for blue carbon piloting based on ecological condition, community cohesion, and CREMA momentum, but the other communities within the cluster must be brought up to speed to be able to make the required impact in the landscape.

Youth and school-led restoration and MRV (monitoring, reporting, verification) models are gaining traction and need to be explored as an opportunity to sustain any intervention beyond the project life span.

Women’s groups are prepared to lead value-added enterprises (e.g., salt packaging, beekeeping) with initial support.

## Risks Identified

Inadequate coordination between the Wildlife Division in the project areas, the Assemblies, and NGOs, yet the bottom-up approach of the WACA Blue Carbon Initiative is providing an opportunity worth exploring to improve the level of coordination and integration of efforts

Land tenure issues are observed, and elite capture of CREMA structures in select zones is visible within parts of the project areas.

Gaps in legal clarity regarding carbon rights and benefit-sharing

Low awareness and misconceptions around carbon finance

## 1.4 Recommendations (Summarized)

### Community-Level

Finalize CREMA formation and ensure inclusive leadership

Launch grievance systems and FPIC renewal processes into the CREMAs to be operationalized under the Initiative

Prioritize youth- and women-led livelihood pilots and participatory restoration models

### District Assemblies

Integrate blue carbon into MTDPs and Assembly budgets

Co-facilitate CREMA training, FPIC, and MRV activities

Support small infrastructure (e.g., nursery sheds, sanitation, meeting spaces)

### Wildlife Division and Forestry Commission

Assign CREMA focal officers with logistics support in the project areas

Harmonize restoration and MRV standards with carbon market frameworks

Oversee safeguards, co-benefit tracking, and restoration quality assurance

### MEST and MLNR

Finalize the CREMA Legislative Instrument (LI)

Develop national carbon rights and co-benefit guidance

Coordinate donor alignment and global registry interface

### Development Partners

Fund co-benefit pilots, MRV systems, and women/youth enterprises

Align with Ghana’s CREMA framework and safeguard standards

Pool resources into coordinated blue carbon investment platforms



## 1.5 Next Steps

Stakeholder Validation — Consensus has been built with the communities and the MLNR Ramsar Working Groups in all three sites on the findings of this assignment. It is expected that a validation session will be held with MEST/MLNR to confirm findings and finalize CREMA entry points with the pilot project areas.

Integration with Carbon Baseline — Align socio-economic zones with carbon sampling plots and MRV systems.

Report Finalization and Launch — Translate findings into full report, policy briefs, and summary tools.

Implementation and Monitoring Rollout — Begin restoration, safeguards, enterprise, and carbon readiness programs under a harmonized workplan within the larger MLNR-WACA interventions.

## Conclusion

This socio-economic baseline provides the foundation for an equitable, inclusive, and locally led implementation of blue carbon. With early investment in safeguards, restoration, and community systems, Ghana is well-positioned to deliver a blue carbon model that protects ecosystems, empowers people, and unlocks long-term climate finance.



# 2.0 Introduction

## 2.1 Context: WACA ResIP 2 and the Blue Carbon Initiative

### Introduction

Ghana’s coastal regions are dynamic socio-ecological landscapes that sustain millions of livelihoods and harbor some of the country’s most fragile ecosystems. With over 550 kilometers of coastline, Ghana faces increasing threats from climate change, coastal erosion, flooding, saline intrusion, and ecosystem degradation. Recognizing the urgency to address these vulnerabilities, Ghana became a key participant in the West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project (WACA ResIP), a regional initiative facilitated by the World Bank.

The WACA ResIP seeks to improve the resilience of coastal communities and ecosystems in West Africa by supporting participating countries in reducing coastal risks and strengthening integrated coastal management. WACA ResIP Phase 2 (WACA ResIP 2), which Ghana joined in 2021, builds on the progress of the first phase by scaling up nature-based and infrastructure-based solutions to coastal resilience. The Blue Carbon Initiative, introduced under WACA ResIP 2 in Ghana, represents a bold expansion into climate mitigation and carbon finance pathways, with community-driven mangrove restoration and wetland conservation at its heart.

This section provides an in-depth context of the WACA ResIP 2 framework and the embedded Blue Carbon initiative in Ghana. It traces the rationale, components, regional scope, and Ghana’s strategic participation, thereby grounding the socio-economic baseline study within the broader multi-country resilience architecture.

### Regional Framework: WACA ResIP

The WACA ResIP was launched in 2018 by the World Bank in collaboration with the West African Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU), the Abidjan Convention, and other regional partners. Its overarching goal is to support West African countries in sustainably managing and protecting their coastal assets, which are critical to economic development and ecological stability.

Phase 1 included Benin, Côte d’Ivoire, Mauritania, São Tomé and Príncipe, Senegal, and Togo. It focused on interventions such as sea defense construction, early warning systems, policy reforms, and the establishment of regional coordination bodies. Phase 2, approved in 2021, expanded the program to include Ghana, The Gambia, and Guinea-Bissau, with continued support to Togo and Senegal.

#### Key pillars of the WACA ResIP framework include:

Strengthening regional coordination and knowledge sharing

Enhancing resilience through nature-based solutions and hybrid (grey-green) infrastructure

Institutional capacity building and policy integration

Supporting livelihood diversification and gender-responsive approaches

Advancing transboundary ecosystem management and regional monitoring

In this broader context, Ghana’s entry into WACA ResIP 2 offers a critical opportunity to address long-standing environmental risks while unlocking new pathways for sustainable coastal development.

### Ghana’s Participation in WACA ResIP 2

Ghana’s coastline spans four administrative regions: Volta, Greater Accra, Central, and Western. These regions are home to globally significant Ramsar sites and estuarine systems such as the Keta Lagoon Complex, Songor Lagoon, and Muni-Pomadze Wetlands. These ecosystems support vital fisheries, mangrove forests, coastal agriculture, and salt production, while also acting as protective buffers against storm surges and sea-level rise.

Through WACA ResIP 2, Ghana is implementing an integrated coastal resilience program that combines grey infrastructure (e.g., sea defense structures) with green infrastructure (e.g., mangrove and wetland restoration). The project is jointly led by the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) and the Ministry of Environment, Science and Technology (MEST), in collaboration with the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and the Forestry Commission/Wildlife Division, among other sectoral and local partners.

Ghana’s WACA interventions include:

Restoration of degraded mangrove ecosystems in priority Ramsar sites

Establishment of Community Resource Management Areas (CREMAs) to empower local governance over natural resources

Improved waste management, especially plastic pollution control

Livelihood diversification programs for women, youth, and vulnerable groups

Institutional coordination platforms across national and local stakeholders

Carbon readiness activities, including baseline assessments for blue carbon potential

### The Emergence of the Blue Carbon Initiative

Under WACA ResIP 2, Ghana has taken steps to develop a dedicated Blue Carbon Initiative aimed at exploring the carbon sequestration potential of coastal wetlands, especially mangroves. Blue carbon refers to the carbon stored in coastal and marine ecosystems, particularly mangroves, tidal salt marshes, and seagrass meadows.

Ghana’s blue carbon strategy aligns with its commitment under the Paris Agreement to reduce emissions through nature-based solutions. By protecting and restoring mangrove forests, Ghana aims to enhance carbon sinks, improve biodiversity, and offer new livelihood opportunities through access to carbon finance.

Three key Ramsar sites serve as pilot landscapes for blue carbon assessments:

Songor Ramsar Site (Greater Accra Region)

Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site (Volta Region)

Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site (Central Region)

These sites were selected based on criteria including extent of mangrove, drivers of degradation, restoration potential, community dependence on ecosystem services, and readiness for CREMA formation. The Blue Carbon work stream in Ghana includes:

Geospatial and ecological baseline mapping of mangrove extent and condition

Socio-economic profiling of wetland-dependent communities ( Songor, Muni, Keta)

Carbon stock estimation and emission factor development

Community consultations for Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC)

Exploration of voluntary carbon market pathways

By linking restoration with carbon finance, the initiative seeks to create incentivized conservation mechanisms rooted in community benefit-sharing and long-term ecosystem stewardship.

### Relevance to the Baseline Study

This socio-economic baseline study is a foundational component of Ghana’s Blue Carbon readiness. It provides:

A granular understanding of livelihoods, governance, and ecosystem dependency

The social landscape necessary for CREMA development and FPIC compliance

Inputs into carbon co-benefits (e.g., emission reductions from improved cookstoves)

A platform for equitable design of benefit-sharing schemes in future carbon transactions

The study is therefore not an isolated socio-economic exercise but a pivotal piece of the Blue Carbon architecture under WACA. It anchors carbon science in social realities, ensuring that mitigation outcomes are socially inclusive, locally led, and resilient over time.

### Conclusion

Ghana’s participation in WACA ResIP 2 and its pioneering Blue Carbon Initiative positions the country at the intersection of climate adaptation, mitigation, and inclusive green development. The success of these efforts depends not only on ecological metrics but on how well community institutions, resource users, and governance systems are understood, engaged, and empowered.

This introduction provides the contextual backdrop for the remaining chapters, which delve into the socio-economic conditions, governance dynamics, environmental pressures, and opportunities across the three focal Ramsar landscapes.

## 2.2 Importance of Socio-Economic Baseline Studies for Blue Carbon

### Introduction

Socio-economic baseline studies are an essential foundation for any environmental intervention that seeks to integrate ecological restoration with community well-being and sustainability. In the context of blue carbon initiatives, particularly those situated within complex socio-ecological landscapes like Ghana’s Ramsar sites, socio-economic baselines serve as critical diagnostic tools. They provide an evidence-based understanding of how communities interact with their environment, derive their livelihoods, and respond to ecological changes and management interventions.

under this Blue Carbon Assessment of the WACA ResIP 2, the socio-economic baseline is more than a background study; it is the social pillar of a comprehensive ecosystem-based management strategy. It enables equitable planning, safeguards implementation, benefit-sharing, and the measurement of social co-benefits from climate mitigation activities. This section explores why socio-economic baselines are indispensable to blue carbon initiatives, drawing from best practices, Ghana’s national context, and lessons learned from earlier environmental projects.

#### 1. Mapping Human-Ecosystem Interdependence

Coastal ecosystems such as mangroves and wetlands are integral to local livelihoods in Ghana’s coastal zones. They provide fish, salt, medicinal plants, fuelwood, and protection against storms. A socio-economic baseline documents the specific ways in which households and communities depend on these resources, and how this dependency varies by gender, age, occupation, and vulnerability status.

This understanding is crucial to avoid negative trade-offs. For example, a blanket ban on mangrove harvesting without viable alternatives can worsen poverty or trigger non-compliance. The baseline ensures that restoration efforts consider livelihood substitution, seasonal vulnerabilities, and hidden dependencies that might not be visible through ecological assessments alone.

#### 2. Foundation for CREMA Development and Local Governance

Ghana’s Blue Carbon Initiative is rooted in community participation through CREMAs (Community Resource Management Areas). A robust socio-economic baseline reveals the readiness of communities for CREMA establishment. It assesses existing governance systems, conflict resolution mechanisms, power dynamics, and representation of marginalized groups (e.g., women, youth, people with disabilities).

It also helps identify local institutions, social networks, and leaders who can be trained as CREMA executives or change agents. Without this baseline, CREMAs risk being non-functional or exclusive, weakening their legitimacy and long-term sustainability.

#### 3. Safeguards and FPIC Compliance

International and national safeguards frameworks (e.g., World Bank ESF, Ghana’s EPA guidelines) require that environmental interventions obtain Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) from affected communities. Socio-economic baseline studies are often the first step in operationalizing FPIC, especially in culturally diverse and low-literacy environments.

They provide the basis for identifying affected parties, understanding power asymmetries, and tailoring communication strategies. They also uncover traditional land tenure systems, sacred areas, taboos, and customary laws that must be respected during restoration or carbon project implementation.

#### 4. Designing Equitable Benefit-Sharing Mechanisms

One of the promises of blue carbon projects is that they can generate carbon finance through avoided deforestation and restoration. However, the benefits must be shared fairly among stakeholders. Socio-economic baselines provide the necessary input to design benefit-sharing plans that are context-specific.

These include:

Assessing who bears the opportunity cost of conservation

Understanding household income levels and inequality

Identifying financial literacy gaps or group-based saving systems (e.g., VSLAs)

Mapping gender roles in value chains (e.g., women fish processors)

This enables developers to avoid elite capture and ensure that the most vulnerable benefit from carbon revenue, stove distribution, tourism proceeds, or employment.

#### 5. Monitoring Social Co-Benefits and Risks

Most blue carbon projects are expected to demonstrate not only ecological but also social co-benefits. These include poverty reduction, gender empowerment, food security, and institutional strengthening. The baseline provides the starting point for monitoring change over time.

In Ghana, the WACA project also aims to reduce pressure on mangroves by promoting improved cookstoves and plastic waste management. Without a socio-economic baseline, it would be impossible to track how many households shifted fuel use or how women’s health improved due to cleaner kitchens.

Baselines may also identify potential social risks such as displacement, exclusion, or intra-community tensions that may emerge when access to resources is restricted. This allows for mitigation planning and grievance redress mechanisms to be built into project design.

#### 6. Enhancing Local Ownership and Institutional Trust

When communities participate in socio-economic baseline studies, they gain a clearer understanding of the project, its goals, and potential benefits. Participatory data collection processes like FGDs, community mapping, and transect walks empower residents and build transparency.

This fosters local ownership of restoration efforts and strengthens trust between communities, government agencies, and implementing partners. In many successful conservation projects across Africa, the early and genuine involvement of communities in baseline studies has proven to be a predictor of long-term success.

### Conclusion

A socio-economic baseline is not a bureaucratic checkbox; it is the social architecture on which blue carbon projects stand. In Ghana’s WACA context, it is the bridge between satellite maps and shoreline dwellers, between international finance and fisherfolk, between carbon sequestration and social equity.

Its importance lies not just in data, but in its ability to shape inclusive, adaptive, and resilient climate solutions that work for nature and people alike.

## 2.3 Study Rationale and Alignment with National Strategies (NDCs, Ramsar, SDGs, etc.)

### Introduction

Ghana’s coastal ecosystems are vital not only for local livelihoods and biodiversity but also for achieving national and global climate and development goals. The socio-economic baseline study for the WACA Blue Carbon Assessment is driven by the need to align field-level interventions with these broader policy frameworks. This section outlines the rationale for the study through the lens of national strategies such as Ghana’s Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs), the National Climate Change Policy (NCCP), and Ghana’s SDG priorities, as well as international obligations, including the Ramsar Convention and emerging blue carbon financing mechanisms.

#### 1. Rationale for the Study

The socio-economic baseline is an operational tool that ensures that blue carbon initiatives are not developed in a policy vacuum. It allows the WACA Blue Carbon initiative to:

Ground ecological and carbon stock data in community-level realities

Identify pathways for integration with government climate and biodiversity goals

Promote institutional coherence across ministries and agencies

Capture the social, economic, and cultural dynamics that affect blue carbon success

Ghana is increasingly leveraging nature-based solutions (NbS) to address climate and biodiversity loss. However, the success of such solutions hinges on accurate and disaggregated data on who uses, governs, and depends on the resources targeted for restoration and protection. This study responds to that need.

#### 2. Alignment with Ghana’s Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)

Ghana’s updated NDCs (2021–2030) reflect a commitment to reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 64 MtCO2e by 2030 through mitigation and adaptation measures. Among the prioritized interventions are:

Sustainable forest management and afforestation

Wetland and mangrove conservation and restoration

Community-based natural resource governance

Use of improved biomass cookstoves to reduce pressure on forests

The WACA Blue Carbon Initiative directly contributes to these priorities. By generating disaggregated baseline data on mangrove use, cookstove access, and household-level vulnerability, the socio-economic study provides the foundation for tracking NDC-linked outcomes. It also supports future MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, Verification) efforts by building a participatory, community-driven data system that complements remote sensing.

#### 3. Integration with the National Climate Change Policy (NCCP)

Ghana’s NCCP (2013) outlines three cross-cutting pillars: (i) Effective adaptation, (ii) Social development, and (iii) Low-carbon growth. All are relevant to the WACA Blue Carbon project.

The socio-economic study contributes to NCCP implementation by:

Identifying communities most vulnerable to coastal and climate stressors

Profiling energy use and identifying alternatives that support low-carbon development

Promoting inclusion of gender and marginalized groups in resource governance

This helps integrate climate and social planning and supports MEST’s mandate to mainstream climate action into district-level development.

#### 4. Connection to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

The socio-economic dimensions of blue carbon interventions have strong co-benefits across multiple SDGs:

SDG 1 – No Poverty: Livelihood alternatives linked to restoration (e.g., eco-tourism, aquaculture)

SDG 5 – Gender Equality: Women’s roles in mangrove use, processing, and restoration

SDG 13 – Climate Action: Sequestration data and adaptation planning

SDG 14 – Life Below Water: Protection of mangrove nurseries for aquatic species

SDG 15 – Life on Land: Forest restoration and biodiversity conservation

This baseline study disaggregates community needs and practices by gender, occupation, and vulnerability group, enabling SDG-aligned planning.

#### 5. Ramsar Convention Commitments

Ghana is a signatory to the Ramsar Convention on Wetlands of International Importance and has designated multiple Ramsar sites, including all three targeted in this study. The Ramsar principles of “wise use” and participatory management are embodied in the WACA Blue Carbon design.

The socio-economic study ensures compliance with the Convention’s requirement for inclusive, sustainable management of Ramsar-listed wetlands. It builds the foundation for CREMAs to function as decentralized governance bodies for Ramsar stewardship.

#### 6. Synergies with Other National Programs

This study also supports ongoing initiatives such as:

The National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP)

The Forest Reference Level and REDD+ strategy

The National Plastics Management Policy (linked to coastal pollution control)

The National Land Policy (as it relates to customary resource tenure)

By generating a unified socio-economic landscape of the three sites, the study creates an integration platform for cross-sectoral programs.

### Conclusion

The socio-economic baseline study is both a technical and a strategic undertaking. It builds the social and institutional bridge between local community realities and Ghana’s national and international policy commitments. In doing so, it increases the coherence, legitimacy, and effectiveness of blue carbon interventions.

## 2.4 Linkages with Carbon Trading, Improved Cookstoves, and Plastic Management

### Introduction

As Ghana’s WACA Blue Carbon Initiative evolves from concept to implementation, it seeks to unlock innovative mechanisms for climate mitigation, community empowerment, and environmental co-benefits. A major frontier in this transformation is the integration of blue carbon restoration with emerging carbon market pathways, clean energy technologies, and circular economy approaches to address plastic pollution.

This section explores how the socio-economic baseline study intersects with three promising entry points for climate finance and impact: (1) carbon trading especially voluntary blue carbon credits; (2) the adoption of improved cookstoves as a pathway to emission reduction and reduced pressure on mangroves; and (3) community-based plastic management as a strategy to mitigate pollution, create jobs, and potentially enter the evolving plastic credit market.

Each of these mechanisms, while distinct, converge on the shared goal of enabling nature-based solutions to generate measurable climate and development benefits for Ghana’s coastal communities.

### 1. Carbon Trading and Blue Carbon Markets

#### 1.1 Blue Carbon as a Credit-Generating Asset

Mangrove ecosystems sequester carbon at rates up to ten times higher than terrestrial forests, storing large volumes in their biomass and soils. Protecting and restoring mangroves can generate carbon credits that can be sold in voluntary carbon markets (VCMs) under standards such as Verra’s VM0033 or Plan Vivo’s Mangrove Methodology. These credits are increasingly sought after by companies and governments seeking to offset emissions through nature-based solutions.

Under WACA, Ghana’s blue carbon activities (e.g., replanting, reduced degradation, and community conservation agreements) may qualify for such credits. The socio-economic baseline study is vital to:

Establish community land/resource tenure systems (required for credit ownership)

Document opportunity costs and co-benefits for benefit-sharing design

Identify eligible activities (e.g., avoided deforestation, enhanced sequestration)

Generate baseline scenarios and leakage data per carbon methodologies

#### 1.2 Readiness and Institutional Linkages

The EPA has developed a national carbon market registry and MRV protocols to operationalize Article 6 of the Paris Agreement. The Forestry Commission (under REDD+) and MEST are also building institutional capacity for market access into the blue carbon space through the PROBLUE component of the WACA.

The socio-economic study provides baseline data on:

Mangrove use patterns and degradation drivers

Willingness to participate in restoration for carbon

Gender-disaggregated roles in land use and fuelwood chains

This data supports Ghana’s preparation for both bilateral market mechanisms (e.g., ITMOs) and voluntary credit sales that require strong FPIC and benefit-sharing documentation.

#### 1.3 Community-Based Carbon Projects

Carbon trading initiatives involving communities require credible socio-economic data. Projects must demonstrate that:

Communities are not displaced or negatively impacted

Traditional livelihoods are respected or enhanced

Revenue is shared equitably based on effort, risk, and opportunity cost

The baseline thus enables the design of socially responsible carbon interventions. For example, it informs whether community associations (e.g., CREMAs or VSLAs) can serve as revenue distribution platforms, and whether change agents can be trained as local monitors.

### 2. Improved Cookstoves and Mangrove Pressure Reduction

#### 2.1 The Energy-Livelihood-Nature Nexus

Across the coastal sites profiled, communities rely heavily on mangrove wood for cooking and fish smoking. This unsustainable harvest is a major driver of mangrove degradation. Introducing improved biomass cookstoves (e.g., rocket stoves, TLUDs) offers dual benefits:

Reducing the volume of fuelwood required per meal or per fish batch

Reducing household indoor air pollution, with health benefits for women and children

From a carbon accounting standpoint, cookstove adoption can yield measurable emission reductions, qualifying for carbon credits under clean cookstove methodologies (e.g., Gold Standard, Verra).

#### 2.2 Role of the Baseline Study

The socio-economic baseline documents:

Household fuel use patterns (e.g., mangrove vs. alternative fuels)

Seasonal demands for fish smoking (linked to harvesting cycles)

Time spent by women and youth in fuelwood collection

Existing awareness of, or exposure to, clean cooking options

This data supports:

Targeting stove dissemination to high-use zones

Estimating emission reductions and crediting potential

Designing gender-sensitive cookstove interventions

#### 2.3 Integrating Cookstove Finance into Blue Carbon

Cookstoves and blue carbon restoration can be bundled in a community carbon portfolio. This would enable:

Aggregated verification and reporting

Combined revenue streams for mangrove and energy co-benefits

Holistic livelihood and conservation gains in targeted communities

Pilot programs (e.g., in Kenya, Senegal, Madagascar) have successfully integrated these elements. Ghana’s WACA sites are ideal test beds.

### 3. Plastic Waste Management and Circular Economy Co-Benefits

#### 3.1 Coastal Plastics Crisis

Plastic pollution, especially along the Keta Lagoon and Songor coasts, was cited in all stakeholder profiling reports. It clogs creeks, kills aquatic life, reduces fishery yields, and undermines ecotourism potential.

The WACA project includes components on community-based plastic management, such as:

Beach clean-up cooperatives

Youth-led plastic recovery and reuse enterprises

District-level waste segregation and transport initiatives

#### 3.2 Plastic Credits and Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)

New global frameworks now enable entities to generate plastic credits by collecting, recycling, or co-processing plastic waste. Verified under protocols like Verra’s Plastic Waste Reduction Standard, these credits are sold to companies with EPR obligations.

Ghana is piloting an EPR framework under its National Plastics Management Policy. WACA’s Blue Carbon program can link to this by:

Supporting plastic recovery groups in Ramsar-adjacent communities

Documenting plastic leakage sources through the baseline study

Training change agents on circular economy business models

#### 3.3 Social Impact and Livelihood Opportunities

The baseline identifies unemployed youth, women in coastal markets, and informal waste pickers who can be mobilized for:

Plastic collection and upcycling

Educational outreach and behavioral change campaigns

Small-scale enterprise incubation with microcredit or revolving funds

These offer indirect blue carbon benefits by reducing pollution stress on wetland ecosystems.

### Conclusion

The integration of carbon trading, improved cookstoves, and plastic management within the WACA Blue Carbon framework is not only innovative but strategic. It offers Ghana a multi-benefit pathway where climate mitigation, ecosystem restoration, and community development intersect.

The socio-economic baseline study anchors this integration by providing granular, localized data on behavior, needs, and capacities, thereby enabling the design of bankable, equitable, and measurable interventions that can access climate finance and enhance resilience for years to come.

# 3.0 Methodology

## 3.1 Study Design

### Introduction

The design of the socio-economic baseline study for the WACA Blue Carbon Assessment was deliberately participatory, qualitative, and community-centered. It reflects the imperative that climate and ecosystem interventions, especially those grounded in carbon finance and community resource governance, must begin with a detailed, lived understanding of how people interact with natural systems. The study was executed across three key Ramsar-designated wetland systems in Ghana: Songor (Greater Accra Region), Keta Lagoon Complex (Volta Region), and Muni-Pomadze (Central Region).

This section presents the overall study design framework, the principles that guided it, and the rationale for choosing a qualitative and participatory approach. It also describes how the design aligns with the ecological objectives of the blue carbon initiative, the carbon market requirements, and the need for safeguards compliance, FPIC (Free, Prior, and Informed Consent), and inclusiveness.

### Purpose of the Study Design

The primary purpose of the socio-economic baseline was to generate:

A multi-dimensional, site-specific understanding of community livelihoods, governance, and vulnerability

Disaggregated data to inform CREMA establishment, blue carbon MRV systems, and equitable benefit-sharing

A reliable foundation for carbon and co-benefit accounting under voluntary and regulated carbon markets

A mechanism for integrating community knowledge, perceptions, and aspirations into environmental decision-making

Given these goals, a traditional extractive survey approach (e.g., large-scale household questionnaires) would have been insufficient. Instead, the study was designed to be iterative, dialogical, and grounded in mutual learning. In addition, the study relied on the Community Profile Reports, Current State Assessment of Natural Resources Management Groups in the Project Areas, prepared by the Communities themselves, as well as secondary data largely from the 2021 Population and Housing Census by the Ghana Statistical Service to triangulate and enhance the field data collected.

### Justification for a Participatory Qualitative Approach

#### 1. Complexity of Community-Ecosystem Relationships

Coastal communities interact with ecosystems in diverse ways, economically, spiritually, culturally, and historically. These relationships cannot be reduced to binary indicators or pre-coded answers. For example:

A mangrove stand may simultaneously serve as a fuel source, a spiritual grove, a fish nursery, and a symbol of clan identity.

Women may use wetlands differently from men, not only in purpose (e.g., fish processing vs. pole cutting), but also in timing, access, and risk exposure.
Capturing these complexities requires a design that privileges narratives, stories, and visual tools.

#### 2. Safeguards and FPIC Requirements

Under the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) and voluntary carbon market standards (e.g., Verra CCB), baseline data must be gathered in a way that reflects:

Free, prior, and informed consent

Local language use and translation

Equitable participation, especially of women, youth, and vulnerable groups

Sensitivity to spiritual sites, cultural protocols, and governance traditions

A participatory qualitative approach allows these standards to be met without imposing external assumptions on community structures or values.

#### 3. Need for Community Ownership and Monitoring

Because the WACA Blue Carbon project aims to build long-term community-led governance structures (e.g., CREMAs), the study design needed to:

Empower communities to describe their own systems and challenges

Provide a rehearsal space for future monitoring roles

Introduce participatory tools that can be reused by CRMCs or local institutions

The methods used were chosen not only for data extraction, but also for capacity-building and trust-building.

### Embedded Flexibility in the Design

Recognizing the diverse socio-ecological landscapes of the three Ramsar sites, the design included flexible features such as:

Site-specific adaptation of tools: For example, lagoon-side communities in Keta emphasized fishing, while Muni communities focused on farming. Discussion guides were adjusted accordingly.

Multiple entry points: Depending on the social structure, some communities were accessed through chiefs, others through CREMA committees or Assembly members.

Community-led timing: Meetings were not scheduled around fishing hours, religious days, and cultural festivals to reduce disruption and increase turnout.

Tool switching: In cases where mapping was too technical or not locally understood, facilitators used storytelling or spatial listing as alternatives.

This flexibility enhanced the responsiveness, accuracy, and cultural appropriateness of the research process.

The study design incorporated Community Change Agents (CCAs)nominated from each community as co-facilitators and mobilizers. Their roles included:

Assisting in participatory mapping and recording of ecological sites

Debriefing field teams on community dynamics, tensions, or sensitivities

Documenting observations for later use in CREMA planning

This embedded approach helped build continuity between the baseline study and future CREMA-based management systems.

### Integration with Ecological and Carbon Assessments

The socio-economic study design was also coordinated with the ecological field teams (GIS, carbon stock inventory, wetland specialists) through:

Shared site maps and visit schedules

Alignment of ecological observation sites with community-defined pressure zones

Cross-referencing of socio-economic indicators (e.g., degraded mangrove zones, fishing effort) with ecological data

Preparation of site-specific socio-ecological basemaps to support restoration planning

This integrated design approach ensures that carbon and social baselines can be analyzed in tandem, making Ghana’s Blue Carbon system more robust and investment-ready.

### Conclusion

The study design adopted for the WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic baseline represents a best-practice approach in community-driven environmental planning. By embedding participation, inclusiveness, respect for tradition, and ecological alignment into every phase of the process, the design provided more than just data; it built relationships, capacities, and a social foundation for long-term climate resilience.

This design was not merely about gathering information; it was about preparing communities and institutions to be full partners in Ghana’s blue carbon future.

## 3.2 Tools Used and Justification

### Introduction

This section presents the specific data collection tools used in the WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic baseline study and provides justifications for their selection. Given the complex social, environmental, and institutional dynamics of Ghana’s coastal Ramsar communities, the study prioritized qualitative tools that could capture lived experiences, local knowledge, seasonal variations, and governance realities. The tools were selected to ensure inclusivity, cultural relevance, methodological rigor, and alignment with FPIC, gender equity, and safeguards standards under both World Bank and voluntary carbon frameworks (e.g., Verra, Plan Vivo).

The tools served not only as research instruments but also as community engagement mechanisms that introduced dialogue, built trust, and supported early mobilization for CREMA and blue carbon planning.

### Tool 1: Focus Group Discussions (FGDs)

#### Purpose

FGDs were the primary tool used to understand collective community experiences, variations in livelihood practices, gender roles, environmental concerns, and social norms.

#### Application

Conducted in all 37+ communities across the three Ramsar sites

Separate FGDs were held for men, women, youth, and sometimes fish processors, farmers, or migrants

Each FGD had 6–12 participants selected with community leader and Change Agent support

Guides were structured around key domains: livelihoods, resource use, seasonal calendars, degradation trends, governance, health, and aspirations

#### Justification

FGDs enabled open discussion of shared community knowledge

Participants validated and corrected previous profiling findings from 2024

Enabled intergenerational and gender-specific perspectives without dominance from elite individuals

FGDs helped surface nuances in cultural practices (e.g., fishing taboos, menstruation norms, land allocation rituals)

#### Notable Outcomes

Identification of seasonal variation in mangrove use

Clarification of gendered time burdens (e.g., fuelwood collection)

Collective mapping of erosion hotspots and plastic accumulation zones

Documentation of women’s informal leadership in trade and resource management

### Tool 2: Key Informant Interviews (KIIs)

#### Purpose

KIIs were used to gather expert or historical insights on governance, policy implementation, environmental change, and CREMA readiness.

#### Application

Conducted with traditional leaders (chiefs, queen mothers, elders), elected officials (assembly members), Wildlife Division officers, CRMC/CEC executives, school heads, and spiritual leaders

At least 3–5 KIIs conducted per community, depending on size and diversity

Semi-structured interview guides tailored per informant group

#### Justification

KIIs provided elite, technical, or historical knowledge that may not surface in FGDs

Offered insight into intra-community dynamics, disputes, and power asymmetries

Verified the existence and functionality of CREMA institutions or past conservation projects

Allowed for sensitive discussions not suited for public FGDs (e.g., land conflicts, chieftaincy disputes)

#### Notable Outcomes

Clarification of land tenure regimes (e.g., family land vs. stool land)

Documentation of past conservation initiatives (e.g., failed mangrove replanting efforts)

Identification of conflicts between traditional authorities and district agencies

Historical narratives on lagoon flooding, mangrove depletion, and migration

### Tool 3: Participatory Resource Mapping

#### Purpose

To enable community members to visualize, demarcate, and explain key physical and ecological features in their environment, including resource zones, degraded areas, sacred sites, and livelihood corridors.

#### Application

Community-drawn maps produced during FGDs or special sessions

Materials included flip charts, markers, local materials (sticks, stones, charcoal), and GPS support where needed

Features mapped: rivers, mangroves, flood zones, erosion sites, spiritual groves, roads, markets, and conflict zones

Maps were photographed and digitized by the field team or shared with GIS experts for integration into ecological overlays

#### Justification

Enabled spatial data collection where technical GIS mapping is not locally accessible

Empowered communities to co-create visual narratives of their own landscape

Facilitated understanding of degradation patterns (e.g., mangrove depletion, farmland salinization)

Enhanced legitimacy of later restoration or carbon MRV mapping by validating with local actors

#### Notable Outcomes

Identification of previously undocumented mangrove stands compared to the global data 

Zoning of degraded vs. intact mangrove stands

Visual layering of livelihood overlaps and seasonal movement

Highlighting of high-risk erosion zones in Songor and Keta Lagoon Complex

### Tool 4: Transect Walks

#### Purpose

Transect walks were used to physically verify ecological, livelihood, and degradation patterns observed or reported in FGDs and KIIs.

#### Application

Conducted with elders, youth, fishers, and women’s leaders as guides

Routes covered mangroves, wetlands, estuaries, degraded forests, and plastic-polluted sites

Observations included signs of biodiversity, erosion, cutting, species presence, sacred markers, and restoration potential

Photos and GPS coordinates were recorded where possible

#### Justification

Directly connected verbal data to physical landscape features

Enhanced credibility of socio-economic data for carbon and ecological teams

Created shared visual evidence for future reporting and community feedback

Enabled triangulation of data across tools

#### Notable Outcomes

Ground-truthing of active mangrove cutting in 14 communities

Identification of low-lying areas vulnerable to tidal flooding

Verification of community-stated erosion and siltation claims

Photo records of charcoal pits, abandoned replanting sites, and fish nursery zones

### Tool 5: Observation and Informal Conversations

#### Purpose

To record spontaneous insights, behaviors, and informal perspectives that arise during transit, breaks, or unstructured encounters.

#### Application

Notes taken during community visits, morning markets, boat rides, or roadside conversations

Observations included: how fuelwood is bundled and stored, presence of women at planning meetings, signage about conservation, fishing gear types, etc.

Informal comments from youth, traders, and PWDs often yielded critical insight

#### Justification

Captured authentic community behavior in natural settings

Allowed field teams to test consistency between formal responses and lived reality

Highlighted contradictions, opportunities, and blind spots in formal data

Reduced courtesy bias, often present in structured sessions

#### Notable Outcomes

Observation of illegal fishing gear not disclosed in FGDs

Spontaneous complaints about leadership exclusion from women and youth

Evidence of plastic burning sites near schools

Informal declarations of interest in mangrove jobs and recycling

### Tool 7: Validation and Feedback Sessions

#### Purpose

To close the loop with communities, verify findings, and build transparency and ownership.

#### Application

Conducted in selected communities at the end of the data collection phase

Shared initial maps, summaries of livelihood data, governance profiles, and identified pressures

Communities asked to confirm, correct, or elaborate on findings

Feedback recorded for incorporation into final reports

#### Justification

Aligned with FPIC and ethical research standards

Improved accuracy by validating community voices

Established trust and credibility ahead of implementation phases

Provided opportunity for the community to propose next steps

#### Notable Outcomes

Clarification of overlapping leadership roles and land claims

Community endorsement of Change Agents for future CREMA roles

Added depth to plastic waste and fuelwood scarcity narratives

Enhanced willingness to engage in future restoration or monitoring roles

### Conclusion

The selection and use of these tools were driven by the unique socio-cultural, ecological, and governance context of Ghana’s coastal Ramsar sites. Together, they created a multi-layered understanding of resource use, vulnerability, decision-making, and opportunity. More importantly, they ensured that data collection was not extractive, but embedded in local dialogue, respect, and capacity-building.

The tools deployed in this study have also laid the groundwork for future participatory monitoring, CREMA planning, carbon MRV, and inclusive benefit-sharing mechanisms. They are replicable, scalable, and can serve as reference models for similar blue carbon assessments across the West African coastline.

## 3.3 Sampling: Community Selection, Representation, and Segmentation

### Introduction

Sampling is a critical component of any field-based socio-economic assessment. In the WACA Blue Carbon initiative, the sampling approach was designed to reflect the diverse ecological zones, socio-cultural structures, and resource-use typologies found across Ghana’s three coastal Ramsar sites, Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze. This section explains how communities were selected for profiling and validation, how respondents were identified to ensure inclusion and representativeness, and how internal segmentation (by gender, age, and occupation) was achieved in the data collection process.

The overall goal was to ensure that the data collected reflected the diversity of community contexts, recognized differentiated resource dependencies, and enabled disaggregation for gender, governance, and vulnerability analyses.

### 1. Community Selection Strategy

#### a. Geographic Scope

The study was implemented across three Ramsar sites selected for their ecological significance, vulnerability to degradation, and policy priority within the WACA ResIP 2 program. Each site hosts a unique ecosystem typology and a mix of wetland-dependent communities:



These sites were selected not only for their ecological value but also because of the presence of restoration potential, past CREMA engagement, livelihood pressure, and policy relevance.

#### b. Criteria for Community Inclusion

The following criteria guided community selection within each site:

Proximity to mangroves, wetlands, estuaries, and lagoon systems

Evidence of natural resource dependency (fishing, farming, salt production, etc.)

Presence of degradation indicators (e.g., erosion, deforestation, pollution)

Existing or potential CREMA initiatives or environmental committees

Inclusion of communities engaged in earlier GIS ground-truthing under WACA

Spatial distribution across upstream, midstream, and coastal zones

Cultural, ethnic, and institutional diversity

These criteria ensured both ecological representativeness and social heterogeneity, allowing the study to capture a broad spectrum of livelihood-environment interactions.

### 2. Community Coverage Overview

A total of 37 communities were sampled across the three Ramsar sites:

a. Keta Lagoon Complex (20 communities)

Communities include: Dzita, Sota, Agbatsivi, Agortoe, Galo, Tunu, Atorkor, Bomigo, Akplorwotorkor, Klonu, Galotse, Anyanui, Agorkedzi, Atiteti, Tunu/Gblife, Salo, Aborlove, Nolopi, Dzogborve, Korsikope.

These communities span the lagoon shoreline, barrier sandspit, estuarine arms, and floodplains. Some are accessible only by canoe (e.g., Bomigo), and others are densely populated fishing hubs (e.g., Dzita).

b. Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site (7 communities)

Communities include: Sankor, Nsuekyir, Woarabeba, Akosua Village, Bewadze, Mankoadze, Osubonpanyin.

Located along the lower Ayensu River and inland hills, these communities represent a mix of fishing, farming, and charcoal production livelihoods.

c. Songor Ramsar Site (10 communities)

Communities include: Ayigbo, Azizanya, Elavanyo, Luhuese, Obane, Ocanseykope, Patukope, Pute, Torgbloku, Totope.

Salt extraction, mangrove harvesting, and contested land-use zones characterize this area. Some communities are semi-isolated due to poor road infrastructure.

The spread across districts and ecological subzones allowed the sampling framework to reflect environmental, cultural, and institutional variation.

### 3. Community Typology and Stratification

To enable comparative analysis and tailored programming, communities were grouped into typologies based on:

Ecosystem Position: upland, coastal, lagoon-edge, estuarine

Livelihood Profile: fishery-dominant, farm-dominant, salt-mining, multi-sectoral

Governance Status: traditional authority-led, CREMA emerging, NGO-supported

Accessibility and Service Levels: accessible by road, island-based, presence of schools or clinics

This typology helped structure the fieldwork plan and informed the segmentation of respondents during FGDs and KIIs.

### 4. Respondent Representation Strategy

Within each community, sampling aimed to capture voices across:

Gender (men, women,)

Age (elders, youth, adults in working age)

Occupation (fishers, farmers, traders, processors, herders)

Social status (chiefs, widows, PWDs, migrants, youth leaders)

Governance roles (assembly members, CREMA leaders, teachers, religious heads)

The respondent selection was guided by the Community Change Agents (CCAs), who helped:

Identify known and trusted individuals across demographic categories

Balance representation without favoritism or political interference

Invite both vocal and quieter individuals for inclusive dialogue

Reach out to marginalized groups not often included in formal processes

### 5. Gender and Age Segmentation

Gender-responsive and youth-inclusive sampling was embedded into the methodology:

a. Gender-Based Sampling

Separate FGDs for women and men in all communities

Priority given to women processors, traders, and caregivers

Female facilitators assigned to women-only sessions, where possible

Target: at least 40% of all total participants to be women

b. Youth Segmentation

FGDs specifically for youth (15–35 years) in most communities

Separate groups from elders to enable freer expression

Participants included in-school and out-of-school youth, fishmongers, apprentices, and unemployed young adults

Inclusion of youth leaders from cultural, sports, and religious groups

c. Marginalized Group Inclusion

Where possible, representatives of persons with disabilities (PWDs) were included

Migrants, tenant farmers, and minority ethnic groups were intentionally sampled

Spiritual practitioners and custodians of sacred sites were interviewed separately

### 6. Sample Size Overview

The overall field engagement spanned:

>100 FGDs, averaging 2–3 per community

>150 Key Informant Interviews, across district and community levels

37 Participatory resource maps, with additional sub-maps in complex settlements

>30 Transect walks, typically one per ecological zone in each community

7 site-level synthesis reports, prepared post-fieldwork to guide analysis

This robust sample ensured both depth and breadth of community representation.

### 7. Inclusion and Exclusion Justifications

Some communities were excluded from this phase of the baseline based on:

Lack of current wetland or mangrove coverage

Security or access concerns during the study period

Prior extensive data saturation with minimal change in context

Recommendations from the Wildlife Division or District Assemblies

These exclusions were documented transparently and may be revisited in future phases.

### 8. Limitations and Mitigation in Sampling

Despite a carefully designed sampling strategy, a few limitations were encountered:

In some areas, social fragmentation (e.g., chieftaincy disputes) limited inclusive participation

PWD participation was lower than targeted 

Mitigation measures included:

Return visits to low-participation sites

Use of youth or women leaders to encourage attendance

Cross-checking narratives with transect walk observations

Supplementing low-representation communities with data from similar typologies

### Conclusion

The sampling strategy employed in the WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic study was grounded in both methodological rigor and social realism. It ensured that the communities most dependent on blue carbon ecosystems were adequately represented and that marginalized voices were heard and respected.

This stratified, participatory sampling approach supports not only baseline integrity but also future targeting of interventions, development of benefit-sharing mechanisms, and design of community-specific restoration and livelihood support packages.

## 3.4 Community Entry, FPIC Process, and Ethical Considerations

### Introduction

Respectful community entry and ethical engagement are foundational to any participatory development initiative, especially those involving sensitive topics like land use, natural resource access, cultural practices, and potential benefit-sharing from carbon markets. The WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic baseline study was designed and executed in accordance with principles of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC), the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (ESF), and best practices for ethical social research in rural and coastal communities.

This section outlines how community entry was structured, how FPIC was operationalized before and during data collection, and what ethical protocols were followed to ensure the safety, autonomy, and dignity of all participants.

### 1. Purpose and Importance of Ethical Entry

Ethical community entry is not only about accessing information, it is about:

Building trust that underpins long-term relationships with communities

Honoring traditional authority structures and gatekeepers

Creating a safe space for genuine dialogue across gender, age, and social group

Avoiding extractive research behavior and power imbalances

Ensuring compliance with national laws, district regulations, and international safeguards

The credibility and validity of the socio-economic data collected rely heavily on the legitimacy of the entry and consent process. It also sets the tone for later phases of CREMA development, restoration works, and carbon project implementation.

### 2. Overview of Entry Protocol

The following steps were followed systematically before entering each community:

#### a. Advance Notification

Letters of intent and endorsement were sent to District Assemblies, copied to relevant departments (Planning, Forestry, Environment, Social Welfare)

Site-level Wildlife Division officers informed traditional leaders ahead of visits

Change Agents and Assembly Members helped schedule convenient entry times

Timing avoided sacred days (e.g., Tuesdays in Keta, Fridays in Songor), market days, and funerals

#### b. Courtesy and Protocol Visits

The field team initiated each visit with a call on the chief or elders

Small tokens of respect (e.g., cola nuts, water) were offered, based on local custom

Chiefs, queen mothers, and council members were briefed in clear and respectful language about:

The purpose of the visit

The voluntary nature of participation

The study’s alignment with ongoing CREMA/blue carbon efforts

How findings would be used (non-commercial, non-political)

That no payments or immediate benefits were involved

Permission was requested, not presumed

#### c. Public Durbars or Introductory Meetings

In many communities, a short durbar or public gathering was held to introduce the project

The facilitation team and Change Agents explained the purpose in the local language

Attendees were encouraged to ask questions, suggest participants, or raise concerns

This three-step process ensured community-wide awareness and support before data collection began.

### 3. Operationalizing FPIC Principles

The study followed the four FPIC pillars:

#### a. Free

Participation was voluntary; no person was forced, coerced, or punished for declining

No monetary or material incentives were offered

Participants were allowed to leave or withdraw at any time

Youth and women’s FGDs were held separately, where needed to reduce pressure from dominant groups

#### b. Prior

Consent was obtained before any interviews, FGDs, mapping, or photography

Schedules were discussed and approved ahead of time with traditional leaders and Change Agents

Participants had at least 24 hours’ notice before attending any group session

#### c. Informed

All facilitators were trained to clearly explain the project’s scope, benefits, risks, and limitations

Explanation covered:

The source of the project (WACA, MLNR, Wildlife Division)

The difference between this study and other NGO/government programs

How data would be stored, used, and anonymized

The fact that participation does not guarantee job opportunities or funding

#### d. Consent

Verbal consent was obtained from all participants and recorded in field logs

Consent was reconfirmed before photos were taken or locations were mapped

In vulnerable groups (e.g., PWDs, the elderly), a helper or relative was allowed to assist with understanding



### 4. Safeguards and Social Protections

The study applied key social safeguards to protect participants:



These protections aligned with ESS7 (Indigenous Peoples/Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Groups) and ESS10 (Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure) of the World Bank ESF.

### 5. Managing Expectations and Misunderstandings

In some communities, past experiences with external projects (e.g., NGOs, donor-funded initiatives) led to confusion or skepticism. To address this:

The study team emphasized that this was a baseline study, not a delivery mission

Expectations were clarified using examples: “This visit is like a camera that takes a picture of how your community is today, not yet the team that brings resources.”

Communities were assured that findings would be shared back with them and their District Assemblies

A small summary leaflet was left behind in some communities summarizing WACA’s purpose and blue carbon themes

This proactive communication helped minimize rumors, disappointment, or resistance

### 6. Ethical Data Collection and Storage

The field team upheld key ethical principles during and after data collection:

a. Anonymity

No names were recorded in field notes unless provided by a formal stakeholder

Audio recordings were not used to protect participant comfort and privacy

b. Confidentiality

Sensitive topics (e.g., internal conflicts, illegal activities) were handled privately

Data shared in reports was generalized to avoid identifying specific individuals or households

c. Data Security

Field notes and photos were stored in password-protected folders

Maps and GPS coordinates were not shared with third parties without MLNR authorization

No data was used for commercial or media purposes

### 7. Community Exit and Follow-Up

At the end of each visit:

The team thanked community leaders and participants formally

Feedback was collected on the experience of the engagement

Key concerns (e.g., youth unemployment, ecosystem degradation, land conflicts) were noted and flagged for discussion in district-level synthesis

Change Agents and Wildlife Officers were tasked with following up on further queries

In a few pilot sites, maps or diagrams were left behind to support community discussions

Exit rituals respected local customs, and teams avoided abrupt departures.

### Conclusion

The integrity and usefulness of this socio-economic baseline rely not only on the information collected but on the way it was collected. By embedding FPIC, ethics, and local protocols into every phase of community entry, the WACA Blue Carbon team has laid a solid foundation for long-term trust, cooperation, and community ownership of Ghana’s coastal restoration and carbon transition agenda.

This approach is not only morally sound, but it is strategically essential for the sustainability, legitimacy, and replicability of blue carbon interventions across West Africa.

## 3.5 Languages and Cultural Protocols

### Introduction

Language and culture are the gateways to meaningful participation in environmental governance. In Ghana’s coastal communities, where ecological management is tightly woven into spiritual life, ancestral practice, and oral knowledge systems, successful project implementation requires more than translation. It requires deep cultural sensitivity, respect for traditional norms, and strategic use of language as a tool for inclusion, clarity, and empowerment.

This section explains how local languages were used during the WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic study and how cultural practices shaped community engagement, data collection, and trust-building. It also discusses how respecting these systems laid the groundwork for CREMA development, blue carbon monitoring, and long-term community buy-in.

### 1. Language Diversity Across Study Sites

Ghana’s coastal belt is linguistically diverse. Each of the three Ramsar sites presents its own language landscape:



Some communities, such as Bomigo or Akplorwotorkor, use localized dialects or terms for ecological and spiritual features that are not easily translatable. In these cases, Change Agents played a key role in providing nuanced interpretation.

### 2. Language as a Tool for Participation

a. FGDs and KIIs in Local Languages

All focus group discussions and key informant interviews were conducted in the dominant local language of the community.

Facilitators worked in teams to translate guides orally, avoiding written questionnaires that alienate low-literacy participants.

Local expressions and metaphors were encouraged to enhance clarity and emotional resonance. For example:

Ewe phrase for degraded mangrove: “dzi le afei” (the land is dying)

Dangme expression for water pollution: “shɔ shɔmi nu” (the river has been insulted)

b. Women’s Sessions in Mother Tongue

In communities where women had limited exposure to formal education, their FGDs were conducted entirely in their mother tongue.

This led to higher confidence, better storytelling, and deeper disclosures, especially around fuelwood use, health impacts, and economic burdens.

In Sota, Sankor, and Klonu, this approach transformed previously silent groups into active co-creators of knowledge.

c. Use of Translators and Local Facilitators

When external facilitators did not speak the local language, Change Agents or CBO reps were used as interpreters.

These interpreters were trained to:

Maintain accuracy and neutrality

Explain technical terms like “carbon market” or “CREMA” in plain language

Respect confidentiality and refrain from inserting personal views

This ensured that all community members, regardless of education level, could fully engage.

### 3. Cultural Protocols Observed

Community culture informed every stage of the study, from entry to exit, and from tool design to feedback delivery.

a. Respect for Traditional Authority

All engagements began with formal greetings and libation rituals, where appropriate.

Chiefs, queen mothers, and elders were seated according to custom, and junior officers deferred to local hierarchies.

Facilitation teams dressed modestly and addressed leaders using proper titles (e.g., Togbui, Nana, Wulomei).

b. Sacred Days and Ritual Taboos

Sacred or taboo days (e.g., Tuesdays in Keta, Fridays in Muni) were strictly avoided for interviews or mapping.

In Dzogborve, Elavanyo, and Akosua Village, these days were used for community rest, ancestral worship, or environmental observance.

Disrespecting these rules would have invalidated community trust and potentially jeopardized future access.

c. Gender Norms in Engagement

Women were given their own spaces and facilitators to avoid shame, interruption, or deference.

d. Handling of Sacred and Spiritual Sites

Mapping of sacred groves, shrines, and ancestral forests was done only with permission.

This demonstrated cultural humility and reinforced the study’s legitimacy.

### 4. Documentation and Recording

Though much of the work was verbal, the team documented:

Local names for ecological features

Translations of key concepts for reference in CREMA training materials

These linguistic artifacts are being compiled to support communication materials for WACA’s next phase.

### Conclusion

By centering local languages and cultural protocols, the WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic baseline study not only improved data quality, it deepened legitimacy, empowered participants, and laid the foundation for durable community ownership. Respecting culture is not an add-on to blue carbon interventions; it is the soil in which they must grow.

As the project advances to restoration, CREMA development, and carbon finance planning, these insights will guide inclusive messaging, benefit-sharing dialogue, and safeguards implementation across Ghana’s coastal communities.

## 3.6 Limitations and Challenges

### Introduction

No socio-economic study, particularly one of this scope, scale, and cultural complexity, can be executed without encountering challenges. The WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic baseline study was implemented across 37+ communities, over three Ramsar sites, involving hundreds of participants across diverse demographic, geographic, and institutional settings. Despite meticulous planning and the strength of the participatory methodology, several logistical, social, environmental, and contextual challenges were encountered.

This section provides a reflective overview of the study’s limitations, the obstacles encountered during planning and fieldwork, and how these were mitigated. Documenting these challenges is essential for:

Transparency and methodological integrity

Interpreting the findings within their context

Informing the design of future studies, CREMA engagements, and carbon MRV systems

Flagging risks that could re-emerge in implementation and monitoring phases

### 1. Logistical and Infrastructure Challenges

#### a. Difficult Terrain and Inaccessibility

Several communities were physically isolated or located in swampy, tidal, or island environments. For example:

Bomigo Island required canoe transport and had no landing site

Galotse and Agbatsivi were partially cut off by floodwaters during field visits

Road access was limited or impassable in wet seasons, especially in the southern sections of Songor and Keta

Some transect routes were unsafe due to slippery or eroded banks

#### Mitigation:

Teams used motorcycles, canoes, and foot travel where possible

Adjusted schedules based on tides and weather forecasts

Prioritized representative communities when terrain prevented full coverage

b. Transportation Costs and Delays

Fuel shortages in some districts affected vehicle availability

Poor road conditions led to extended travel times and team fatigue

Last-minute vehicle breakdowns caused missed appointments in 6 communities

Mitigation:

Teams built in buffer days and cross-covered for each other

Change Agents communicated delays and rescheduled when needed



### 2. Environmental and Seasonal Constraints

#### a. Unpredictable Weather Conditions

Heavy rains in some areas delayed transect walks, limited outdoor FGDs, and increased absenteeism

High tides submerged parts of mapped zones in Muni-Pomadze

#### Mitigation:

Teams conducted indoor sessions where possible

Used visual prompts (photos, diagrams) when physical observations were not feasible

In some cases, ecological observations were deferred to GIS or carbon teams

b. Seasonal Migration of Participants

Fishing communities had reduced male populations during active sea harvest periods

Youth participants had left for urban centers or salt jobs (especially in Songor)

Seasonal farming activities pulled participants away from FGDs mid-discussion

Mitigation:

Scheduled visits outside peak livelihood hours (e.g., early mornings, late afternoons)

Rescheduled or split sessions to reach mobile participants

Relied on women and elders for longitudinal memory in absent groups

### 3. Sociopolitical and Institutional Dynamics



a. Low Institutional Memory or Data Fatigue

Many communities had participated in previous studies or consultations, leading to:

Disinterest or “survey fatigue”

Expectations of immediate benefits or handouts

Inconsistency in recollections of past project engagements

Mitigation:

Teams clarified the purpose and limits of the current study upfront

Used Change Agents to motivate honest, renewed participation

Triangulated with past profiling documents to fill gaps

### 4. Social and Cultural Sensitivities

a. Taboos and Sacred Practices

Cultural taboos limited access to sacred groves, estuaries, or family-owned mangrove lands

Religious holidays and funerals caused cancellations in 9 communities

Mitigation:

Respected all sacred days and practices; avoided probing restricted topics

Held gender-specific and age-appropriate discussions for sensitive issues

Returned to communities after funerals or festivals to complete sessions

### 5. Challenges with Change Agent Availability and Capacity

Some Change Agents had competing livelihood or domestic obligations

Others lacked facilitation confidence, note-taking skills, or familiarity with research protocols

Turnover occurred in three sites where original CCAs migrated for seasonal work

Mitigation:

Briefed agents ahead of sessions.

Provided printed materials and talking points

District officers filled in as backstops in critical communities

### 6. Limitations in Representation and Coverage

a. Underrepresentation of PWDs and Minority Groups

Persons with disabilities were underrepresented in most FGDs

Fulani herders and migrant fishers were mobile and hard to reach

Mitigation:

PWD representatives were sought through social welfare officers

Snowball sampling used to reach migrants

### Conclusion

These limitations do not compromise the integrity of the socio-economic baseline. Rather, they provide necessary context for interpreting the findings, refining the data collection instruments, and improving the next phase of community engagement. The fact that these challenges were recorded, reflected upon, and, where possible, mitigated speaks to the methodological maturity of the WACA Blue Carbon assessment process.

As the project advances toward CREMA formation, restoration implementation, and carbon credit generation, this honest appraisal of field challenges will serve as a vital tool for adaptive management, risk anticipation, and process improvement.

## 4.1 Songor Ramsar Site

### Introduction

The Songor Ramsar Site is one of Ghana’s six internationally recognized wetlands under the Ramsar Convention and holds critical ecological, economic, and cultural importance. Located in the Ada East District of the Greater Accra Region, Songor is a complex mosaic of lagoons, mangrove stands, salt flats, tidal floodplains, and brackish water systems. It is also one of the most anthropogenically pressured Ramsar sites in the country, facing challenges from salt mining expansion, mangrove deforestation, coastal erosion, and growing land-use conflict.

This section provides an in-depth overview of the site’s geography, ecological significance, demographic profile, resource use, governance structures, and its relevance within the WACA Blue Carbon initiative. The Songor landscape reflects the tensions and opportunities at the heart of blue carbon restoration, where fragile ecosystems meet intense livelihood dependence, layered governance, and high potential for carbon and co-benefit interventions.

### 1. Geographic and Ecological Setting

The Songor Ramsar Site lies approximately 90 kilometers east of Accra and spans roughly 51,000 hectares. It is situated between the Volta River estuary to the east and the Dangme East coastal settlements to the west. The site encompasses:

Songor Lagoon: A shallow saline waterbody with seasonal connections to the sea

Tidal wetlands and salt flats: Critical habitats for migratory birds, crustaceans, and marine nurseries

Fringing mangroves: Though heavily degraded, patches of Rhizophora and Avicennia species persist

Floodplains and grasslands: Supporting rain-fed farming and seasonal grazing

Sand dunes and estuarine channels: Acting as natural coastal defenses

The lagoon’s seasonal dynamics, marked by alternate flooding and drying, shape the livelihood patterns and ecological cycles of surrounding communities. The area is also part of the East Atlantic Flyway and supports over 70 species of waterbirds during migratory periods.

### 2. Socio-Economic and Demographic Profile

Songor hosts over 40,000 people across multiple peri-coastal and inland communities. Ten of these communities were profiled in this study:

Totope, Torgbloku, Pute, Patukope, Obane, Elavanyo, Ocanseykope, Azizanya, Luhuese, and Ayigbo

Key demographic trends observed include:

Youth-dominated populations: Over 60% of residents in some communities are below age 35

Large, extended households: Common in fishing and salt-producing settlements

Gendered economic roles: Men dominate salt mining and fishing, while women lead processing and trade

High poverty incidence: Due to fluctuating incomes, limited infrastructure, and high resource competition

Low formal education levels: Particularly among adult women and migrant groups

Ethnic groups are predominantly Dangme, with Ewe and Ga minorities. Religion is a mix of Christianity, traditional spirituality, and Islam, with strong cultural adherence to local rituals and land-based taboos.

### 3. Livelihood Systems and Resource Use

The Songor landscape sustains a multi-sectoral livelihood economy centered on:

a. Salt Mining

Songor is a nationally significant salt-producing area

Local residents, cooperatives, and large companies (e.g., Electrochem Ghana Ltd) extract salt from pans and flats

Salt production is seasonal but labor-intensive, employing thousands in both formal and informal systems

Conflicts exist between large-scale operators and local communities over access and control

b. Fishing

Lagoon and estuarine fishing are seasonal and declining due to siltation and overuse

Fishing gears include cast nets, traps, and hook lines

Women engage in drying, smoking, and selling of fish at local and regional markets

c. Mangrove Harvesting

Mangroves are cut primarily for fuelwood (fish smoking) and construction poles

No formal harvesting permits or replanting schemes exist at scale

Communities like Totope and Obane rely almost entirely on mangrove fuel during the dry season

d. Farming and Gardening

Inland communities (e.g., Luhuese, Ayigbo) grow cassava, okra, maize, and vegetables

Coastal erosion and soil salinity increasingly reduce productivity

Irrigation is limited and rainfall is unreliable

e. Petty Trading and Labour Migration

Women engage in fish, salt, and charcoal trading

Seasonal migration to Accra, Tema, and Togo is common among youth

### 4. Degradation Pressures and Environmental Challenges

The ecological integrity of the Songor site is under serious threat from:

Large-scale salt mining expansion: Leading to land dispossession, mangrove clearing, and erosion

Unregulated fuelwood collection: Causing loss of mangrove buffers

Siltation of the lagoon: Due to catchment degradation and sediment runoff

Coastal erosion: Resulting in loss of farmland and displacement in Azizanya and Totope

Plastic and waste accumulation: Particularly along fishing zones

Weak enforcement: No functional zoning system or effective by-laws for resource use

Community awareness of degradation is high, but capacity and alternatives remain limited.

### 5. Governance and Institutional Landscape

Songor’s governance is marked by overlapping and often contested layers:

a. Traditional Authority

The Ada Traditional Council oversees most land and spiritual practices

Chiefs in individual communities play critical roles in land allocation, conflict resolution, and ritual observances

Chieftaincy disputes and internal factionalism occasionally undermine unity

b. District and State Agencies

The Ada East District Assembly has environmental, planning, and community development mandates

The Wildlife Division holds statutory responsibility for Ramsar site management

EPA and Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources provide technical oversight but are operationally distant

c. Emerging Local Institutions

Community Change Agents (CCAs) have been selected across all profiled communities

Local environmental committees are weak or dormant in most areas

No CREMA has yet been formally established, though discussions have begun in Pute and Patukope

The institutional context is complex, under-resourced, and marked by low trust in external actors.

### 6. Cultural and Spiritual Dimensions

Songor communities maintain strong spiritual connections to land and water:

Sacred sites (e.g., groves near Pute, estuary shrines) are still respected by elders and traditionalists

Taboos (e.g., no fishing or salt mining on certain days) are widely observed

Cultural festivals like Asafotufiam blend ancestral remembrance with environmental observance

Totems and land-based rituals shape access and harvesting practices

These systems present both constraints and opportunities for engaging communities in participatory restoration and co-management.

### 7. Relevance to the WACA Blue Carbon Initiative

Songor’s inclusion in the WACA Blue Carbon Assessment is strategic due to:

High restoration potential: Extensive degraded mangrove zones and opportunity for woodlot development

Clear climate co-benefits: Reducing pressure on forests (mangroves) through improved cookstoves and sustainable salt processing

Intense carbon interest: Opportunities for salt industry and communities to pilot carbon-linked livelihood interventions

CREMA potential: High community interest, Change Agent availability, and Wildlife Division presence

Urgent need: Accelerating degradation and socio-political fragility demand coordinated action

The site represents a test case for balancing restoration, livelihoods, and land conflict resolution under blue carbon frameworks.

### Conclusion

The Songor Ramsar Site is a microcosm of the blue carbon challenge: immense ecological and carbon value coexisting with extreme livelihood dependence, governance complexity, and degradation pressure. This study’s baseline data confirms that any intervention must be multi-layered, addressing environmental protection, livelihood diversification, institutional strengthening, and cultural engagement simultaneously.

As Ghana advances in designing CREMAs, piloting carbon methodologies, and implementing co-benefit strategies, Songor stands out as both a high-risk and high-opportunity site, a front line for innovation, inclusion, and restoration.

## 4.2 Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site

### Introduction

The Keta Lagoon Complex is the largest of Ghana’s designated Ramsar sites and one of the most biologically and culturally significant wetland ecosystems in West Africa. Stretching along the southeastern coast of the Volta Region, it spans an estimated 1,200 km², incorporating expansive lagoon waters, mangrove forests, estuarine arms, floodplains, freshwater swamps, and a fragile sandspit that separates the lagoon from the Gulf of Guinea.

This sub-section presents a comprehensive profile of the Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, detailing its ecological features, socio-economic setting, community dynamics, environmental pressures, governance architecture, and strategic relevance under the WACA Blue Carbon initiative. The site is home to 20 communities profiled under this study and serves as a central landscape in Ghana’s blue carbon and coastal resilience agenda.

### 1. Ecological Setting and Significance

The Keta Lagoon Complex is defined by a unique blend of hydrological, estuarine, and coastal processes. The lagoon’s brackish waters are fed by the Volta River system, with inputs from the Tordzie, Aka, and Belikpa rivers, and tidal influxes from multiple inlets including Anyanui and Salo.

Key ecological features include:

Mangrove zones: Patches of Rhizophora racemosa and Avicennia africana, primarily around Sota, Galo, Agbatsivi, and Bomigo

Lagoon and estuarine waterbodies: Habitat for shellfish, finfish, mollusks, and crustaceans

Sandspit barrier and beach ridges: Stretching from Anloga to Akplorwotorkor, protecting the lagoon from direct ocean intrusion

Floodplains and freshwater wetlands: Supporting seasonal agriculture and waterbird feeding grounds

Biodiversity corridors: Part of the East Atlantic Flyway, hosting over 80 migratory bird species

The site’s fragile equilibrium is threatened by human pressure, reduced freshwater inflow, sedimentation, and sea-level rise. Yet, its potential for carbon sequestration and biodiversity recovery remains significant with the right interventions.

### 2. Community Profiles and Demographics

The WACA baseline covered 20 communities across the Keta Municipality, Anloga District, and parts of South Tongu. These include:

Anyanui, Agorkedzi, Atiteti, Tunu/Gblife, Agbledomi, Dzita, Akplorwotorkor, Atorkor, Salo, Agortoe, Agbatsivi, Galo, Sota, Bomigo, Klonu, Aborlove, Nolopi, Dzogborve, Korsikope, Galotse

Demographic trends across these communities reveal:

Predominantly Ewe-speaking populations, with interrelated clan systems

High rates of youth dependency (ages 15–35 comprise over 55% in most areas)

Migrant fishers and traders from Togo and Benin in coastal settlements

Lower educational attainment among adults, especially women

Extended households and seasonal movement linked to fishing and petty trading

The populations are spiritually, economically, and historically bonded to the lagoon system. Nearly every household interacts with the water in some way, fishing, trading, bathing, or using it for rituals.

### 3. Livelihood Systems and Seasonal Cycles

Livelihoods in the Keta Lagoon Complex are intricately tied to the wetland and coastal ecosystem. The primary livelihood sources include:

a. Fishing

Lagoon fishing is the dominant occupation in communities like Dzita, Sota, Agbatsivi, and Tunu

Artisanal gears used include traps, gillnets, and hooks

Seasonal fish migration influences income reliability

Declining fish catch was reported in all profiled communities

b. Fish Processing and Trade

Women dominate post-harvest processes: smoking, drying, and selling

Firewood (especially mangrove) is the primary energy source

Processed fish is sold locally, across Volta Region, and into Greater Accra and Togo

c. Mangrove and Firewood Harvesting

Mangroves are heavily harvested for fuelwood and fencing

High-use communities include Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, and Salo

Harvesting is unregulated and highly extractive, with no active replanting in most communities

d. Farming

Floodplain agriculture includes cassava, okra, maize, and pepper

Soil salinization has reduced yields in Atorkor, Agortoe, and Dzogborve

Upland farming is limited to patches of land with low waterlogging risk

e. Artisanal Craft and Trade

Boat construction, fish basket weaving, and mat making support marginal income

Petty trading of food, clothes, and herbal medicine is widespread among women and youth

f. Eco-cultural Tourism (Emerging)

Bomigo Island and Dzita attract low-scale visitors for birdwatching, cultural festivals, and water-based recreation

Lack of infrastructure and waste management hampers eco-tourism potential

### 4. Environmental Pressures and Degradation Drivers

Despite its ecological value, the Keta Lagoon Complex is among the most degraded wetland systems in Ghana. Pressures include:

a. Overfishing and Gear Conflicts

Juvenile fish harvest, net poisoning, and unapproved gear use were reported

Competition among fishers leads to resource tension, especially in Anyanui and Tunu

b. Uncontrolled Mangrove Cutting

No harvesting guidelines or permits are in place

Mangrove loss is most severe near settlements, Bomigo, Galo, and Sota recorded >70% loss since 2000

Deforestation increases erosion and sedimentation, reducing water quality

c. Siltation and Lagoon Drying

Reduction in freshwater inflow from dam-regulated rivers (e.g., Tordzie, Belikpa)

Increased sedimentation reduces navigability and fish habitats

Some communities have had to shift landing sites or abandon fishing channels

d. Coastal Erosion and Flooding

The sandspit is eroding, particularly around Atiteti and Agbledomi

Flooding events in low-lying zones (e.g., Klonu) displace households annually

e. Waste and Plastic Pollution

Accumulated plastic waste was observed in Dzogborve, Galotse, and Salo

Fish processors burn plastic waste alongside biomass, releasing toxic fumes

### 5. Governance and Institutional Structures

a. Traditional Governance

Most communities are under the Anlo Traditional Council, with sub-chiefs and family heads exercising daily authority

Customary rules guide fishing practices, land allocations, and sacred observances

Queen mothers and elders are active in some areas (e.g., Akplorwotorkor, Tunu)

b. District-Level Agencies

District Assemblies (Keta, Anloga, South Tongu) provide oversight on planning and development

Wildlife Division manages Ramsar governance but lacks staffing for regular enforcement

Fisheries Commission officers are active in Anyanui and Dzita zones

c. Community Institutions

Fishers and fish processors' unions exist in Dzita, Sota, and Agortoe

Local NGOs and church-based groups support some environmental education

Emerging CREMA discussions have begun, but are at various stages

Community Change Agents (CCAs) have been selected in all 20 profiled communities

The governance environment is relatively stable but suffers from fragmentation, weak enforcement, and overdependence on customary structures.

### 6. Cultural Practices and Spiritual Systems

Spirituality and culture remain integral to environmental behavior:

Sacred estuaries and shrines exist in Galo, Tunu, and Akplorwotorkor

Taboos on fishing days (e.g., Tuesdays) are widely respected and enforced

Spirit-based restrictions protect certain groves or fish nurseries

Annual festivals like Hogbetsotso and Atradoza promote cultural unity and environmental reflection

These systems can be harnessed to support blue carbon restoration if approached respectfully.

### 7. Relevance to the WACA Blue Carbon Project

The Keta Lagoon Complex is of high strategic value to Ghana’s blue carbon efforts due to:

Extensive degraded mangrove areas suitable for restoration and carbon sequestration

Intense community dependence on ecosystem services, ideal for modeling co-benefit delivery

Presence of Change Agents and institutional interest in CREMA development

Feasibility for cookstove, fishery zoning, and plastic credit pilots

Transboundary linkages to Togo and Benin, positioning it for regional collaboration under WACA

It also provides a diverse testing ground for combining carbon, biodiversity, and socio-economic metrics under a scalable monitoring system.

### Conclusion

The Keta Lagoon Complex reflects both the promise and peril of Ghana’s coastal wetland systems. With its vast resource base, complex livelihoods, strong cultural identity, and urgent degradation patterns, it is poised to serve as a core landscape for integrated blue carbon action. The site demands interventions that are inclusive, science-driven, and culturally attuned, and offers the scale and complexity required to refine Ghana’s national model for coastal resilience and climate-smart development.

## 4.3 Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site

### Introduction

The Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site, located in Ghana’s Central Region, is a compact but ecologically rich wetland system that straddles the Effutu Municipality and Gomoa West District. Compared to the expansive Songor and Keta Lagoon Complexes, Muni-Pomadze is smaller in scale, yet it exhibits high biodiversity, spiritual significance, and diverse livelihood systems tied to its lagoon, freshwater swamp, and adjoining upland ecosystems.

This section presents a comprehensive profile of the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site based on the fieldwork and community validation conducted under the WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic baseline study. It covers ecological features, community structures, resource dependence, degradation threats, governance dynamics, and the site’s strategic role in Ghana’s coastal resilience and blue carbon agenda.

### 1. Geographic and Ecological Context

Muni-Pomadze spans approximately 9,500 hectares and is centered around the Muni Lagoon, which drains into the Gulf of Guinea just south of Winneba. The site’s ecological zones include:

Muni Lagoon: A brackish lagoon influenced by the Ayensu and Pratu rivers; prone to seasonal opening and closing

Freshwater swamps and floodplains: Dominated by grasses, shrubs, and aquatic plants

Mangrove fringes: Small but ecologically important patches around the lagoon edges

Hillslopes and gallery forests: Serving as transition zones to the north

Sacred groves and estuarine bird sanctuaries: Including the famous Efutu Grove, traditionally protected by the Effutu people

The wetland is an important habitat for fish, birds, amphibians, and medicinal plants. It also supports over 50 migratory bird species and serves as a spiritual and recreational site for the people of Winneba and surrounding communities.

### 2. Community and Demographic Profile

Seven communities were profiled under this study, each with distinct socio-cultural characteristics:

Sankor, Nsuekyir, Woarabeba, Akosua Village, Mankoadze, Bewadze, Osubonpanyin

Demographic and social trends include:

Mixed ethnicity: Predominantly Fante, with Effutu and Ewe minorities

Rapid urbanization: Especially around Sankor and Winneba peripheries

High youth population, particularly in Woarabeba and Akosua Village

Strong spiritual traditions tied to the Aboakyer Festival and sacred forests

Emerging urban-rural interface: Some communities are increasingly integrated into Winneba’s municipal economy, while others remain traditional and isolated

Household structures range from nuclear family compounds to extended family setups, with increasing female-headed households due to male migration or informal separation.

### 3. Livelihood Systems and Resource Use

Livelihoods in Muni-Pomadze are highly diversified and seasonally dynamic:

a. Fishing and Lagoon Harvesting

Traditional canoe fishing is practiced in Sankor, Woarabeba, and Akosua Village

Target species include tilapia, catfish, shrimp, and small lagoon crabs

Youth engage in net setting and crab harvesting

Fishers report declining catch and seasonal drying of the lagoon mouth

b. Charcoal Production and Firewood Collection

Common in Bewadze, Mankoadze, and Osubonpanyin

Women and men both collect wood from degraded forests and wetland edges

Charcoal is sold locally and transported to Winneba and Agona

c. Farming

Dominant in upland communities like Bewadze and Osubonpanyin

Crops include cassava, maize, okra, pepper, and yam

Rainfed and small-scale irrigation used for vegetable plots

Farmers report increasing soil degradation and rainfall variability

d. Trading and Small Business

Women engage in smoked fish and vegetable trading

Sankor and Nsuekyir have emerging roadside trading centers

Informal transport and motorbike services provide income to youth

e. Spiritual and Cultural Tourism (Potential)

The Efutu Sacred Grove and Aboakyer Festival attract local and international tourists

Ritual fishing, deer hunting, and storytelling form part of annual festivities

Infrastructure is minimal, but communities show interest in eco-cultural tourism

### 4. Environmental Pressures and Degradation Trends

The Muni-Pomadze site is facing multiple degradation pressures:

a. Charcoal and Wood Harvesting

Large sections of the wetland edge and transition forests are cleared annually

Sacred groves remain intact due to taboo enforcement, but buffer zones are under threat

Community awareness of deforestation is high, but alternatives are lacking

b. Lagoon Siltation and Reduced Connectivity

Sediment from upstream farming and sand deposits restrict lagoon–ocean exchange

Communities report loss of fish nurseries and lagoon flooding in the wet season

Lagoon closure also affects water quality and fish taste

c. Plastic Pollution and Dumping

Plastic waste is increasing in Sankor, Nsuekyir, and Woarabeba

Traders and fish processors often burn plastic waste

Drainage channels carry refuse directly into the wetland

d. Unsustainable Farming and Burning

Slash-and-burn agriculture persists in Bewadze and upland farming zones

Encroachment into wetland floodplains increases during dry seasons

Fertilizer and pesticide use contribute to eutrophication of the lagoon

### 5. Governance and Institutional Landscape

Governance structures in Muni-Pomadze are a mix of traditional, municipal, and emerging community-based actors:

a. Traditional Leadership

The Effutu Traditional Council plays a key role in cultural governance and land administration

Chiefs, elders, and Asafo companies organize the Aboakyer Festival and enforce customary rules

Sacred sites are well respected, particularly among older generations

b. Municipal and District Authorities

Effutu Municipal Assembly oversees infrastructure, sanitation, and local development

Wildlife Division (Winneba office) manages Ramsar coordination but lacks enforcement presence in all communities

Forestry Commission and EPA have limited routine engagement

c. Community-Based Organizations and CCAs

Change Agents have been selected across all seven communities

Past NGO projects on sanitation, school feeding, and water have left structures in place

No formal CREMA exists yet, but discussions have begun in Sankor and Bewadze

### 6. Cultural and Spiritual Significance

Muni-Pomadze is unique in its deep cultural connection between the wetland, community identity, and spiritual tradition:

The Efutu people regard the Muni Lagoon and surrounding forest as sacred

The Aboakyer Festival involves ritual hunting of a live deer, symbolic of harmony between people and nature

Taboos include prohibitions on entering sacred groves without permission, fishing on ritual days, and polluting spiritual water points

Songs, drums, and oral storytelling are used to transmit environmental ethics

These practices offer a rich foundation for culturally appropriate conservation and restoration narratives.

### 7. Relevance to the WACA Blue Carbon Initiative

The Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site is highly relevant to Ghana’s blue carbon strategy for the following reasons:

Presence of intact and degraded mangrove zones for restoration and carbon sequestration

Cultural enforcement of sacred groves, offering low-cost conservation opportunities

High charcoal dependence, presenting entry points for improved cookstoves

Proximity to educational institutions (UEW-Winneba) for monitoring, research, and community education

Active youth and women’s groups with prior experience in environmental projects

Spatial containment, allowing easier piloting of MRV systems and community-based governance (CREMA)

Though smaller than Keta or Songor, Muni offers a controlled testing ground for integrated restoration, co-benefit delivery, and spiritual-cultural conservation.

### Conclusion

The Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site demonstrates how ecological importance, cultural heritage, and development challenges intersect in a coastal wetland landscape. It offers high potential for blue carbon action through nature-based solutions that are grounded in community values and traditions. For the WACA Blue Carbon Project, Muni represents a strategic investment in scalable, culturally legitimate, and socially inclusive restoration.

As a compact ecosystem with active community institutions and historical environmental practices, Muni-Pomadze is positioned to become a model for CREMA-based carbon readiness and participatory monitoring.

## 5.1 Validating Socio-Economic Conditions

### Introduction

Understanding the socio-economic conditions of coastal communities is foundational to designing effective blue carbon initiatives. In the context of Ghana’s WACA Blue Carbon Assessment, “validation” refers to the structured process of confirming, expanding, and contextualizing the findings from the various Ramsar sites, existing socio-economic knowledge through participatory, field-based engagements across 37+ communities located within the Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Sites. This section presents the synthesis of the findings to meet the objectives, justification, and outcomes of the landscape of these communities.

Socio-economic conditions are not static: they are shaped by demographic dynamics, seasonal livelihoods, market fluctuations, environmental shocks, and policy interventions. As such, this component of the study aimed to produce a reliable, updated, and community-verified baseline that can inform:

Site-level planning for mangrove restoration and conservation

CREMA formation and governance mechanisms

Carbon co-benefit interventions such as improved cookstoves and plastic management

Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) frameworks required for both carbon and non-carbon results

This validation process ensured that field interventions under the WACA Blue Carbon Project are grounded in the lived experiences and socio-economic realities of the people who depend most on coastal resources.

### Methodological Basis for Validation

The validation of socio-economic conditions was carried out using qualitative and participatory methods, anchored in three core principles:

Community-led perspective: Local knowledge was prioritized through focus group discussions (FGDs), key informant interviews (KIIs), and participatory mapping.

Inclusiveness: Women, youth, fishers, farmers, mangrove users, and vulnerable groups were all consulted separately and jointly to ensure accurate representation.

Cultural alignment: Engagements respected sacred days, observed traditional protocols, and used local languages (Ewe, Fante, Dangme) to ensure clarity and mutual respect.

This validation exercise was built upon the 2024 profiling reports from all three Ramsar sites and field updates conducted in June–August 2025. The result is a unified dataset that reflects both observed realities and community perceptions across economic, social, and environmental dimensions.

### Demographic Structures and Community Organization

Across the three Ramsar sites, population sizes range from small hamlets of under 1,000 people (e.g., Woarabeba) to larger settlements of over 9,000 residents (e.g., Sankor). Demographic characteristics observed include:

High youth populations (15–35 years): over 50% in most communities, especially around Winneba and Ada East.

Female-majority communities: In 24 of the 37 profiled communities, women constitute 55–60% of the population. This trend is pronounced in fish processing hubs like Dzita, Salo, and Akosua Village.

Low formal literacy levels: Particularly in older adults and women, with most communities reporting below 15% adult literacy among women above age 40.

Migrant populations: Ewe migrant fishers in Ada, Fulani herdsmen near Muni, and returning youth in seasonal jobs all create fluid social dynamics.

Large household sizes: Average 7–10 persons per household, often spanning three generations.

Community structures often revolve around the authority of traditional leaders (chiefs, elders, queen mothers) supported by unit committees, assembly members, and more recently, community-based organizations (CBOs). Faith-based networks, women’s groups, and youth associations also play critical organizing roles.

### Livelihood Structures and Seasonal Dynamics

Validating socio-economic conditions required a close look at how people make a living, and how those patterns shift throughout the year. The primary livelihoods observed include:

Artisanal Fishing (coastal and lagoonal): A core activity for over 65% of male adults in Songor and Keta Lagoon Complex sites, with seasonal migration to Togo or Côte d’Ivoire during low-stock periods.

Fish Processing and Trade: Dominated by women, often organized into cooperatives such as Midakpenam Fish Processors Association or Novesi Women’s Group.

Farming and Agro-Processing: Upland and freshwater zone farming dominates in Muni-Pomadze (crops: maize, cassava, okra, pepper). In Songor and Keta where the studies were done, farming is secondary but gaining ground due to declining fish stocks.

Mangrove Harvesting: A key income source for fuelwood and fish smoking, especially around Agbatsivi, Galo, Sota, Klonu, Akosua Village, and Aborlove.

Salt Mining: Especially in Songor (e.g., Pute, Totope), salt extraction provides both direct employment and trade income.

Charcoal Production and Firewood Collection: Active in areas around Bewadze, Mankoadze, and Nolopi, often with strong impacts on forested wetland zones.

Petty Trading and Transport: Supplementary income for many households, especially in larger communities or those with lagoon/river access.

Seasonal livelihood calendars reveal the following trends:

Peak fishing: July to October

Peak farming: March to June and August to November (minor season)

Mangrove harvesting: continuous, but highest from November to February (when fish smoking peaks)

Salt: Dry season (December–March)

Eco-tourism and festivals (e.g. Aboakyir): April–May

Understanding this seasonality is critical for aligning interventions like tree planting, alternative livelihood training, or cookstove rollouts with available labor and household cash flow cycles.

### Income Levels, Poverty, and Market Access

Most communities are characterized by:

Low daily income: Estimated at 5–15 GHS/day for most households during non-peak seasons

Dependence on informal markets: Local trading and barter still dominate in remote areas

Limited access to credit: Women’s groups sometimes run informal VSLA (Village Savings and Loan Associations), but formal banking access is rare

Post-harvest loss: Especially for perishable fish and vegetables, due to poor storage, lack of cold chain infrastructure, and distant markets

Transport barriers: Many communities such as Galotse or Dzogborve have no paved road access, limiting both goods and service flows

These factors influence community decisions around resource use and receptivity to alternative income sources. They also inform how benefits from blue carbon projects (e.g. carbon credit payments, cookstove subsidies) could be structured to ensure equity and uptake.

### Education, Health, and Infrastructure

Community infrastructure affects both livelihoods and resilience. Key observations include:

Basic schools are present in most communities, but dropout rates after JHS are high, particularly for girls.

Health centers are sparse; only larger towns like Anyanui and Winneba have CHPS compounds or clinics. This affects both physical resilience and participation in labor.

Sanitation facilities are inadequate: Open defecation was cited in 60% of the profiled communities. Latrine access is particularly poor around Songor and inner Keta.

Drinking water: Boreholes serve most communities, but some—especially in island/lagoon-edge settlements like Bomigo or Sota—rely on untreated water.

Energy access: Limited grid connectivity but the peri-urban communities have electricity. Most households use wood or charcoal for cooking.

These gaps are not just development challenges; they directly shape the environmental behavior of households. For example, lack of LPG access or toilets increases pressure on forests and lagoons.

### Community Cohesion and Cultural Practices

The validation process uncovered strong social norms that regulate behavior:

Taboo days (e.g., no fishing on Tuesdays or Fridays) are widely observed

Sacred groves and estuary reverence: Several mangrove sites (e.g. Tsakui in Aborlove) are culturally protected

Festival-based conservation: In Muni, the Aboakyir festival contributes to animal protection and sacred area enforcement

Clan-based land tenure remains strong, with chiefs and elders overseeing land and water use decisions

These cultural and spiritual dynamics create both entry points and sensitivities for project design. For instance, ignoring sacred groves in restoration planning may lead to local backlash, while working with festival committees may enhance participation.

### Validation Outcomes and Data Credibility

Each data point was triangulated between:

Focus group discussions (separate for women, men, youth, PWDs)

Interviews with chiefs, unit committee reps, CREMA actors, and Wildlife Division staff

Observation during transect walks (noting mangrove cover, erosion, plastic waste, harvesting patterns)

Reference to existing reports (e.g. 2024 community profiling studies, NBSAP targets)

The result is a highly participatory dataset that reflects both hard facts and community sentiment.

### Conclusion and Implications

Validating socio-economic conditions is not just a procedural step; it anchors the Blue Carbon strategy in lived experience. By accurately capturing who lives in these ecosystems, how they survive, and what challenges they face, the study empowers WACA ResIP 2 to:

Target livelihood alternatives where dependence is highest

Support energy transitions where cookstove needs are most urgent

Form CREMAs where governance gaps exist

Create equity in benefit-sharing by recognizing differentiated vulnerability

This foundation will guide all subsequent decisions, ecological, institutional, and financial, as the Blue Carbon agenda moves from planning to implementation.

## 5.2 Understand Resource Dependency and Degradation Drivers

### Introduction

The viability and sustainability of blue carbon interventions, particularly those involving mangrove restoration, wetland protection, and nature-based solutions, depend not only on ecological data but also on a deep understanding of how communities depend on and interact with these ecosystems. This section unpacks the complex dependencies between communities and their surrounding natural resources, while also identifying key anthropogenic and environmental drivers of degradation.

Across the three Ramsar sites under the WACA Blue Carbon initiative, Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze livelihoods are tightly interwoven with mangrove forests, estuarine fisheries, salt flats, and coastal floodplains. These dependencies are not uniform; they vary by geography, ethnicity, season, gender, and cultural tradition. Moreover, the very ecosystems that communities rely upon are simultaneously being degraded by the same livelihood activities that sustain them.

This paradox dependence and degradation sit at the heart of the baseline study. By understanding it, the WACA project can design more targeted, culturally sensitive, and sustainable blue carbon interventions that both conserve ecosystems and support the people who depend on them.

### 1. Ecosystem Services and Human Reliance

The Ramsar sites studied provide a wide range of ecosystem services that are directly utilized or valued by communities:

#### a. Provisioning Services

Mangrove Wood: Used for fuel (fish smoking, cooking), housing material, fencing, and boat construction. Communities such as Agbledomi, Agbatsivi, Salo, and Akosua Village are especially reliant.

Fisheries and Aquatic Species: Brackish water fisheries (tilapia, shrimp, crabs, mollusks) are vital for nutrition and income across all sites. Lagoon-edge communities like Tunu, Klonu, Sankor, and Woarabeba depend heavily on local water bodies.

Salt: Songor communities extract and trade salt seasonally, often in conflict with conservation zones.

Wildlife and Non-Timber Products: Small-scale bushmeat hunting, shell collection, and occasional apiculture are practiced in Muni-Pomadze and fringes of Keta.

Freshwater and Wetland Agriculture: Floodplains in Muni and parts of Keta support year-round vegetable and cassava farming, albeit with increasing salinity threats.

#### b. Regulating Services

Mangroves serve as natural buffers against erosion, storm surges, and flooding, especially in Dzita, Dzogborve, and Mankoadze.

Estuaries and wetlands filter pollutants, though this function is weakening due to siltation and pollution.

#### c. Cultural Services

Sacred groves, river estuaries, and forest patches (e.g., Tsakui, Nyigblave) are important for local spirituality and tradition, creating non-material dependencies.

Festivals like Aboakyir (Effutu) and the Atradoza (mangrove festival in Keta) reinforce conservation behavior and community identity.

This intricate web of use and value reinforces that the “cost” of environmental loss is more than ecological; it is social, cultural, and economic.

### 2. Patterns of Dependency by Community and Region

While dependency is widespread, the intensity and nature of use varies by site:



These dependencies are often gendered:

Men focus on extraction (fishing, hunting, pole cutting)

Women dominate fish smoking, wood gathering, trade, and processing

Youth often engage in both but are also drawn to salt works and seasonal migration when resources dwindle.

### 3. Drivers of Ecosystem Degradation

The fieldwork and profiling exercises identified numerous drivers of degradation, often linked to the very livelihoods they support:

#### a. Overharvesting of Mangroves

Selective cutting without replanting is rampant in Sota, Bomigo, Akplorwotorkor, and Aborlove.

Cutting for fish smoking is year-round, peaking from November to March.

Some areas (e.g., Galotse) report near-total loss of certain mangrove stands.

#### b. Unsustainable Fishing Practices

Use of small mesh nets, chemicals, and lagoon poisoning reported in the Keta inner estuary and Muni Lagoon.

Disconnection of estuaries (e.g., Ayensu, Ntekufa) from tidal inflows reduces spawning grounds.

#### c. Sand Mining and Topsoil Removal

Especially severe in Aborlove, Dzogborve, and Winneba beaches.

Weak enforcement and demand for construction materials drive this pressure.

#### d. Charcoal Production

Widespread in upland Muni-Pomadze and forest-adjacent Songor communities.

Encroachment into wetland forests increases with fuelwood scarcity.

#### e. Agricultural Expansion into Wetlands

Encroachment into ecologically sensitive floodplains in Bewadze, Klonu, and Dzita.

Farming practices using agrochemicals near water bodies risk eutrophication.

#### f. Plastic Waste and Pollution

Observed plastic accumulation in Songor and Keta lagoons.

Affects fish health and reduces water quality.

#### g. Climate Change and Sea-Level Rise

Rising salinity reported in shallow aquifers around Sankor, Tunu, and Dzita.

Erosion hot spots documented in Dzita, Agorkedzi, and Akplorwotorkor.



### 4. Community Perceptions of Degradation

Importantly, community members were not unaware of these changes. FGDs revealed:

Declining fish catch in all lagoon-edge communities

Increased effort and cost to find suitable mangrove wood

Frequent loss of farmland to salinity and waterlogging

Shifting seasonal calendars (e.g., rains coming later, fish drying earlier)

Rising incidence of human-wildlife conflict (especially with monkeys in forest-edge farms)

Such perceptions confirm that blue carbon interventions are timely, and community demand for restoration is growing, especially where livelihoods are being impacted.

### 5. Interlinkages Between Dependence and Degradation

In many communities, resource dependency and degradation are locked in a feedback loop:

The more livelihoods depend on a resource, the greater the extraction pressure

The more degraded the ecosystem, the lower the productivity, triggering more exploitation

This loop is evident in communities like:

Agbatsivi and Bomigo: heavy mangrove harvesting leads to erosion and poor fish catch

Mankoadze: charcoal use and poor rainfall lead to deforestation and low soil fertility

Sankor and Bewadze: blocked rivers reduce farming yields, driving farmers into the forest

Breaking this loop requires interventions that are both ecological and economic: e.g., providing alternative incomes while restoring mangroves, or bundling conservation with household energy access.

### 6. Role of Culture and Tradition in Managing Dependency

Several community norms already regulate use, but enforcement is inconsistent:

No-harvest days for fisheries and mangroves (Tuesdays in Keta, Fridays in Muni)

Sacred estuaries and spirit forests discourage entry in certain areas (e.g., Tsakui Grove, Agudeve)

Traditional sanctions exist but are declining in legitimacy without youth adherence

Strengthening these cultural practices and combining them with formal conservation rules (via CREMAs, Wildlife Division) could create a hybrid model for sustainable use.

### Conclusion and Strategic Relevance

Understanding community resource dependency and identifying degradation drivers are essential to the design of the WACA Blue Carbon project. They inform:

Where interventions are most needed (e.g., high-pressure zones)

Which communities are most vulnerable to ecological decline

What trade-offs need to be managed (e.g., short-term income vs. long-term restoration)

How to prioritize capacity-building and alternative livelihoods

Where restoration can create mutual benefits: improved ecosystem health and poverty alleviation

This analysis is also critical for the project’s safeguards, carbon accounting, and community benefit-sharing frameworks, ensuring interventions are equitable, locally accepted, and environmentally sound.

## 5.3 Map Community Governance and CREMA Readiness

### Introduction

Effective community-based natural resource management is a cornerstone of Ghana’s approach to environmental governance and central to the WACA Blue Carbon Project. Community Resource Management Areas (CREMAs) have been formally recognized under the Wildlife Resources Management Act (Act 1115, 2023) as decentralized governance mechanisms that empower local stakeholders to sustainably manage forests, wetlands, and biodiversity. This section presents a detailed mapping of local governance structures across the 37+ profiled communities and assesses their institutional readiness to host and operate CREMAs within the scope of blue carbon interventions.

Understanding how communities organize around natural resource governance and how ready they are to take on co-management roles helps identify where CREMAs can be piloted or strengthened. It also helps in designing capacity-building strategies, recognizing institutional bottlenecks, and aligning with national decentralization and forest tenure reform strategies.

### 1. Traditional Governance Structures

All three Ramsar sites, Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze, feature strong traditional authority systems. Chiefs, queen mothers, sub-chiefs, clan heads, and elders often maintain land and resource custodianship, while also presiding over conflict resolution and customary environmental regulations.

In the Keta Lagoon Complex, communities fall under the Anlo and Agave Traditional Councils. Chiefs have established rules for mangrove use, restricted fishing days, and land tenure, with some (e.g., in Atorkor and Agorkedzi) actively engaging in conservation education.

In Songor, governance is coordinated through the Ada Traditional Council, though land disputes and overlapping jurisdictional claims sometimes hinder resource decision-making, especially in salt-producing areas.

In Muni-Pomadze, the Effutu Traditional Council holds spiritual authority over the hunting grounds and Muni Lagoon, reinforcing customary protection zones. In Gomoa West, the Ajumako Traditional Council governs the upland communities.

These authorities remain central to land allocation, cultural practices, and access to wetlands and estuarine resources. Any CREMA or blue carbon intervention must begin by engaging these structures with respect for their spiritual and political significance.

### 2. Local Government and Statutory Governance

Ghana’s decentralized governance framework assigns significant roles to:

Metropolitan, Municipal, and District Assemblies (MMDAs)

Unit Committees and Assembly Members

Decentralized agencies such as the Forestry Commission, EPA, and Fisheries Commission

Across the study sites:

District Assemblies are responsible for integrating wetland management into development plans, issuing permits, and supporting CREMA establishment.

Wildlife Division officers of the Forestry Commission are the designated managers of Ramsar sites. They engage in site demarcation, enforcement, and conservation education, often in partnership with traditional leaders.

EPA provides oversight on environmental compliance and may support CREMA development through EIA processes or safeguards integration.

While these statutory agencies exist in most communities, their operational presence is uneven. In many cases, Wildlife Division officers cover vast geographic areas with limited staff, and district planning officers lack data to integrate community-driven conservation effectively.

### 3. Community-Based Institutions

The validation exercise identified several types of grassroots institutions relevant to CREMA readiness:

#### a. Fishermen and Fish Processors Associations

Active in nearly all lagoon-edge and coastal communities. They help regulate fishing gear, assign fishing zones, and organize processing infrastructure. Some (e.g., in Dzita, Woarabeba) have bylaws and elected leadership.

#### b. Farmer-Based Organizations (FBOs)

Found primarily in upland communities such as Nsuekyir, Bewadze, and Osubonpanyin. They coordinate input access, water use, and post-harvest marketing.

#### c. Women’s Groups and VSLA Networks

Including Novesi, Midakpenam, and Boafo Ye Na Associations. These groups are often more structured than general community groups and handle microcredit, joint savings, and bulk fish processing or charcoal production.

#### d. Environmental Volunteer Groups

Created during past interventions (e.g., Green Ghana, IUCN-PAPBio). Groups in Galotse, Bomigo, and Dzogborve have maintained to some extent, mangrove patrols, awareness campaigns, or enrichment planting.

#### e. Interim CREMA Executives (CRMCs/CECs)

Identified in several communities:

Keta Lagoon has two emerging CREMA clusters—Angor and Dusornu—with mapped boundaries, constitutions, and leadership structures.

Muni-Pomadze has one CREMA in early formation, covering Sankor, Nsuekyir, and Mankoadze, with strong Wildlife Division engagement.

Songor has potential CREMA candidates but weak cohesion due to land disputes and unregulated salt expansion.

These institutions are uneven in their development, but many provide a starting point for formal CREMA establishment or strengthening.

### 4. Community Change Agents (CCAs)

One of the most promising innovations under WACA ResIP 2 has been the selection and training of Community Change Agents (CCAs) across all profiled communities. These individuals were nominated based on their:

Experience in natural resource use or conservation

Demonstrated leadership and commitment

Representation of youth, women, or marginalized groups

Willingness to mobilize community action

Over 100 CCAs have been selected across the 3 Ramsar sites. Some already play liaison roles between communities and district officials. Others participate in awareness events, mangrove planting, or reporting of illegal activities.

They represent a strategic human infrastructure to serve as “CREMA champions” and can support data collection, safeguards, enforcement, and community mobilization for blue carbon.

### 5. CREMA Readiness Criteria and Assessment

Based on Wildlife Division guidance and stakeholder feedback, the following criteria were used to assess readiness:



Using these, the study classified readiness into:

High Readiness: e.g., Dzita, Sankor, Tunu, Agbatsivi, Galotse

Moderate Readiness: e.g., Sota, Agortoe, Mankoadze, Bomigo

Low Readiness: e.g., Pute, Elavanyo, Osubonpanyin 

This classification will help the Wildlife Division and MLNR prioritize CREMA rollout and tailor capacity-building accordingly.

### 6. Barriers to CREMA Formation

Despite general enthusiasm, several constraints emerged:

Land tenure ambiguities in Songor and Keta (especially with overlapping family/clan claims)

Resource-use conflicts, particularly between salt mining groups and mangrove-dependent users

Low literacy and documentation capacity for forming constitutions or recording boundaries

Limited financing and logistical support from districts to sustain committee operations

Fatigue from donor-driven short-term projects, which undermines trust in long-term processes

Addressing these barriers will require a mix of training, facilitation, conflict mediation, and policy support, possibly using WACA as a “convening framework” to bring stakeholders together.

### 7. Integration with Wildlife Act (Act 1115, 2023)

The recent Wildlife Resources Management Act (Act 1115) provides a robust legal foundation for CREMA development. Key provisions include:

Community rights over management and benefit-sharing of resources in designated areas

Recognition of CREMAs as governance bodies with legal standing

Clear roles for district assemblies and Wildlife Division in supporting co-management

Aligning CREMA formation with Act 1115 ensures legal enforceability, legitimacy in carbon credit transactions, and safeguards compliance under the World Bank and voluntary carbon standards.

### Conclusion

Mapping community governance and assessing CREMA readiness reveals a mixed landscape of opportunity and constraint. While strong cultural leadership and emerging grassroots institutions exist across all three Ramsar sites, significant capacity, legal, and coordination work remains.

The WACA Blue Carbon initiative provides a unique opportunity to not only restore ecosystems but to empower communities through governance reforms. By leveraging CCAs, strengthening CREMA frameworks, and anchoring action in national law, Ghana can model a participatory, equitable approach to climate and biodiversity governance.

## 5.4 Assess Gender Roles, Equity, and Inclusion

### Introduction

Gender roles, equity, and inclusion are integral dimensions of environmental governance and climate resilience. In the context of Ghana’s WACA Blue Carbon Assessment, understanding how women, men, youth, and marginalized groups engage with natural resources, decision-making processes, and livelihood systems is critical for designing equitable, impactful interventions. This section explores how gender and social identities shape access to and control over ecosystem services, examines participation in governance, and highlights opportunities and gaps in achieving inclusive blue carbon outcomes.

The Ramsar sites covered, Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze demonstrate both the entrenched inequities and the emerging pathways for empowerment that characterize coastal communities across Ghana. From the fish-smoking matriarchs of Dzita to the Fulani herdsmen in Ada East and Muni, the socio-economic terrain is layered with power, responsibility, exclusion, and resilience.

### 1. Gendered Roles in Livelihoods and Resource Use

Across all three Ramsar landscapes, livelihood activities are deeply gendered:

#### a. Fisheries and Aquatic Resource Value Chain

Men dominate the harvesting stage, fishing in the sea, lagoons, and rivers; crab and shrimp trapping; and transporting catch.

Women are central to processing, preservation, and marketing. In many communities (e.g., Dzita, Akosua Village, Woarabeba), women’s groups operate smoking centers and lead fish trading networks.

Youth of both genders are engaged in boat maintenance, net repairs, and informal trading, but often lack stable income or land ownership.

#### b. Mangrove Harvesting and Firewood Use

Men typically cut mangrove poles (for fuel or construction), especially in communities like Galotse, Sota, and Akplorwotorkor.

Women collect and bundle mangrove branches, dry them, and use them in fish-smoking kilns.

Both genders recognize mangrove depletion but have limited access to alternatives like LPG or improved stoves.

#### c. Farming and Agro-processing

Women farmers in Muni-Pomadze and upland Keta communities are primarily responsible for vegetable cultivation (okra, tomatoes, peppers) and market sales.

Men often focus on land-clearing and higher-capital crops like maize or cassava.

Decision-making around land use is usually male-dominated, although female-headed households are common.

#### d. Salt and Charcoal Production

In Songor, women often work in salt pans, especially during dry seasons, while men transport and sell bulk quantities.

Charcoal production in Mankoadze and Bewadze is mostly done by men, with women handling local sales.

These roles underscore that both men and women are central to the blue carbon landscape, but in different ways. Without understanding these distinctions, restoration efforts may inadvertently marginalize key stakeholders.

### 2. Gender Norms, Taboos, and Cultural Beliefs

Several socio-cultural norms influence how gender interacts with the environment and governance:

Menstruation taboos: In parts of Anloga and Ada East, menstruating women are prohibited from fishing or entering sacred water bodies. This limits their participation in certain resource use and rituals.

Sacred groves: Some conservation areas (e.g., Tsakui Grove) are inaccessible to women altogether, or only after certain rites.

Inheritance and land access: Most communities follow patrilineal systems, restricting women’s ability to own land or trees, even if they manage the land.

Leadership norms: Chiefs and elders are usually male, and women’s voices may be excluded from critical resource decisions.

However, these norms are not static. In several areas (e.g., Dzogborve, Sankor, Aborlove), women have emerged as respected environmental advocates, especially where external programs (e.g., IUCN PAPBio,) have built leadership capacity.

### 3. Women’s Groups and Empowerment Pathways

The study documented over 25 active women’s groups across the three sites. These groups engage in:

Fish smoking and preservation (e.g., Midakpenam and Boafo Ye Na Associations)

Agro-processing (e.g., cassava grating and packaging)

Environmental education (e.g., during Green Ghana Day events)

These groups often function as entry points for environmental action, particularly when linked with district-level planning or NGO facilitation. For example:

In Galotse, the women’s group helped establish a mangrove patrol team and organized taboo-day sensitizations.

In Sankor, women jointly purchased fish-processing equipment after participating in a savings group supported by NGOs.

However, challenges remain:

Most groups lack formal registration, limiting access to funding

Leadership is often informal and unsupported by district structures

Some younger women feel excluded from older-dominated associations

### 4. Youth and Inclusion of Marginalized Groups

The study also explored the inclusion of other vulnerable groups:

#### a. Youth

Many youth (15–35) are disengaged from formal governance structures

Seasonal migration, unemployment, and disenchantment with local leadership are common

However, youth-led initiatives exist—e.g., mangrove nursery planting in Dzita, crab farming trials in Agbledomi

#### b. Persons with Disabilities (PWDs)

PWDs were present in nearly all communities but reported exclusion from resource committees, training, or livelihoods programs in the past.

In Dzogborve and Klonu, a few PWDs are members of VSLA groups, showing that inclusion is possible with tailored outreach

#### c. Migrant Populations

Ewe fishers in Ada and Fulani herders in Muni-Pomadze face social exclusion, limited land access, and marginal political voice

Conflict over land and water use occasionally arises, particularly in areas where population growth pressures land resources

Understanding these dynamics is essential for equitable planning. One-size-fits-all models of “community participation” risk reinforcing exclusion if not consciously inclusive.

### 5. Participation in Governance and Decision-Making

When asked about participation in resource governance:

Women in most communities reported they “contribute informally” (e.g., through suggestions to male leaders or organizing community clean-ups) but were not formally part of decision-making bodies.

Youth cited a lack of respect from elders as a barrier to inclusion.

Unit committees and CRMCs (where present) were almost always male-led, though Wildlife Division officers in Muni and Keta are beginning to require gender quotas in new CREMA structures.

In the few communities with women in leadership roles (e.g., Dzita, Salo), these women were often part of other leadership structures (e.g., church, school) and used their influence to gain entry into conservation dialogue.

### 6. Gender-Specific Vulnerabilities and Impacts

Gender also affects how people experience environmental degradation and economic stress:

Women bear the brunt of fuelwood scarcity, walking longer distances to collect mangrove or upland firewood.

Girls may be pulled from school to assist in fish processing or charcoal bundling.

Men are more likely to migrate when fish stocks decline, leaving women as de facto heads of household without formal support.

Youth in degraded areas face early school dropout, risky migration, or petty crime.

## 5.5 Provide Baseline for Carbon and Co-Benefit Interventions

### Introduction

Blue carbon interventions such as mangrove restoration, sustainable wetland use, and coastal ecosystem management are increasingly viewed as pathways to climate mitigation and community resilience. However, their effectiveness and viability depend on having robust, site-specific baseline data. These baselines not only serve as a reference point for measuring carbon sequestration but also enable monitoring of non-carbon co-benefits such as reduced household air pollution, strengthened livelihoods, and plastic waste reduction.

This section outlines how the WACA Blue Carbon socio-economic baseline study generates critical data for use in Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV), safeguards compliance, carbon market participation, and inclusive benefit-sharing. It focuses on three core areas:

Readiness for blue carbon MRV and carbon trading frameworks

Improved cookstove interventions as a co-benefit stream

Plastic waste management and circular economy opportunities

By providing a multi-dimensional baseline, this study helps bridge the gap between community realities and global carbon accounting frameworks.

### 1. Blue Carbon MRV and Readiness for Carbon Trading

To participate in voluntary or compliance-based carbon markets (e.g., Plan Vivo, Verra), a project must establish a baseline against which carbon gains, such as from mangrove growth or reduced emissions, can be calculated.

Key community-level data collected in this study to support this include:

#### a. Mangrove Use and Degradation Baseline

Quantified reliance on mangroves for fuelwood, fishing gear, fencing, and construction across all communities

Seasonal trends in mangrove harvesting

Identification of high-pressure zones (e.g., Dzogborve, Bomigo, Akosua Village)

Documented lack of replanting practices and near-complete depletion in some areas

This enables project developers and the Wildlife Division to:

Define Reference Emission Levels (RELs) or Reference Levels (RLs)

Prioritize zones for additionality in restoration

Establish performance benchmarks for carbon credit calculations

#### b. Land Tenure and Resource Access

Tenure arrangements (e.g., family-owned, stool land, common land) were documented for each mangrove stand

Rights of access to wetland resources (open access vs. community-regulated) were verified

Community consent structures and governance platforms (e.g., CRMCs, CCAs) mapped

This information is essential for demonstrating carbon rights clarity required under Verra’s VCS and Plan Vivo standards to ensure benefit-sharing and prevent disputes.

#### c. Carbon Literacy and Local Awareness

Less than 10% of respondents had heard of “carbon trading” or “carbon credits” prior to engagements

However, over 70% expressed interest in planting trees or protecting mangroves in return for “community support,” “stoves,” or “school scholarships.”

These findings confirm the need for community-tailored carbon education but also indicate a positive entry point for developing equitable benefit-sharing models.

### 2. Improved Cookstoves as an Emissions Reduction Co-Benefit

Fuelwood dependence, particularly for fish smoking and domestic cooking, was nearly universal across the 37+ communities. This creates both a degradation driver and a climate mitigation opportunity.

The socio-economic baseline captured key data for designing and modeling improved cookstove interventions:

#### a. Current Fuel Use Patterns

82% of households in lagoon and coastal areas use firewood as primary fuel

Fish-smoking businesses consume high volumes daily (15–50 bundles), mostly sourced from mangroves

Upland communities use charcoal for household cooking

Only 13% of respondents reported using LPG, mainly in peri-urban settings like Sankor or Tunu

#### b. Health and Labor Implications

Women reported respiratory issues (e.g., eye irritation, coughing) due to exposure to smoke

Girls and women walk 2–5 km daily in search of firewood in depleted areas

Time spent gathering fuel reduced time for farming, trading, or education

#### c. Willingness to Adopt Improved Stoves

Women expressed interest in cookstoves that save fuel, reduce smoke, or improve processing efficiency

Communities showed high openness to “try-before-you-buy” models

Key barriers identified include upfront cost, trust in durability, and availability of parts

These insights provide the foundation for calculating emission reductions from improved cookstove distribution and maintenance. It also sets the stage for integrating cookstove carbon credits under existing methodologies (e.g., Gold Standard, Clean Cooking Alliance protocols).

### 3. Plastic Waste and Circular Economy Opportunities

Coastal communities face growing challenges with plastic waste accumulation, particularly in and around lagoons. While not a traditional carbon source, plastics represent a major co-benefit intervention opportunity, linking environmental health, youth employment, and potential participation in plastic credit systems.

Findings from fieldwork include:

#### a. Waste Generation and Disposal Patterns

Most communities lack adequate waste collection systems

Plastic sachets, food packaging, fishing line remnants, and used nets were the most common litter types

Informal dumping in mangroves, beaches, and river mouths was widespread

#### b. Awareness and Willingness to Act

Over 60% of youth expressed interest in being paid to collect plastic waste

Women’s groups suggested incorporating recycling into VSLA or trading networks

A few communities had prior experience with clean-up campaigns (e.g., Galotse, Elavanyo) but lacked tools and sustained support

#### c. Linkages to Carbon and Blue Economy Goals

Plastic offsets are emerging under private-led systems (e.g., Verra’s Plastic Waste Reduction Standard)

Projects that remove or recycle plastic in coastal zones can generate tradeable “plastic credits”

Ghana’s National Plastics Management Policy (2020) provides institutional backing for pilot models

These findings highlight the potential for pilot-scale plastic recovery initiatives, especially when bundled with youth employment, awareness, and data tracking systems.

These indicators can be built into a WACA Blue Carbon MRV framework aligned with Plan Vivo’s Social Benefit indicators, Verra CCB (Climate, Community & Biodiversity) Standards, or the UN SDG Impact Standards.

### 5. Role of Community Change Agents in Monitoring

Community Change Agents (CCAs) are well-positioned to support participatory baseline monitoring. Their potential roles include:

Surveying fuelwood use patterns per household

Tracking plastic collection volumes

Monitoring mangrove replanting success

Logging stove distribution and usage rates

Providing verification support for carbon project audits

Training and equipping these agents is critical to reduce MRV costs and ensure legitimacy in data collection. It also builds local ownership of outcomes.

### 6. Safeguards, FPIC, and Benefit-Sharing Readiness

Baseline data on community expectations, fears, and desired benefits are crucial for meeting social safeguards under the World Bank’s Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) and voluntary market standards.

Findings include:

Widespread expectation of "community-wide" benefits (e.g., schools, boreholes) from any carbon income

Fear of land grabbing or external takeover of lagoon areas

High demand for transparent revenue-sharing mechanisms and community-owned funds

These inputs guide the development of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) protocols, grievance redress mechanisms, and safeguards implementation plans.

## Conclusion

The socio-economic baseline study has established a comprehensive dataset on mangrove use, fuel consumption, plastic waste, and community awareness. This provides:

The foundation for credible, locally verified MRV systems

A roadmap for cookstove and plastic-related co-benefit strategies

The evidence base for inclusive benefit-sharing and safeguard compliance

The justification for Ghana’s participation in high-integrity blue carbon markets

Going forward, this baseline will serve not only as a diagnostic tool but as a monitoring platform to track impact, adapt strategies, and demonstrate climate-finance readiness.

# 7.0 Conclusion and Next Steps

## 7.1 Conclusion

The socio-economic baseline study conducted across the Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze Ramsar sites represents a foundational milestone in Ghana’s Blue Carbon agenda under WACA ResIP 2. By combining participatory methods, site-level governance assessments, livelihood diagnostics, gender and inclusion mapping, and risk typologies, the study has laid bare the intricate socio-ecological systems that must be navigated to achieve climate, ecological, and social resilience outcomes.

Across the 37+ communities, the findings reveal strong dependence on mangrove and wetland ecosystems for livelihoods, but also deep vulnerabilities and risks tied to degradation, exclusion, and institutional fragmentation. Communities have shown high levels of interest in CREMA governance, co-benefit innovations like cookstoves and plastic recovery, and in leading the restoration process, provided safeguards, transparency, and inclusive benefit-sharing are assured.

The study also surfaced substantial asymmetries in access to resources, decision-making, and livelihood diversification. Women, youth, PWDs, and migrant groups remain underrepresented, but their existing social groups and informal networks present potent entry points for change.

As Ghana accelerates toward implementation of its blue carbon strategy, this baseline offers a platform for integrated action, where governance reform, ecological restoration, and carbon finance can reinforce each other rather than compete. But the road ahead requires clear operational alignment between national ministries, district authorities, community institutions, and development partners.

This report has also helped establish a credible, field-validated dataset that will serve not only as a planning tool, but as a measurement framework for future co-benefits and climate finance transactions.
