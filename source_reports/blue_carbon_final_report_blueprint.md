# Blue Carbon Final Report Blueprint

**Project:** Assessment of Blue Carbon Ecosystem Potentials of Ghana (Mangrove and Tidal Salt Marsh)  
**Purpose:** Working blueprint for drafting the Final Report as an integrated synthesis document  
**Prepared for:** Ecological Nest / MESTI / MLNR  
**Date:** March 2026

---

## 1. Why this blueprint exists

The Final Report should not read like six separate reports bound together. It should synthesize the evidence generated across the consultancy into one decision-oriented document that:

1. establishes the national and site-level baseline for mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems;
2. identifies the main ecological, socio-economic, and carbon-related findings;
3. prioritizes areas for protection, restoration, and monitoring; and
4. shows how the EOSDA-hosted monitoring setup and MRV system will support ongoing management after the consultancy.

This approach is consistent with the contract and inception documents, which require a final report that consolidates the findings, provides baseline data for policy and management action, and supports monitoring and future restoration action.

---

## 2. Working report logic

### Core storyline

A strong report narrative is:

> The consultancy established the baseline condition of Ghana's priority mangrove and tidal salt marsh systems through geospatial mapping, socio-economic assessment, ecosystem health analysis, and carbon inventory; identified priority areas and pressures; and laid the foundation for continuous monitoring through drone products, EOSDA hosting, and an MRV system.

### The key questions the Final Report must answer

- What blue carbon ecosystems were assessed, where are they, and what is their current extent and condition?
- What human uses, livelihood dependencies, and governance factors shape these ecosystems?
- What is the current ecosystem health status and what are the main pressures or degradation drivers?
- What are the baseline carbon stocks and why do they matter for climate and restoration planning?
- Which areas should be prioritized for protection, restoration, and monitoring?
- How will monitoring continue through drone outputs, EOSDA hosting, and the MRV system?

---

## 3. Drafting principles

### Principle 1: Synthesis over compilation
The Final Report should summarize each technical workstream, but its real value is in integrating the findings and drawing management implications.

### Principle 2: Start writing before all inputs are final
Draft the fixed chapters now and hold placeholders for the final carbon inventory results, drone survey outputs, EOSDA screenshots/maps, and the finalized MRV architecture.

### Principle 3: Use sites as the integration unit
A site-based synthesis matrix will make the writing easier and more useful than a long sequence of disconnected summaries.

### Principle 4: Keep detailed methods in annexes or source reports
The Final Report should contain enough method summary for credibility, but not reproduce every technical detail already covered elsewhere.

## 4. Proposed chapter structure

### Front matter
- Cover page
- Table of contents
- List of tables
- List of figures
- Acronyms
- Executive Summary

### Chapter 1. Introduction
Include:
- background to the WACA blue carbon assignment;
- policy and management relevance;
- purpose of the Final Report;
- scope of the consultancy;
- linkage to contract outputs and inception commitments.

### Chapter 2. Objectives, Scope, and Approach
Include:
- overall objective and specific objectives;
- ecosystems covered (mangroves and tidal salt marshes);
- geographic coverage and priority landscapes;
- summary of the overall assessment approach;
- summary of limitations and assumptions.

### Chapter 3. Methodological Overview
A short integrative chapter covering:
- literature review and secondary data;
- mangrove extent / geospatial mapping methods;
- socio-economic methods;
- ecosystem health methods;
- carbon inventory methods;
- drone survey methods;
- EOSDA hosting approach;
- MRV design approach.

Detailed protocols should be placed in annexes or referenced from source reports.

### Chapter 4. Study Areas and Context
Include:
- national coastal context;
- priority sites / landscapes covered;
- ecological setting;
- community and livelihood context;
- institutional and governance setting.

### Chapter 5. Integrated Baseline Findings
This is the core synthesis chapter and should be organized as follows.

#### 5.1 Mangrove geospatial extent and distribution
Use the Mangrove Geospatial Extent Report and drone products to present:
- extent and spatial distribution;
- ecosystem typologies;
- fragmented vs intact areas;
- degradation hotspots;
- restoration opportunity zones.

#### 5.2 Socio-economic conditions, uses, and pressures
Use the socio-economic report to present:
- livelihood dependence and resource use;
- social and economic importance of mangroves and salt marshes;
- local knowledge and management practices;
- governance and tenure issues;
- principal drivers of degradation.

#### 5.3 Ecosystem health baseline
Use the ecosystem health report to present:
- health indicators by site;
- ecosystem condition and stressors;
- water/vegetation/biodiversity observations;
- spatial variation in ecosystem quality.

#### 5.4 Carbon baseline inventory
Use the carbon inventory report to present:
- carbon pools assessed;
- baseline carbon stock estimates by site or stratum;
- differences across sites/ecosystem types;
- implications for blue carbon project potential.

#### 5.5 Cross-cutting synthesis and priority analysis
This subsection should integrate the preceding findings and answer:
- where the most intact and high-value ecosystems are;
- where ecological degradation overlaps with high community pressure;
- where carbon significance and restoration potential coincide;
- which areas should be prioritized for protection, restoration, and monitoring.

This should end with a clear prioritization matrix.

### Chapter 6. Drone Survey Outputs and Spatial Monitoring Readiness
This chapter should show how the project moves from baseline assessment to operational monitoring.

Include:
- purpose and scope of the drone survey;
- survey timing and coverage;
- key outputs (orthomosaics, plot imagery, boundary refinement, condition mapping);
- how drone outputs improve baseline precision;
- how plots and areas are prepared for hosting and ongoing monitoring.

### Chapter 7. EOSDA Hosting and Continuous Monitoring Setup
Include:
- why EOSDA is being used;
- which survey areas / polygons / plots are being hosted;
- how EOSDA supports recurring observation;
- expected monitoring workflow;
- data access, update frequency, and practical use for managers.

### Chapter 8. MRV System
Include:
- objective of the MRV system;
- users and institutional roles;
- indicators to be monitored;
- data flows from field, drone, satellite, and reporting layers;
- reporting cycle and governance arrangements;
- current status of the web app / skeletal system;
- recommendations for full operationalization.

### Chapter 9. Implications for Protection, Restoration, and Management
This is the policy and management bridge chapter.

Include:
- priority areas for immediate protection;
- priority areas for restoration assessment;
- safeguards and community engagement considerations;
- institutional actions needed to sustain monitoring;
- how the findings should inform the restoration consultancy ToR and follow-on work.

### Chapter 10. Recommendations and Next Steps
Organize recommendations under:
- protection and conservation;
- restoration planning;
- monitoring and MRV operationalization;
- community engagement and governance;
- data management and institutional handover.

Recommendations can also be grouped by timeframe:
- immediate (0-6 months);
- medium term (6-18 months);
- longer term (18+ months).

### Chapter 11. Conclusion
A concise closing chapter that restates the significance of the baseline and the value of the monitoring platform and MRV system as the basis for future blue carbon action in Ghana.

### Annexes
Potential annexes:
- detailed methodology notes;
- site profiles;
- detailed maps;
- carbon inventory tables;
- socio-economic summary tables;
- ecosystem health indicators;
- drone survey technical notes;
- EOSDA workflow/screenshots;
- MRV indicators and data dictionary;
- stakeholder engagement summary.

## 10. Immediate drafting sequence

### Step 1: Build the shell
Create the full table of contents and section headings in the master draft.

### Step 2: Populate the fixed chapters
Draft Chapters 1 to 4 and the non-numeric portions of Chapters 5, 9, 10, and 11.

### Step 3: Build the synthesis matrix
Extract comparable findings from the geospatial, socio-economic, ecosystem health, and carbon workstreams.

### Step 4: Insert visuals and tables early
Even draft placeholders for maps and figures will help the narrative structure.

### Step 5: Insert pending technical outputs when ready
Add carbon inventory results, drone outputs, EOSDA details, and finalized MRV content.

### Step 6: Write the Executive Summary last
The summary should reflect the fully integrated evidence and recommendations.

---

## 11. Team writing arrangement

A practical team structure is:

- **Lead writer/editor:** owns the master draft, style, and synthesis;
- **Geospatial lead:** extent maps, drone outputs, EOSDA-related narrative;
- **Socio-economic lead:** livelihoods, pressures, governance, safeguards;
- **Ecological lead:** ecosystem health interpretation;
- **Carbon lead:** carbon inventory summary and climate interpretation;
- **MRV lead:** monitoring architecture, indicators, and operational workflow;
- **Data/graphics lead:** maps, figures, tables, numbering, and formatting consistency.

---

## 12. Key drafting risks to avoid

- turning the report into a stitched bundle of summaries;
- inconsistent numbers, site names, or categories across sections;
- recommendations not clearly linked to evidence;
- treating EOSDA and MRV as side notes instead of implementation tools;
- waiting for every final input before starting the draft.

---

## 13. Suggested opening paragraph for the Final Report

> This Final Report presents the integrated findings of the consultancy on the Assessment of Blue Carbon Ecosystem Potentials of Ghana, focusing on mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems. It synthesizes the evidence generated through geospatial mapping, socio-economic assessment, ecosystem health analysis, carbon inventory, drone-based spatial products, and the development of a monitoring framework. The report establishes the baseline for policy, restoration planning, and long-term monitoring, and identifies practical pathways for protection, restoration, and sustained blue carbon management in Ghana.
