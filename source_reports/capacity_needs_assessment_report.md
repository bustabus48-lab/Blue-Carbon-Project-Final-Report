Institutional Foundations for Community-Led Blue Carbon in Ghana: A Diagnostic of Capacity Needs, Gaps, and Strategic Interventions



## Executive Summary

This report provides an in-depth analysis of the institutional, technical, and infrastructural prerequisites for the success of the West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project (WACA) Blue Carbon initiative in Ghana. It serves as a foundational evidence base, diagnosing critical capacity gaps and proposing a strategic framework for intervention across the 37 profiled communities in the Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze Ramsar sites. The analysis synthesizes extensive field data from the project’s Socio-Economic Report with established best practices in community-based natural resource management and international development safeguards.

The key findings indicate that the viability and equity of Ghana's blue carbon model are critically dependent on addressing foundational gaps that currently pose significant risks to project sustainability. Foremost among these are weaknesses in community governance, particularly the nascent and inconsistent operational readiness of Community Resource Management Areas (CREMAs), the designated local management institutions. This is compounded by low levels of financial literacy, which are misaligned with the complex requirements of carbon finance and benefit-sharing, and a pervasive lack of basic "enabling infrastructure", such as access roads, sanitation facilities, and community meeting centers, which directly hinders participation, inclusion, and operational effectiveness.

In response to this diagnosis, the report puts forward a strategic framework for sequenced, multi-stakeholder intervention. It argues that building core governance capacity and establishing trust must precede the rollout of complex carbon finance mechanisms to mitigate risks of elite capture and community disillusionment. Priority interventions are identified to guide immediate action. These include: (1) fast-tracking the finalization of CREMA legal structures and providing intensive, role-specific training for their leadership; (2) delivering targeted financial literacy and technical skills training, particularly for women’s groups and youth who are poised to lead in enterprise development and participatory monitoring; and (3) financing small-scale, community-prioritized infrastructure that removes direct barriers to participation and safeguards compliance.

Ultimately, this report presents a roadmap for building the human and institutional architecture required for a just and durable blue carbon future in Ghana. It concludes that capacity is not a secondary activity but the central pillar upon which ecological restoration, climate mitigation, and community empowerment must be built.

## Section 1: A Diagnosis of Capacity Needs for Community-Led Blue Carbon Governance

This section analyzes the human and organizational capacity gaps that must be filled to create a functional, equitable, and resilient governance environment for the WACA Blue Carbon initiative. It moves beyond a simple inventory of needs to diagnose the root causes and potential consequences of these deficits, drawing direct evidence from community consultations across the three Ramsar sites. The analysis underscores that without targeted investment in governance, financial acumen, and technical skills, the project's ecological and carbon objectives rest on a fragile institutional foundation.

### 1.1 Strengthening CREMA Governance and Leadership: From Concept to Operation

The Community Resource Management Area (CREMA) is the designated institutional vehicle for community-led natural resource management in Ghana and is central to the WACA Blue Carbon strategy.1 However, field assessments reveal that its operational readiness is highly variable and, in most cases, nascent.3 This gap between policy intent and on-the-ground reality presents a primary institutional risk. Many elected leaders of the Community Resource Management Committees (CRMCs) and Community Executive Committees (CECs) lack a fundamental understanding of their roles, a challenge captured by a community leader in Dzogborve: “We were elected, but no one told us what to do”.3 This sentiment reflects a broader governance vacuum that requires immediate and structured intervention.

The specific gaps identified are foundational. First, there is a widespread lack of mandate clarity. CRMC and CEC members across the sites expressed confusion regarding their specific roles, responsibilities, and legal authority under the recently passed Wildlife Resources Management Act (Act 1115, 2023).3 This ambiguity paralyzes decision-making and undermines their legitimacy. Second, the process of

constitutional development is stalled. The majority of emerging CREMAs have incomplete or unvalidated constitutions and bylaws, leaving them without a formal, agreed-upon basis for resource management rules, benefit-sharing mechanisms, or conflict resolution procedures.3 This legal and procedural void makes consistent and transparent governance nearly impossible. Third, a deficit in

inclusive facilitation skills means that even when meetings occur, leaders often struggle to manage them in a way that ensures meaningful participation from women, youth, and persons with disabilities (PWDs). This not only silences crucial perspectives but also risks non-compliance with the stakeholder engagement standards required by funding partners like the World Bank.3

Compounding these issues is the complex interface with traditional authorities. Chiefs, queen mothers, and elders remain central to resource access and social cohesion, and their role in land allocation and conflict resolution is undisputed.3 However, many are unaware of the formal safeguard requirements of international projects, such as Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC), or specific protocols for gender inclusion and grievance redress. Consequently, they often act as traditional gatekeepers during consultations rather than as facilitators of a broader, more inclusive dialogue, which can inadvertently perpetuate existing inequities.3

The analysis reveals a critical vulnerability arising from this nascent state of CREMA governance. While the CREMA model is designed as an inclusive, democratic institution for resource management 1, the widespread lack of training and mandate clarity for newly elected leaders 3 creates a functional governance vacuum. In such environments, decision-making power invariably reverts to established, traditional authority structures. While these structures are culturally significant, they may not align with the project's explicit goals of equitable benefit-sharing. This dynamic directly enables the risk of "elite capture," a phenomenon where the benefits of a community project are disproportionately appropriated by powerful local individuals, a threat already identified as a significant risk in the Songor and Keta landscapes.3 This pattern, where weak formal community-based organizations (CBOs) are dominated by traditional elites, is a well-documented failure mode in community-based natural resource management (CBNRM) globally.6 Therefore, the failure to provide immediate, intensive governance training to CRMCs is not merely an administrative delay; it actively cultivates the conditions for the project's core equity objectives to be undermined from the outset.

### 1.2 Building Financial Acumen for Enterprises and Benefit-Sharing

The blue carbon model is predicated on the assumption that communities can not only manage natural resources but also manage financial flows, whether from alternative livelihood enterprises or the eventual sale of carbon credits. The socio-economic baseline, however, reveals a profound gap between this expectation and the on-the-ground reality. Communities across the three sites are characterized by low daily incomes, a heavy reliance on informal markets, and extremely limited access to formal financial services.3 This context of low financial literacy presents a formidable challenge to the equitable and transparent management of project-related benefits.3

Specific gaps in financial capacity are evident at multiple levels. At the grassroots enterprise level, women’s groups managing nascent businesses in salt processing, beekeeping, and tree nurseries lack fundamental skills in basic bookkeeping and record-keeping.3 This prevents them from tracking profitability, securing loans, or demonstrating financial viability to potential partners. Similarly, Village Savings and Loan Associations (VSLAs), while successful at mobilizing local savings, require a significant step-up in capacity for

cooperative management to transition into formal group businesses. This includes training in budget planning, product costing, marketing, and equitable profit-sharing models.3 At the institutional level of the CREMA itself, the gaps are even more critical. Most CRMCs lack a treasurer with any formal training in

fund management, internal controls, or financial reporting.3 This poses a severe risk for managing potential carbon revenues and is a direct threat to compliance with international carbon standards, which demand strict traceability and transparency for all benefit flows to meet social safeguard requirements.7

This lack of financial readiness creates a profound disconnect between the complex, long-term, and often unpredictable nature of carbon finance and the immediate, subsistence-level economic realities of the communities. The baseline study found that less than 10% of community members had ever heard of "carbon trading," indicating very low "carbon literacy".3 In stark contrast, there is a high expectation for immediate and tangible community-wide benefits, such as schools and boreholes, based on experiences with past development projects.3 The process of generating and selling carbon credits, however, is a multi-year endeavor that requires rigorous monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) before any revenue is realized.7 This temporal and conceptual misalignment means that the project's central financial incentive is likely to be perceived as a broken promise if revenues are delayed, smaller than anticipated, or abstract in form. Such a perception would inevitably lead to a loss of trust, disillusionment, and the withdrawal of community participation in the very conservation activities upon which the entire model depends. Mitigating this risk requires intensive and realistic financial literacy training from the project's inception, designed not only to build skills but to proactively manage expectations.

### 1.3 Developing Competencies in Conflict Management and Dispute Resolution

The WACA Blue Carbon initiative operates within complex social landscapes characterized by both pre-existing and potential conflicts over natural resources. The socio-economic assessment identified multiple conflict drivers, including disputes over land boundaries between clans, particularly in the contested salt-mining areas of Songor; disagreements over the zoning of areas for restoration versus traditional use; and intra-community friction regarding representation on governance committees.3 

The capacity to manage these conflicts effectively is currently weak across all stakeholder levels. CRMCs and traditional councils, the first ports of call for local disputes, generally lack formal skills in mediation and negotiation. Their methods are often based on custom, which may not be sufficient to address new types of conflict introduced by the project, such as those related to carbon rights or benefit-sharing formulas.3 Furthermore, project implementers themselves, including Wildlife Division officers and partner NGOs, require training in conflict-sensitive planning. Without this, well-intentioned activities like demarcating restoration zones could inadvertently exacerbate existing tensions or create new ones.3 Finally, a critical gap exists in skills for inclusive decision-making facilitation. Effective conflict resolution demands processes that ensure resolutions are not dominated by powerful voices, but instead protect and integrate the interests of women, youth, and other marginalized groups who may be disproportionately affected by resource-use decisions.3 Building this multi-layered capacity for conflict management is not an ancillary activity; it is a core requirement for ensuring the social sustainability of the project and preventing it from becoming a source of division rather than cohesion.

### 1.4 Enhancing Technical Skills for Restoration, MRV, and Co-Benefit Delivery

Effective community participation in a modern blue carbon project extends beyond governance to include a suite of new technical skills. These skills are essential for communities to contribute meaningfully to project activities, ensure the quality of outcomes, and take ownership of long-term monitoring and enterprise management. The diagnosis reveals significant gaps in these technical areas, alongside a strong willingness among community members, particularly youth and women, to acquire new competencies.

The most prominent technical gap relates to participatory Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV). The credibility of any carbon project hinges on its ability to accurately measure, report, and verify carbon sequestration and other outcomes.7 While youth across the sites expressed high interest in these roles, they currently lack the skills to use digital tools such as GPS for mapping, geo-tagged photography for tracking progress, and basic mobile applications for data entry.3 Training in these areas is crucial for establishing a cost-effective, community-led MRV system, which aligns with the global push toward Digital MRV (D-MRV) to enhance transparency and reduce transaction costs.8

In the area of restoration, women’s groups have shown high interest in managing community nursery operations, a critical first step in the mangrove replanting process. However, they require practical training in seedling propagation techniques, pest and disease management, and the basic business skills needed to run a nursery as a sustainable small enterprise.3 Similarly, while there is high community demand for

alternative livelihoods such as aquaculture and mangrove beekeeping to reduce pressure on existing resources, there is a corresponding lack of local technical expertise to guide these ventures.3 For co-benefit interventions like improved cookstoves, the need extends to

cookstove entrepreneurship. Women are not only the primary users but are also well-positioned to lead local enterprises for stove distribution, maintenance, and repair, but they require the necessary business and technical skills to create a sustainable local supply chain.3

### 1.5 Targeted Capacity Building for Women, Youth, and Marginalized Groups

While women and youth are consistently identified as key agents for change and are often the most enthusiastic potential participants, they face specific and systemic barriers to accessing training and leadership opportunities. Field consultations revealed that conventional training programs often exclude them due to inconvenient timing that clashes with domestic or livelihood duties, the use of technical language without local translation, and physically inaccessible venues.3 Persons with disabilities (PWDs) are almost entirely excluded from planning processes, representing a significant failure of inclusion.3 Addressing these barriers requires a targeted approach that recognizes the unique needs and potential contributions of these groups.

The specific training priorities identified for each group reflect their potential roles within the blue carbon framework. For women, the priorities are skills that build on their existing roles in the household and local economy: cooperative leadership, financial management for VSLAs, cookstove entrepreneurship, and nursery operations.3 For youth, the focus is on leveraging their aptitude for technology and communication: training in digital MRV tools (GPS, photography), technical skills for new enterprises like aquaculture, and developing their capacity for "storytelling for restoration" to become effective community advocates and educators.3 For PWDs, the immediate need is for foundational training on inclusion tools, the provision of accessible documentation, and clear guidance on how to participate in project governance and grievance mechanisms.3

This targeted approach represents a strategic opportunity to foster empowerment rather than mere token inclusion. Traditional governance structures are frequently male-dominated and systematically exclude women and youth from substantive decision-making.3 The blue carbon project, however, introduces new technical functions, such as digital MRV and formalized nursery management, that do not have pre-existing traditional roles or hierarchies.3 The evidence indicates that youth possess a high interest and aptitude for digital tools, while women’s groups are already organized around cooperative enterprises and are prime candidates for leading nursery management.3 By formally training and equipping these specific groups to perform these essential new project roles, the initiative can vest them with a source of authority and influence based on technical competence, not just traditional status. This creates a parallel pathway to empowerment that can gradually enhance their voice in broader community governance, turning a potential weakness (systemic exclusion) into a strategic strength by cultivating new leaders for new, critical tasks.

## Section 2: Foundational Infrastructure Deficits Hindering Participation and Performance

The success of community-led blue carbon initiatives is contingent not only on human and institutional capacity but also on the presence of basic physical infrastructure. This section reframes infrastructure not as a separate development "ask" or a long-term co-benefit, but as a core component of the immediate enabling environment. The analysis demonstrates how deficits in access, sanitation, and communication directly undermine participation, compromise inclusion, and threaten the operational viability of restoration and monitoring activities. Addressing these gaps is a prerequisite for effective and equitable project implementation.

### 2.1 Access and Mobility Constraints on Restoration and Monitoring

A significant operational barrier identified across all three Ramsar sites is the physical difficulty of reaching key restoration and monitoring zones. Many communities are semi-isolated due to poor or non-existent feeder roads, paths that become flooded and impassable during the rainy season, and a lack of basic bridges or culverts.3 This severely hampers the ability of restoration teams, Wildlife Division officers, and community monitors to transport seedlings, conduct planting and maintenance activities, and carry out essential verification tasks.

The impact of these constraints is most acute in the most remote and ecologically sensitive areas. For instance, the community of Bomigo in the Keta Lagoon Complex is only accessible by canoe, a logistical challenge that severely limits the scale of restoration efforts and the frequency of monitoring.3 Other communities, such as Klonu, Akosua Village, and Patukope, face seasonal inaccessibility, meaning that crucial planting or monitoring windows can be missed entirely.3 The direct consequence is a high risk that restoration targets will not be met and that MRV data, which is essential for carbon credit issuance, cannot be collected reliably or consistently. This physical isolation translates directly into programmatic and financial risk.

### 2.2 Sanitation and Community Facilities as Enablers of Inclusive Participation

The diagnosis revealed a stark lack of basic community facilities that are fundamental to ensuring inclusive participation. A critical and recurring finding was the absence of adequate sanitation infrastructure, particularly public toilets, at community gathering places. This was identified as a major barrier to the full participation of women in project activities.3 Furthermore, the lack of dedicated meeting shelters or "CREMA huts" means that community governance meetings are often held under trees, making them vulnerable to weather disruptions and leaving no formal, secure space for storing records, displaying information, or conducting official business.3

The impact of these deficits is not abstract; it is a direct and quantifiable barrier to inclusion. During focus group discussions, women in Elavanyo and Sota explicitly stated their reluctance to attend all-day training sessions or extended community meetings due to the lack of toilets.3 This is not a matter of convenience but a fundamental obstacle that systematically disenfranchises half the community.

This finding necessitates a reframing of such infrastructure from a "co-benefit" to a "safeguard prerequisite." The WACA project is bound by the World Bank's Environmental and Social Framework (ESF), which includes Environmental and Social Standard 10 (ESS10) on "Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure.".4 This standard requires that consultations be conducted in a manner that is accessible and inclusive, allowing all segments of the community to participate meaningfully.4 The field data provides direct testimony that the absence of gender-segregated sanitation facilities systematically prevents women from participating fully in project meetings and training. This exclusion is a predictable and preventable outcome of the infrastructural deficit. Therefore, any consultation or decision-making process conducted in a location without adequate sanitation fails the test of inclusivity mandated by the project's own safeguard policies. This means that investing in small-scale, gender-sensitive infrastructure like toilet blocks is not simply "development assistance"; it is a non-negotiable operational cost required to ensure the project's legal and ethical compliance.

### 2.3 Technology and Communication Gaps in the Digital MRV Era

The ambition to leverage modern, cost-effective Digital MRV (D-MRV) systems is directly undermined by a significant "digital divide" in the most remote project communities. Field assessments confirmed poor or non-existent mobile network coverage, a lack of reliable electricity for charging devices like smartphones or GPS units, and the absence of public announcement systems for disseminating information broadly.3 

This technological gap has serious implications for project equity and efficiency. It threatens to exclude the most marginalized communities from participating in and benefiting from modern MRV approaches, thereby reinforcing existing inequalities. Operationally, it makes real-time data submission impossible, increases the risk of data loss, and forces a continued reliance on costly and infrequent physical visits by external technical staff. Without targeted interventions to address these communication and energy deficits, the goal of a truly participatory and decentralized MRV system will remain unattainable.

### 2.4 Essential Infrastructure for Nurseries and Livelihood Enterprises

The physical infrastructure needed to support the project's core restoration and livelihood activities is largely absent. Community nurseries, which are the cornerstone of the mangrove restoration strategy, are particularly vulnerable. They typically lack permanent shade structures, relying instead on temporary palm fronds or tarpaulins, and have no reliable water sources, such as boreholes or rainwater harvesting systems. Secure storage sheds for tools and materials are also missing.3 These deficiencies have already led to tangible negative outcomes, with reports of significant seedling mortality in Tunu and Salo due to a lack of adequate shade and water.3

The community's own understanding of these operational bottlenecks is clear. When asked to rank their infrastructure priorities, residents consistently focused on small-scale, practical solutions directly linked to project activities. The most frequently requested items were storage shelters for tools, reliable water sources for nurseries, canopies or sheds for community meetings, and toilet blocks.3 This convergence of community-identified needs and project operational requirements provides a clear and compelling case for prioritizing investment in this "enabling infrastructure" as an integral part of the project's start-up phase.

## Section 3: A Strategic Framework for Sequenced Capacity and Institutional Strengthening

Based on the comprehensive diagnosis of capacity and infrastructure gaps, this section synthesizes the findings into a coherent, actionable, and phased implementation plan. The framework is designed to guide the WACA Blue Carbon initiative in building institutional resilience from the ground up. It provides a strategic roadmap for sequencing interventions, ensuring that foundational elements of governance and trust are established before more complex activities, such as carbon finance, are introduced. This approach is designed to be participatory in its delivery, adaptive in its management, and targeted in its focus on different stakeholder groups.

### 3.1 Core Principles: A Phased, Participatory, and Adaptive Approach

A successful capacity-building strategy cannot be a one-size-fits-all, single-shot intervention. It must be guided by principles that reflect the complexity of community dynamics and the long-term nature of institutional change. The following principles form the foundation of this strategic framework.

First, sequencing is critical. The framework is structured in logical phases, recognizing that certain capacities are prerequisites for others.3 Attempting to introduce complex financial mechanisms before establishing basic governance structures and community trust is a primary risk factor, particularly given the identified misalignment between community expectations and the realities of carbon finance. The proposed sequence mitigates this risk:

Phase 1 (0–6 months): Foundational Governance and Trust Building. The immediate focus must be on establishing the basic architecture for community management. This includes facilitating the finalization of CREMA constitutions, conducting elections for CRMCs, delivering intensive training on roles and responsibilities, establishing inclusive consultation processes compliant with FPIC, and building skills in basic conflict resolution.3

Phase 2 (6–12 months): Applied Skills and Systems Development. With governance structures in place, this phase introduces the practical skills needed for project operations. This includes training youth and other designated groups in participatory MRV tools, providing enterprise management training for pilot livelihood groups, and establishing and operationalizing formal grievance redress mechanisms.3

Phase 3 (12–24 months): Advanced Management and Market Readiness. This final phase focuses on higher-level capacities required for long-term sustainability and market engagement. Activities include supporting the formal legal registration of CREMAs, providing training on carbon contract literacy and negotiation, and developing advanced financial management systems for transparent benefit-sharing.3

Second, the framework advocates for participatory delivery models. To overcome the identified barriers of literacy, language, and accessibility, training must employ a diverse range of pedagogical approaches. These include a Training-of-Trainers (ToT) model to build a cadre of local facilitators from among Community Change Agents and teachers; peer-to-peer learning through exchange visits between communities; and the use of accessible media like local radio and community durbars, which are particularly effective for reaching low-literacy audiences and disseminating information broadly.3

Third, the strategy must be grounded in adaptive management. The institutional landscape is dynamic, and the capacity-building plan must be flexible enough to respond to emerging challenges and opportunities. This requires building in regular monitoring and feedback loops, such as the use of participatory scorecards where communities can self-assess their progress, and holding regular forums to discuss challenges and adjust the implementation plan accordingly.12

### 3.2 Stakeholder-Specific Intervention Packages

To ensure that training is relevant and targeted, the framework proposes the development of "bundled packages" of materials and modules tailored to the specific needs of different stakeholder groups.3 These kits would standardize the core content while allowing for local adaptation, ensuring consistency in quality and messaging across the project sites.

Blue Carbon Basics Kit: Designed for community-wide awareness campaigns, this package would be targeted at youth, women's groups, and teachers. It would use accessible formats like posters, short videos, and simple facilitation guides to explain the core concepts of climate change, blue carbon, and the project's objectives.

CREMA Governance Starter Kit: This is a critical package for newly formed CRMCs, CECs, and traditional authorities. It would contain a sample constitution and bylaw templates, guides for conducting inclusive meetings, simple voting procedures, and modules on conflict resolution and leadership ethics.

MRV and Restoration Tracker Kit: Targeted at designated youth groups and nursery managers, this kit would provide practical, hands-on tools for monitoring. It would include guides for using GPS-enabled mobile apps, templates for photo logbooks, and simple checklists for tracking mangrove survival rates and nursery production.

Safeguards and FPIC Toolkit: This package is essential for Wildlife Division officers, District Assembly staff, and Community Change Agents who will be responsible for leading consultation processes. It would include standardized consent forms, templates for grievance logs, and practical tips and checklists for ensuring inclusive and culturally appropriate engagement.

Women’s Enterprise Pack: Aimed at VSLAs and women's cooperatives, this package would provide practical business planning templates for priority enterprises identified in the baseline, such as improved salt processing, soap making, or agroforestry products.

## Section 4: An Ecosystem of Partnerships for Sustainable Implementation

The successful delivery of the comprehensive capacity-building framework outlined in this report requires a robust and coordinated collaborative architecture. No single institution possesses the full range of skills, resources, and community trust needed to address the multifaceted challenges identified. This final section outlines the essential ecosystem of partnerships required for sustainable implementation. It defines the specific and complementary roles of government agencies, civil society, research institutions, and the private sector, and proposes concrete mechanisms to ensure their efforts are aligned, efficient, and mutually reinforcing.

### 4.1 Mapping Partner Roles: Government, Civil society, Research, and Private Sector

A multi-stakeholder approach is essential, leveraging the unique strengths of different actors to create a synergistic support system for communities.3

Government Agencies: The role of government institutions is to provide the essential policy framework, regulatory oversight, and legal legitimacy for the entire initiative. The Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) and the Wildlife Division (WD) are the lead agencies for CREMA policy alignment, legal registration, and technical supervision of restoration.3 The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) is critical for ensuring alignment with national climate strategies (NDCs) and for overseeing MRV systems for co-benefits.3 At the local level, District Assemblies (DAs) are indispensable for integrating CREMA management plans into official Medium-Term Development Plans (MTDPs), providing budgetary support for small infrastructure, and enforcing local bylaws.3

NGOs and Civil Society Organizations (CSOs): These actors are the primary implementation partners at the community level. Organizations with long-standing relationships in the coastal landscapes, such as A Rocha Ghana and Hen Mpoano, are uniquely positioned to act as trusted intermediaries. Their key functions include facilitating CREMA formation, delivering specialized training in governance and livelihoods, and conducting independent monitoring of safeguard implementation to ensure accountability.3 They bridge the gap between government policy and grassroots reality.

Academic and Research Institutions: To ensure technical robustness and innovation, partnerships with academic institutions are vital. The University of Ghana (IESS) and the University of Cape Coast (UCC) can provide independent scientific validation for carbon stock measurements, advise on best practices for mangrove restoration, and conduct policy-relevant research to inform adaptive management.3 Institutions like the
Center for Remote Sensing and GIS (CERSGIS) can support the development and analysis of spatial data for MRV.3

Private Sector: The engagement of the private sector is crucial for ensuring the market viability of alternative livelihoods. This includes eco-tourism operators who can help develop community-based tourism products, salt distributors who can support value chain upgrades, and manufacturers of clean cookstoves who can assist with supply chain development.3
Mobile network operators can also be key partners in developing digital platforms for MRV data submission and mobile money systems for transparent benefit payments.3

### 4.2 Coordination Platforms for Effective Collaboration

A collection of well-meaning partners acting independently can lead to fragmented, duplicative, and ultimately unsustainable interventions. The Socio-Economic Report explicitly identifies "inadequate coordination" between agencies as a key project risk.3 Furthermore, the history of CREMA development in Ghana shows a pattern of collapse when short-term, donor-funded projects end, highlighting the need for more durable coordination structures.2 To mitigate this risk, the following coordination platforms are proposed:

National Blue Carbon Working Group: A high-level strategic body co-chaired by MLNR and MEST, bringing together key government agencies, lead development partners, and civil society representatives. Its purpose is to ensure policy coherence, coordinate major funding streams, and resolve high-level bottlenecks.3

District CREMA Coordination Committees: This is the most critical operational platform. Chaired by the District Chief Executive or a designate, this committee would bring together the District Assembly, the local Wildlife Division officer, representatives from traditional authorities, and the leadership of the CRMCs within the district. This forum would be responsible for joint work planning, monitoring progress, and resolving local-level implementation challenges.3

CREMA Technical Support Hub: This represents a necessary institutional innovation to ensure the quality, consistency, and long-term sustainability of capacity building across all sites. The historical model of CREMA support in Ghana has been project-based, with different NGOs providing varying levels of support to different CREMAs, often leading to inconsistencies and a loss of capacity when a specific project ends.2 To avoid repeating this failure, a permanent or semi-permanent "Hub," potentially hosted by a lead NGO consortium, is needed. This Hub would serve as a repository of knowledge, a guarantor of training standards, and a source of continuous technical backstopping, independent of any single project's lifecycle. It would standardize training curricula, harmonize MRV methodologies, and provide on-demand support to all sites, ensuring that the capacity built during the WACA project is maintained and can be scaled to other CREMAs in the future.



### 4.3 Aligning Development Partner Support with National Systems

Finally, the role of development partners, including the World Bank and other donors, must be strategically aligned to support and strengthen these national and local systems, rather than creating parallel project structures.3 The recommendations for partners are clear: move beyond direct, short-term project implementation towards a model of platform-level support. This involves:

Financing National Systems: Channeling funds through government and local systems where feasible to build their capacity and ensure long-term ownership. This includes providing financial support for the operationalization of the District CREMA Coordination Committees and the CREMA Technical Support Hub.

Prioritizing Institutional Development: Earmarking a significant portion of funding for the "soft" aspects of the project, governance training, conflict mediation, and financial literacy, recognizing that these are the foundations for all "hard" restoration outcomes.

Adopting Long-Term, Flexible Funding Models: Supporting institutional development is a slow and non-linear process 15 Donors should adopt funding models that are long-term (5+ years) and flexible, allowing for adaptive management in response to evolving community needs, rather than rigid, output-based contracts that can stifle local innovation and ownership. By investing in the institutional ecosystem, development partners can ensure their resources are catalytic, helping Ghana to build a blue carbon model that is not only globally relevant but also locally grounded and built to last.
