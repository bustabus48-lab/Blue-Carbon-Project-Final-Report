Livelihood Diversification and Blue Carbon Readiness (LDBR)



1.0 Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive assessment of livelihood diversification options compatible with the objectives of the West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project (WACA ResIP 2) Blue Carbon initiative in Ghana. The analysis covers the Songor, Keta Lagoon Complex, and Muni-Pomadze Ramsar sites, evaluating the feasibility of specific enterprises—aquaculture, apiculture, ecotourism, and artisanal salt value-chain upgrading—and assessing the technical, market, and social readiness for their implementation.

The rationale for diversification is compelling: over 70% of households across the project sites depend on mangrove-linked activities that contribute to ecosystem degradation, creating a cycle of environmental decline and economic vulnerability.1 Blue carbon-compatible livelihoods offer a pathway to break this cycle by reducing pressure on natural resources, building community resilience, and creating direct incentives for conservation.

Key findings indicate that while all four assessed options hold potential, their readiness and risk profiles vary significantly across the sites.

Mangrove Apiculture (Beekeeping) emerges as the highest-potential, lowest-risk option for immediate piloting across all three Ramsar sites. It requires low capital, is highly inclusive of women and youth, and directly supports restoration goals through pollination, creating a powerful ecological and economic synergy.1

Small-Scale Aquaculture is economically viable but is constrained by systemic challenges, primarily the high cost of fish feed and limited access to quality fingerlings and extension services.3 Its success is contingent on integrated interventions that address the entire value chain.

Artisanal Salt Value-Chain Upgrading in the Songor lagoon presents a major economic opportunity but is mired in deep-seated land-use conflicts with large-scale commercial operations.6 Realizing its potential requires facilitated dialogue and the formalization of community benefit-sharing agreements alongside technical support.

Community-Based Ecotourism holds significant long-term potential, particularly in the Keta Lagoon Complex, but suffers from critical gaps in infrastructure, governance, and market linkages.8 It requires substantial public investment and institutional strengthening before it can become a viable community enterprise.

The enabling environment for these enterprises is nascent. While national policies for carbon markets and community resource management are advancing, a significant gap exists in translating this policy into tangible support at the district and community levels.1 Strengthening the capacity of District Assemblies and the Wildlife Division to act as intermediaries is paramount.

To mitigate risks such as the displacement of destructive activities (leakage), this report recommends a phased approach to implementation. This strategy begins with foundational pilots in high-readiness communities—Sankor, Pute, and Dzita—to generate demonstrable successes and refine models before scaling. Livelihood diversification is identified as the single most critical strategy for mitigating leakage risk, as providing viable economic alternatives reduces the incentive for communities to continue or shift destructive practices.11

This report concludes that a strategic, site-specific investment in livelihood diversification is not merely a co-benefit of the blue carbon initiative; it is a prerequisite for its long-term success. By empowering communities with sustainable enterprises, the project can secure the social license and local stewardship necessary to protect and restore Ghana's vital coastal ecosystems for generations to come.

2.0 Diversification Rationale & Criteria for Blue Carbon Landscapes

The strategic imperative for livelihood diversification within Ghana's coastal Ramsar sites is anchored in the profound and often precarious relationship between communities and their natural environment. Field assessments confirm that over 70% of households in the Songor, Keta, and Muni-Pomadze landscapes depend directly on mangrove-linked activities, including fish smoking, salt production, and the harvesting of fuelwood and construction poles.1 This intense dependency has created a reinforcing feedback loop: essential livelihood activities drive the degradation of mangrove ecosystems, and as these ecosystems decline, the productivity and resilience of the very livelihoods they support are diminished.1 This cycle increases community vulnerability to climate shocks and economic instability, undermining both local well-being and national conservation goals.

Livelihood diversification is a proven strategy for breaking this cycle. In the context of natural resource management, it is recognized as a critical tool for reducing direct pressure on ecosystems, spreading household economic risk, and building resilience against environmental and market shocks.13 However, for diversification to be successful and gain community acceptance, it must be approached not as "job substitution", which can be perceived as a threat to cultural identity and traditional practices like fishing, but as "portfolio enhancement".13 This approach focuses on introducing complementary income streams that align with existing skills and seasonal labor calendars, thereby strengthening, rather than replacing, the traditional livelihood base.1

Within the WACA Blue Carbon initiative, the goal extends beyond simple economic diversification. The objective is to foster blue carbon-compatible livelihoods: enterprises that are not only economically viable and socially inclusive but also actively contribute to the conservation and restoration of coastal ecosystems. These activities must align with climate mitigation and adaptation goals by reducing drivers of degradation (e.g., deforestation) and, where possible, generating measurable social and environmental co-benefits.15 The Livelihoods Carbon Fund in Senegal provides a compelling international precedent, where funding for mangrove restoration has simultaneously improved biodiversity, supported sustainable farming by reducing soil salinization, and contributed to poverty alleviation.17

To ensure a systematic, transparent, and defensible selection process for such enterprises, a multi-dimensional assessment framework is required. This framework moves beyond a narrow focus on profitability to incorporate critical parameters of sustainability, equity, and alignment with the project's core carbon objectives. 3.0 Site-Specific Livelihood Option Profiles

This section provides a detailed analysis of four high-potential livelihood options: small-scale aquaculture, mangrove apiculture, community-based ecotourism, and artisanal salt value-chain upgrading. Each profile assesses the option's rationale, viability, and specific challenges within the context of the three Ramsar sites.

3.1 Small-Scale Aquaculture (Tilapia & Catfish)

Rationale & Viability: With Ghana's marine fish stocks in decline, the government has strategically positioned aquaculture as a priority to bridge the national fish supply deficit, enhance food security, and create employment in coastal communities.3 This is supported by national initiatives such as the 'Aquaculture for Food and Jobs' program, which aims to expand production and increase opportunities for women and youth.19 Economic analysis confirms that smallholder freshwater aquaculture in coastal Ghana is a profitable venture. A five-year assessment revealed an average Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR) of 1.14, indicating positive returns on investment. Tilapia farming, in particular, appears slightly more attractive for smallholders, with a higher BCR of 1.16 and a shorter payback period of 7 years, compared to 9 years for catfish.3

Challenges: Despite its profitability, the small-scale aquaculture sector is beset by significant sustainability challenges that constrain its growth. The most critical bottleneck is the high cost and inconsistent availability of quality fish feed, which is often imported and constitutes the largest operational expense.4 This single factor drives up first-sale prices, making locally farmed fish less competitive against cheaper imports.21 Furthermore, small-scale producers face limited access to formal credit for start-up capital, a lack of quality, disease-resistant fingerlings, and inadequate technical support from overstretched and generalized government extension services.4 The sector is also highly vulnerable to the impacts of climate change, such as extreme temperatures, flooding, and drought, which can lead to stock loss and damage to pond infrastructure.4

The success of any aquaculture intervention, therefore, cannot be achieved by simply funding the construction of ponds. A sustainable model requires an integrated, value-chain approach. The WACA project has an opportunity not just to support farmers directly but to catalyze solutions to systemic bottlenecks. This could involve partnering with technical institutions to research and pilot low-cost, locally sourced feed formulations (e.g., using black soldier fly larvae, agricultural by-products, or fish processing waste) and linking farmer cooperatives with microfinance institutions or VSLAs to create accessible credit products tailored to the aquaculture production cycle.1

3.2 Mangrove Apiculture (Beekeeping)

Rationale & Viability: Mangrove apiculture is an exemplary blue carbon-compatible enterprise, offering a powerful synergy between livelihood improvement and ecosystem restoration. Mangrove forests provide a rich and consistent source of nectar throughout the year, particularly during dry seasons when other floral resources are scarce. This results in healthy, productive bee colonies and high yields of premium-quality honey, ensuring a reliable income stream for beekeepers.2 The practice creates a direct and tangible economic incentive for communities to protect and expand mangrove habitats, perfectly embodying the principle that "healthy mangroves mean more honey.".2

Beekeeping is a highly viable and inclusive option for the project's target communities. It requires a low capital outlay, does not depend on land ownership, and can be practiced on a flexible schedule, making it exceptionally accessible for women, youth, and landless households.14 Evidence from coastal communities in The Gambia demonstrates that beekeeping initiatives are often led by women, who manage the processing and marketing of honey and value-added products like beeswax balms, soaps, and creams, thereby gaining economic empowerment and self-reliance.2 The socio-economic baseline for the WACA project confirms a high level of interest and readiness among women's groups in the Ramsar sites to lead beekeeping enterprises with initial support.1

Market & Support: While Ghana's formal honey export market is currently small, robust local and regional markets exist.25 More importantly, successful social enterprise models have already been established in the country. Organizations like Bees for Development Ghana operate Honey and Beeswax Trade Centres that provide smallholders with a guaranteed market, fair and transparent pricing, quality control, and a traceability system, effectively de-risking the enterprise for new entrants.26 This demonstrates a clear and replicable pathway for connecting community producers to a stable value chain.

The value of apiculture extends beyond honey production. The pollination services provided by bees are critical for the regeneration of mangrove forests and also boost the productivity of nearby agricultural crops.2 This creates a positive ecological feedback loop, where the livelihood activity actively enhances the health of the ecosystem it depends on, strengthening both environmental resilience and local food security. This dual benefit passively reduces pressure on wood resources while actively supporting restoration, positioning apiculture as a high-priority, "win-win" intervention for the WACA Blue Carbon initiative.

3.3 Community-Based Ecotourism

Potential: Ghana's coastal Ramsar sites, particularly the expansive Keta Lagoon Complex, possess significant, largely untapped potential for ecotourism. Their international importance as habitats for vast populations of migratory waterbirds, combined with their unique cultural landscapes, makes them attractive destinations for a growing global market of tourists seeking authentic, nature-based experiences.8 Government policy is supportive of developing non-mass, community-based ecotourism that respects and conserves natural and cultural heritage.29 Successful models within Ghana, such as the restoration of the Weto landscape in the Volta Region, which subsequently spurred the development of ecotourism facilities, demonstrate that ecological investments can yield tourism dividends.28

Challenges: Despite this high potential, the current reality is one of severe underdevelopment and systemic failure. The primary barrier is a critical deficit in basic infrastructure. Field assessments in the Volta Region reveal that many potential sites are inaccessible due to poor road networks, and lack essential visitor facilities such as accommodation, sanitation, restaurants, and security, rendering them unattractive and uncomfortable for tourists.9

Furthermore, the governance model for existing community-based ecotourism initiatives in Ghana is often flawed. Community participation is frequently described as "tokenistic" or "elusive," with local people having little to no involvement in planning and decision-making.30 This leads to inequitable distribution of the minimal revenues that are generated, fostering community disillusionment and undermining the sense of ownership required for long-term sustainability.31 Compounding these issues is a lack of professional skills in hospitality, business management, and interpretive guiding, as well as a near-total absence of effective marketing strategies to connect the sites with potential visitor markets.9

Ecotourism is therefore a high-investment, high-risk proposition that cannot be successfully piloted at a small, household scale. It requires a coordinated, multi-stakeholder effort with significant upfront capital. A simple intervention like training tour guides is destined to fail if the enabling environment is not in place. This means that ecotourism development must be strategically integrated into the Medium-Term Development Plans (MTDPs) of the respective District Assemblies, which can unlock public funding for essential infrastructure like roads and sanitation.1 A phased approach is essential, beginning with foundational work on strengthening CREMA governance to manage sites effectively and establishing transparent benefit-sharing agreements. Only after these governance and infrastructure prerequisites are met can the project viably invest in product development and marketing.

3.4 Artisanal Salt Value-Chain Upgrading

Potential: The Songor Ramsar site is home to West Africa's largest naturally occurring solar salt basin, presenting a monumental economic opportunity for Ghana.6 Projections indicate that with strategic investment and management, the salt industry could increase its contribution to national export earnings to over $1 billion annually by 2028, transforming Ghana into a major global producer.6 For local communities, there is a significant opportunity to upgrade the artisanal value chain. This involves moving from the production of low-value, unrefined bulk salt to higher-value products, such as purified, iodized, and branded table salt for domestic consumption and specialized industrial-grade salt for regional export markets.6

Challenges: The primary challenge in the Songor lagoon is not technical but socio-political. The landscape is characterized by intense and sometimes violent conflict between large-scale commercial salt production, led by Electrochem Ghana Ltd., and the indigenous small-scale producers who have been displaced from their ancestral lands.6 This has eroded trust and created a highly contentious environment, questioning the legitimacy of the large-scale operations. Small-scale producers, predominantly women, face a host of additional barriers, including inconsistent product quality due to impurities, a widespread lack of iodization practices, inadequate storage facilities, and limited access to formal markets, where they are often outcompeted by larger, more organized producers.33

Any intervention aimed at supporting small-scale salt producers must therefore adopt a dual strategy. A project that focuses solely on technical training in salt purification and iodization will be ineffective if the producers have no secure access to the salt pans. The first, and most critical, line of action is facilitation and mediation. The WACA project, with its high-level government backing from MLNR and MEST, is uniquely positioned to convene a structured dialogue between small-scale producer groups, traditional authorities, and the management of Electrochem Ltd. The goal of this dialogue should be to formalize and implement the "community-focused masterplan" that has been committed to, ensuring it codifies secure resource access rights and transparent benefit-sharing mechanisms for local communities.6 In parallel, the project can provide technical and business development support to producer cooperatives, focusing on improving quality control, establishing branding, and forging direct linkages to higher-value markets.

4.1 Technical Readiness (Skills & Inputs)

Technical readiness varies significantly by enterprise. For aquaculture, while traditional knowledge of fish ponds exists, there is a clear gap in modern husbandry skills related to site selection, water quality management, feed optimization, and disease control.22 A major technical barrier is the widespread lack of access to quality, affordable fingerlings, with a single government-run center serving the entire country.5 Government extension services for aquaculture are often generalized and lack the specialized expertise needed to effectively support farmers.22

In contrast, apiculture builds on a strong foundation of indigenous knowledge. The transition to more productive and sustainable methods, such as the use of Kenyan Top Bar hives, requires specific training, but this is readily available from several specialized NGOs and social enterprises operating in Ghana.38 These organizations also provide access to necessary inputs like starter kits and protective equipment, making the technical barrier to entry relatively low.

Ecotourism exhibits the lowest technical readiness. Existing community skills are largely informal and insufficient for operating a professional tourism enterprise. There is a profound need for capacity building in areas such as interpretive guiding, hospitality management, visitor safety, and business administration.29

For artisanal salt production, communities possess deep, generationally transferred knowledge of solar evaporation techniques.35 However, technical skills related to modern quality control, such as purification methods to reduce impurities and the proper application of potassium iodate for iodization, are largely absent.34

4.2 Market Readiness (Value Chains & Access)

Market readiness is a critical determinant of an enterprise's sustainability. For aquaculture, there is a strong and guaranteed domestic market, driven by Ghana's significant national fish deficit.3 The primary challenge is not a lack of demand, but the inability of small-scale producers to compete on price due to high input costs, particularly for feed.21 Poor road infrastructure in some remote communities further limits their ability to get fresh products to urban markets efficiently.1

Apiculture has demonstrated market readiness through established local and regional value chains. Social enterprises have successfully created aggregation models that provide beekeepers with a guaranteed off-take market at fair prices, overcoming a key barrier for small producers.26 While the formal export market for Ghanaian honey is still developing, there is clear potential for growth.25

The market for ecotourism is characterized by high potential but weak linkages. While national marketing campaigns and regional trade fairs signal growing interest in Ghana's tourism offerings, the community-level sites are disconnected from these market channels.43 The lack of a developed, marketable "product" (e.g., well-maintained trails, trained guides, visitor packages) and effective promotion means that potential tourists are often unaware of or unable to access these sites.9

For artisanal salt, small-scale producers currently serve local and informal cross-border markets in neighboring countries like Niger, Benin, and Burkina Faso.47 However, they are largely excluded from the more lucrative domestic industrial and formal export markets, which demand higher standards of quality, consistency, and volume that small, unorganized producers cannot meet.36

4.3 Social License & Inclusion

Social license, the level of community acceptance and support, is crucial for project success. Apiculture enjoys a very high social license across all sites. It is widely perceived as a beneficial, low-conflict activity, and is particularly favored by women's groups who see it as an accessible and empowering enterprise.1 Its non-exclusive use of land means it does not generate resource-based conflicts.

Aquaculture also has high community interest but carries a moderate risk of elite capture if access to land and water resources for pond construction is not managed equitably. However, it offers clear opportunities for including women and youth in post-harvest processing and local sales.19

The social license for ecotourism is mixed and fragile. While communities are eager for the economic benefits tourism can bring, this is often tempered by skepticism born from past experiences with projects that featured "token" participation and inequitable benefit sharing.30 Without transparent and genuinely participatory governance and benefit-sharing mechanisms established from the outset, ecotourism projects risk generating significant community conflict.

In the Songor lagoon, the social license for artisanal salt enterprises is complex. While there is strong community support for small-scale production as a traditional livelihood, the overarching context is one of deep-seated conflict and grievance against large-scale commercial operations.7 Any intervention must navigate this history of mistrust and actively work to rebuild social cohesion around a shared and equitable vision for the resource. The enterprise has high potential for the inclusion of women, who form the backbone of the artisanal workforce 33

5.0 The Enabling Environment for Blue Carbon Enterprises



The success of community-level livelihood diversification depends on a supportive external environment that provides access to finance, coherent policies, and effective partnerships. The current enabling environment for blue carbon-compatible enterprises in Ghana is emerging but requires significant strengthening to bridge the gap between national ambition and local reality.

Access to Finance: A primary constraint for rural entrepreneurs across all sectors is the limited access to affordable formal credit.4 In this context, informal Village Savings and Loan Associations (VSLAs) have become a vital part of the grassroots financial infrastructure, particularly for women's groups.1 These VSLAs provide a trusted and accessible platform for savings mobilization and small-scale lending, representing a critical entry point for financial support.1 A key strategy for creating an enabling financial environment is to build upon this existing social capital by linking established VSLAs to larger microfinance institutions, providing them with matching grants, or integrating conservation-based incentives into their lending models.1

Supportive Policies: At the national level, Ghana is developing a supportive policy framework. This includes the National Aquaculture Development Plan, which prioritizes smallholder production; the ongoing development of a national carbon market framework and registry under the NDC Support Programme; and the legal recognition of Community Resource Management Areas (CREMAs) as decentralized governance bodies under the Wildlife Resources Management Act (Act 1115, 2023).1 However, a significant disconnect exists between these national policies and their implementation at the local level. The critical gap lies in the capacity of District Assemblies (DAs) to integrate these national priorities into their Medium-Term Development Plans (MTDPs), enact supportive by-laws, and allocate budget for community-level initiatives.1

Strategic Partnerships: No single actor can create the enabling environment alone. A multi-stakeholder partnership approach is essential. Development partners play a crucial role in providing catalytic funding and technical assistance, but their interventions must be carefully aligned with national systems like the CREMA framework to avoid creating parallel structures and ensure long-term sustainability.1 Partnerships with the private sector can offer vital market linkages, technology transfer, and business development expertise, as seen in the apiculture sector.38 Collaborations with national research and training institutions, such as the Aquaculture Research and Development Center and universities, are necessary to address technical skill gaps and pilot innovations.37

The most critical component of the enabling environment is the meso-level infrastructure, the District Assemblies, and the local offices of the Wildlife Division. These institutions are uniquely positioned to act as the bridge between national policy and community action. However, they are often under-resourced and lack the specific capacity for this role. Therefore, a primary investment of the WACA project should be in strengthening the capabilities of these key local government and state agency partners to facilitate joint planning with communities, co-manage the CREMA establishment process, and enforce the by-laws that give local effect to national environmental and climate policies.

6.0 Pilots & Scale-up Pathways

A phased and adaptive implementation strategy is essential for successfully launching and scaling blue carbon-compatible livelihoods. This approach allows for learning and risk management, starting with targeted pilots in communities with the highest readiness before expanding to other areas. The socio-economic baseline study identified Sankor (Muni-Pomadze), Pute (Songor), and Dzita (Keta) as "early action hubs" based on a combination of favorable ecological conditions, strong community cohesion, and existing momentum for CREMA development.1 The multi-dimensional readiness assessment further refines this, indicating a need for differentiated strategies even within these hubs.1 The following three-phase pathway is proposed to guide implementation over a five-year period.

Phase 1 (Year 1): Foundational Pilots in High-Readiness Hubs

The initial phase focuses on establishing proof of concept by launching targeted pilots in the most promising communities. The objective is to generate tangible results, test operational models, and build local capacity and confidence.

Muni-Pomadze (Sankor and surrounding communities): Given its high readiness in CREMA development, institutional capacity, and community cohesion, this site is ideal for piloting an integrated aquaculture and apiculture model. The intervention would work with established women's groups to develop fish ponds and beekeeping enterprises. Support would be explicitly linked to the finalization of the CREMA constitution and the establishment of a transparent benefit-sharing plan, creating a direct link between enterprise development and improved governance.

Songor (Pute and Totope): The focus here will be on piloting artisanal salt value-chain upgrading. The intervention will support small-scale producer cooperatives (primarily women) with training and equipment for salt purification, iodization, and improved packaging. Critically, this technical support will be paired with high-level, facilitated dialogue, convened by the WACA project, to address the underlying resource access and tenure conflicts with large-scale producers, aiming to formalize a community-focused masterplan.6

Keta Lagoon Complex (Dzita): In Keta, where CREMA structures are fragmented and carbon literacy is low, the initial focus must be foundational.1 The pilot will concentrate on
strengthening governance and building awareness. This involves facilitating a process to harmonize and formalize the CREMA structure, alongside launching a comprehensive climate and carbon literacy campaign led by trained youth and Community Change Agents (CCAs). This will build the necessary social and institutional groundwork for subsequent livelihood interventions.

Phase 2 (Years 2-3): Adaptive Scale-up and Diversification

Based on the lessons and successes from Phase 1, the second phase will involve scaling proven models to communities with moderate readiness and introducing new livelihood options.

Successful models from the pilot hubs will be replicated in adjacent communities, using the initial participants as mentors and trainers.

Apiculture, given its low-risk and high-inclusion profile, will be scaled up across all three Ramsar sites, becoming a core component of the diversification strategy.

In the Keta Lagoon Complex, contingent on demonstrable progress in strengthening CREMA governance and securing commitments from the District Assembly for infrastructure improvements, the project will initiate planning for community-based ecotourism. This will involve feasibility studies, site planning, and the development of a comprehensive business and benefit-sharing plan.

Phase 3 (Years 4-5): Consolidation and Institutionalization

The final phase will focus on ensuring the long-term sustainability and institutional integration of the livelihood enterprises.

Support the formation of CREMA federations or landscape-level cooperatives that can manage value chains, negotiate with buyers, and access financial services at a larger scale.

Fully integrate the monitoring of livelihood outcomes (e.g., income changes, number of enterprises) into the national blue carbon Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system.

Actively connect mature community enterprises with larger private sector partners, impact investors, and emerging climate finance mechanisms to secure long-term funding and market access.

7.0 Implications for Carbon Outcomes & Leakage Risk

The introduction of alternative livelihoods is a central component of the project's strategy to achieve positive carbon outcomes by reducing the drivers of mangrove degradation. However, it also introduces the risk of carbon leakage, which occurs when activities that cause greenhouse gas emissions are displaced from the project area to areas outside its boundaries, thereby negating the project's climate benefits.11 A thorough understanding of these dynamics is critical for designing a project with high environmental integrity.

Positive Carbon Outcomes: The primary positive impact of successful livelihood diversification is the reduction of pressure on mangrove ecosystems. Enterprises such as apiculture, sustainable aquaculture, and upgraded salt production can provide alternative sources of income and energy, decreasing the community's reliance on harvesting mangrove wood for fuel and construction.1 This directly contributes to the project's goals of avoiding deforestation and promoting forest regeneration, which in turn protects and enhances carbon stocks stored in mangrove biomass and soils.15

Leakage Risk: Leakage poses a significant threat to the credibility of any carbon project. Two primary forms of leakage are relevant to this initiative 11:

Activity-Shifting Leakage: This occurs when individuals or groups who previously engaged in a destructive activity within the project area simply move their operations to an unprotected area outside the project boundary. For example, if a CREMA by-law restricts charcoal production within the Muni-Pomadze wetland, producers might relocate their kilns to an adjacent, non-CREMA forest patch.

Market Leakage: This is a more indirect effect. If the project successfully reduces the local supply of a commodity like mangrove fuelwood, it could lead to a price increase in the local market. This price signal could then incentivize producers in neighboring, non-project communities to increase their own harvesting of mangroves to meet the market demand.

The experience from REDD+ projects globally shows that leakage risk is highest when interventions focus on restricting the activities of smallholders without providing them with sufficient and viable economic alternatives.11 When a household's source of income is blocked without being replaced, the economic imperative to continue that activity elsewhere becomes overwhelming. This establishes a clear and direct relationship: the success of the livelihood diversification component is the project's single most important leakage mitigation strategy. By providing profitable and socially acceptable alternative enterprises, the project reduces the economic displacement that drives leakage.

Proposed Mitigation Strategies:

To proactively manage and minimize leakage, the project must integrate mitigation measures into its design and monitoring systems.

Landscape-Level Planning: The project should work closely with District Assemblies and traditional authorities to promote the harmonization of land-use plans and by-laws across the entire landscape, not just within the initial CREMA boundaries. This reduces the availability of nearby, unregulated "leakage zones" where destructive activities can easily be displaced.

Prioritize High-Adoption Livelihoods: Investment and support should be concentrated on livelihood alternatives that have the highest likelihood of being adopted and becoming profitable, such as apiculture. Maximizing the uptake of sustainable enterprises directly minimizes the number of households that would still have an economic incentive to engage in destructive practices.

Establish and Monitor a Leakage Belt: The project's MRV system must extend beyond the physical boundaries of the CREMAs. A "leakage belt," a defined buffer zone of several kilometers around each project site, should be established. Within this belt, land-use change should be monitored periodically using a combination of remote sensing (satellite imagery) and community-based monitoring to detect any signs of displaced deforestation or degradation. This data will allow for adaptive management, enabling the project to respond if leakage is identified.

8.0 Conclusion

This assessment confirms that livelihood diversification is not merely an ancillary component of the WACA Blue Carbon initiative but a central pillar essential for achieving its ecological and climate objectives. The analysis of aquaculture, apiculture, ecotourism, and salt value-chain upgrading reveals a landscape of differentiated potential, where no single solution fits all contexts. The success of these interventions hinges on site-specific strategies that are responsive to the unique technical, market, and social readiness of each community.

Apiculture stands out as the most promising, low-risk, and highly inclusive enterprise for immediate, broad-based implementation across all three Ramsar sites. Its unique ability to simultaneously provide income and enhance ecosystem health through pollination makes it an ideal "early win" to build momentum and community trust.

Aquaculture and artisanal salt upgrading represent significant economic opportunities but are constrained by systemic bottlenecks, high feed costs for the former, and deep-seated resource conflicts for the latter. These options require strategic, catalytic interventions that address the entire value chain and the broader political economy, rather than just providing direct support to producers.

Ecotourism remains a long-term aspiration. Its viability is fundamentally dependent on significant public investment in enabling infrastructure and a comprehensive reform of community governance and benefit-sharing models. To pursue it prematurely would risk project failure and community disillusionment.

Ultimately, the long-term sustainability of Ghana's blue carbon ecosystems will be determined by the degree to which local communities become empowered stewards of their own resources. Strategic investment in a portfolio of blue carbon-compatible livelihoods provides the most effective and equitable pathway to achieving this vision, transforming a cycle of degradation into a virtuous circle of conservation and prosperity.
