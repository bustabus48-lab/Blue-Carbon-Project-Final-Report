**Draft status:** working draft for authoring and evidence extraction

## 1. Executive Summary
This report provides a standalone draft of the Drone Survey / EOSDA workstream for the Ghana Blue Carbon reporting package. The current evidence supports a readiness-focused narrative rather than a claim of fully operational continuous monitoring.

The repository already contains named spatial boundary datasets associated with restoration areas, protection areas, a permanent sample plot, and Keta Lagoon boundary layers. These files provide a practical foundation for preparing areas of interest for EOS Data Analytics (EOSDA) hosting and recurring observation workflows. The monitoring-plan evidence for Keta and Ada also supports a structured, map-based monitoring design with quarterly field sampling.

At the same time, the current repository evidence does not yet show complete drone deliverable packages, orthomosaic outputs, full mission metadata, or proof that all relevant areas have already been uploaded into EOSDA and linked to an active monitoring workflow. The report therefore distinguishes between:
- defined monitoring structure and spatial readiness; and
- fully evidenced continuous monitoring implementation.

## 2. Purpose and Scope of This Standalone Report
The purpose of this standalone report is to:
- summarize the drone-linked and spatial-monitoring evidence already available in the repository;
- identify the named areas of interest and boundary assets that can support EOSDA hosting and recurring monitoring;
- explain the current status of monitoring readiness in practical terms for managers and development partners; and
- provide a markdown source report that can be used in the authoring layer and later integrated into the Final Report.

This report should be read as a readiness and setup report unless additional visual and platform evidence is added.

## 3. Evidence Base and Reporting Logic
The source evidence currently supports four core propositions.

1. The Terms of Reference require baseline maps and drone or aerial shots of current area conditions to be stored and used within the reporting package.
2. The final-report blueprint expects drone outputs, boundary refinement, plot imagery, and EOSDA-linked monitoring to help move the project from baseline assessment to operational monitoring.
3. The monitoring plan for Keta and Ada defines a supervised-map-based sampling structure and a quarterly monitoring cycle.
4. The repository contains dated boundary files that identify concrete monitoring units and potential areas of interest.

Taken together, these sources support a practical statement of readiness. They do not yet support a firm claim that full drone-to-EOSDA continuous monitoring is already operational.

## 5. Monitoring Readiness Signals
The monitoring-plan evidence for Keta and Ada supports a practical monitoring design with:
- 57 sampling sites in Keta;
- 23 sampling sites in Ada; and
- a proposed quarterly field-monitoring schedule.

The plan also indicates that community change agents were trained during field work and that data should be transmitted to a centralized point for analysis, storage, and backup. These design features are important because they connect the drone and spatial workstream to the broader monitoring and reporting system.

The available evidence therefore supports a cautious statement that map-based monitoring architecture has been defined for key sites. It does not yet support a statement that all recurring observation workflows are already functioning in a live platform environment.

## 6. Drone Survey Outputs: What Is Evidenced and What Is Not
### 6.1 What is evidenced
- named polygons and boundaries associated with restoration, protection, lagoon, and permanent sample-plot contexts;
- explicit project expectation for drone and aerial imagery in the Terms of Reference;
- a final-report blueprint that expects orthomosaics, plot imagery, boundary refinement, and condition-mapping outputs; and
- a monitoring design that can use supervised maps and recurring site visits.

### 6.2 What is not yet evidenced
- report-ready orthomosaic outputs;
- complete mission logs or flight specifications;
- sensor metadata and processing notes;
- before/after visual panels or condition-mapping figure sets; and
- explicit linkage between each named polygon and a confirmed implementation status such as planned, active, uploaded, or completed.

This means the current report should focus on readiness, setup, and the existing spatial evidence base rather than claiming fully packaged survey outputs.

## 8. How the Drone / EOSDA Workstream Supports the Final Report
Within the Final Report, this workstream helps bridge the gap between one-time baseline production and recurring monitoring. It contributes by:
- improving spatial specificity through named monitoring units and polygons;
- helping define which areas can be hosted and tracked through recurring observation tools;
- providing a structure for integrating field sampling with mapped areas of interest; and
- creating a practical link between site monitoring and the wider MRV system.

These contributions are real and useful even before every visual output or hosting step is complete. The report should therefore emphasize practical readiness and sequenced operationalization.

## 9. Management-Facing Reading of Current Readiness
For managers and development partners, the safest interpretation of the current evidence is:
- the project has already defined important spatial monitoring units;
- the monitoring plan provides a workable quarterly field structure for priority sites;
- the repository contains the inputs needed to begin EOSDA setup and hosting preparation; but
- the evidence package is not yet sufficient to claim full operational continuous monitoring.

This reading preserves credibility while still making it clear that the workstream has advanced beyond abstract planning.

## 10. Evidence Boundaries and Drafting Constraints
This draft should preserve the following constraints:
- do not claim drone-derived area statistics or accuracy values unless explicitly documented in source materials;
- do not claim full national drone coverage, because the current explicit evidence is site-specific;
- do not infer ecological outcomes from the existence of polygon files alone;
- do not treat filename date stamps as confirmed completion dates without supporting metadata; and
- do not claim EOSDA hosting is operational until account setup, polygon upload, and platform views are evidenced.
