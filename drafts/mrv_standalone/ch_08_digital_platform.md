# Chapter 8: Digital Platform and Data Architecture

---

## 8.1 Purpose of the Digital Platform

A national Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system generates data across multiple institutional tiers, dozens of monitoring sites, and several parallel reporting obligations. Ghana's national Blue Carbon MRV system generates data from 57 field monitoring stations at the Keta Lagoon Complex, 23 stations at Ada, a network of Permanent Sample Plots (PSPs), satellite-derived mangrove extent analyses, governance participation records, and quarterly pressure surveillance reports compiled by Community Resource Management Areas (CREMAs) and Community Change Agents (CCAs) at sites including Dzita, Pute, and Sankor. Without a centralised digital architecture to receive, store, validate, and present this data, the system cannot function as an integrated national monitoring framework. Field observations collected at Keta remain disconnected from national carbon accounting unless there is a platform that links them. PSP re-measurement records have no auditable continuity unless they are archived in a structured database with version control and access logging. Governance participation data cannot be assessed for trends unless it is deposited in a system that allows comparison across time periods and across the three landscapes.

The digital platform is not, therefore, a supplementary reporting convenience. It is the connective tissue between field data collection, institutional validation, national reporting, and carbon market disclosure. Since March 2026, the platform has transitioned from a design prototype to an operational **National Framework**, capable of hosting multiple jurisdictional pilots under a unified national umbrella. The Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) and the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI) — the joint custodians of the national MRV system — depend on this platform to fulfil their oversight and reporting obligations. Carbon market verifiers assessing Ghana's blue carbon assets require access to an audit-grade data environment that demonstrates institutional continuity, methodological consistency, and traceability from individual plot measurements to national stock estimates. The platform architecture described in this chapter meets both requirements: operational utility for government institutions managing the monitoring system, and investment-grade data infrastructure for development partners and market participants.

## 8.2 Platform Architecture

The Blue Carbon MRV digital platform is developed and maintained by GAB Climate Smart Investment Ltd, a Ghanaian company with responsibility for the technical build and deployment of the MRV system's digital infrastructure. The platform is built on a modern, cloud-native technology stack designed for scientific data accuracy and secure multi-institutional access.

The front-end application is built in Next.js using the App Router architecture, which enables both server-rendered and client-side interface components. This allows the platform to serve lightweight, role-appropriate views to field-based CCAs and CREMA members accessing the system via smartphone, while simultaneously providing richer, data-intensive dashboards to national-level users at MLNR and MESTI. The user interface is designed with Tailwind CSS and shadcn/ui component libraries, with Mapbox GL JS providing the interactive spatial map views that are central to the platform's monitoring and visualisation functions.

The database is hosted on Supabase Pro — a managed PostgreSQL service with PostGIS spatial extension support — under the project reference `eavqytqxeaswfbytguxs`. The use of PostgreSQL with PostGIS is technically appropriate for this application: the MRV system is fundamentally a spatial data system, and PostGIS enables the platform to store, query, and serve georeferenced data — including mangrove extent polygons, PSP locations, restoration site boundaries, and pressure hotspot overlays — within a relational database that also manages user authentication, access control, and non-spatial records such as field data submissions and governance participation logs. The Supabase environment enforces role-based access control at the database level, meaning that data visibility and editing rights are not simply managed through application logic but are enforced by the underlying data architecture.

The platform is deployed and hosted on Vercel at `blue-carbon-mrv-green.vercel.app`, with continuous deployment managed through the GitHub repository at `GAB-Climate-Smart/blue-carbon-mrv`. The Vercel hosting environment manages automated deployment on code commit, environment variable management, and serves both the Next.js application and API layer. This configuration enables a disciplined development workflow in which database schema changes and application updates pass through version-controlled release stages before reaching the production environment.

What the platform stores and what workflows it enables are inseparable from its architecture. The database is designed as a dual-tier system: a **National Overview** providing top-level metrics for the 30,025 hectares of coastal mangrove extent, and a **Project-Level View** for jurisdictional pilots like the Keta Lagoon Complex. The archive holds the Year 0 baseline geospatial layers; the PSP registry with georeferenced plot locations, carbon pool classifications, and measurement histories; field-based ecological monitoring records from the 80 monitoring stations across Keta and Ada; environmental pressure surveillance data; and governance and safeguard records including CREMA participation records, grievance logs, and benefit-sharing summaries. The workflows the platform enables are structured around the institutional tiers that the MRV system is designed to serve: community-level data submission, district-level validation, national-level review and reporting, and independent audit access.

## 8.3 EOSDA Continuous Satellite Monitoring

The Blue Carbon MRV platform is designed to integrate with EOS Data Analytics (EOSDA), a commercial satellite imagery and monitoring platform that enables continuous observation of defined areas of interest using recurring satellite passes. Within the MRV architecture, EOSDA is intended to provide the continuous satellite monitoring layer that complements ground-based field observations: detecting changes in mangrove canopy cover, identifying potential land conversion events within restoration and protection zones, and generating the automated alert signals that trigger escalation through the institutional response chain.

The named monitoring units for EOSDA integration are already spatially defined. The repository contains dated boundary files for the Dzita restoration areas, the Salo restoration area, the Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1, the Avu protection area, the Aborlove protection area, and the Keta Lagoon and Keta Lagoon Ramsar boundaries. These files — produced between February and March 2026 as part of the spatial baseline package — constitute a practical set of polygons that can be uploaded to EOSDA as areas of interest, enabling recurring observation schedules to be attached to each named monitoring unit. The spatial scaffold is in place.

What the current evidence base does not yet confirm is that EOSDA account configuration, area-of-interest uploads, and active recurring monitoring workflows have been completed. No upload confirmations, EOSDA platform screenshots, or monitoring output exports are currently archived in the project repository. The EOSDA integration must therefore be described accurately as a prepared and well-grounded next step, not as an already-operational continuous monitoring service. The distinction matters for accountability: MLNR and MESTI should understand precisely what is ready to be operationalised and what still requires institutional action to activate.

Once EOSDA is activated, the intended workflow is as follows. Named polygon boundaries for restoration areas, protection zones, and PSP locations are uploaded as areas of interest within the EOSDA platform. EOSDA then applies recurring satellite observation schedules — drawing on Sentinel-2 and higher-resolution imagery — to those defined areas. Normalised Difference Moisture Index (NDMI) outputs, which the existing boundary files already reference in their naming conventions (e.g., `dzita_restoration_areas_ndmi_03mar2026_boundary`), provide a proxy measure of vegetation water stress and condition change that is ecologically meaningful for mangrove monitoring. Where NDMI values fall outside expected seasonal ranges, or where change detection algorithms identify canopy loss or encroachment within a protected or restoration zone, the system flags the relevant area for institutional review. MLNR and MESTI access EOSDA outputs through the platform's reporting interface, which aggregates satellite-derived change signals alongside field-based monitoring records. The intended monitoring cadence is weekly satellite imagery updates, with automated alert generation for detected changes intersecting ecologically sensitive areas.

The integration between EOSDA and the Supabase database ensures that satellite-derived signals are stored alongside field observations in a single data environment. This is operationally important: a satellite alert is most useful when it can be cross-referenced against the field monitoring record for the same location, the PSP re-measurement history for plots within the flagged area, and the governance record for the CREMA responsible for that landscape segment. The platform architecture is designed to enable this cross-referencing. Activating the EOSDA integration is the next implementation milestone required to make continuous satellite monitoring operational.

## 8.4 Year 0 Data Archiving

The 2025 national baseline constitutes Ghana's Year 0 blue carbon dataset: the anchor against which all future monitoring is benchmarked and all carbon stock changes are measured. This dataset includes the national mangrove extent mapping covering 30,025 hectares of coastal mangroves across 35 districts; the stratified carbon inventory results for above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (BGB), deadwood where relevant, and soil organic carbon (SOC) to 100 cm depth; georeferenced PSP locations and their initial measurement records; soil core sampling results from the field campaign; field data sheets from the monitoring station network at Keta and Ada; governance baseline records including CREMA readiness assessments, participation documentation, and community profiles for the 37 communities surveyed; and the geospatial boundary files that define restoration areas, protection zones, and PSP plot extents.

The integrity of this archive is not a technical nicety. It is a legal and scientific requirement. Carbon market standards — including the Voluntary Carbon Standard (Verra/VCS), Gold Standard, and Plan Vivo — require that baseline carbon stocks be documented with methodological transparency and that the data underpinning those stocks be preserved in a form that supports independent verification. Any future carbon credit issuance will be assessed against the Year 0 inventory. If the archive is degraded, overwritten without versioning, or insufficiently documented, the entire accounting chain from baseline to credit issuance is compromised.

The platform's data archiving architecture addresses these requirements through several mechanisms. All data entries are time-stamped at the point of submission and assigned unique record identifiers within the Supabase PostgreSQL database. Database schema changes are version-controlled through the Supabase migration system, ensuring that structural changes to the data environment are tracked and reversible. The Supabase Pro hosting environment provides automated database backups with point-in-time recovery, meaning that data can be restored to any earlier state if records are accidentally modified or corrupted. Role-based access control at the database level ensures that the Year 0 baseline layers — once deposited — can only be modified by authorised administrators, with any modification logged in the audit trail.

Beyond the platform itself, the Year 0 archiving strategy requires that the raw field data — physical data sheets, laboratory analysis reports, GPS coordinates from field measurements, and drone-linked spatial boundaries — are digitised, named consistently, and deposited in the project repository alongside the database records. The existing boundary files in the repository already demonstrate this practice: the NDMI-linked polygon files carry explicit date stamps and descriptive names that allow any user to identify what the file represents, when it was produced, and which monitoring unit it corresponds to. This naming discipline must be applied consistently to all Year 0 materials as they are finalised and deposited. Version management for the baseline geospatial layers — particularly the national mangrove extent classification — requires that the Year 0 raster and vector outputs are archived as read-only reference layers, with any updated classification products stored as versioned annual updates rather than overwrites of the original.

## 8.5 User Roles and Data Flows

The platform's user role structure maps directly to the four institutional tiers of the national MRV system. Each tier has a defined relationship to data: submitting it, validating it, reviewing it, or auditing it. The role architecture ensures that data flows upward through the institutional hierarchy with validation checks at each level, and that the integrity of the national dataset is protected against unvalidated or erroneous submissions.

At the community tier, CCAs and CREMA members submit field observation data through the platform's smartphone-compatible interface. Submissions include quarterly monitoring records from the 57 stations at the Keta Lagoon Complex and the 23 stations at Ada, covering the water quality and soil parameter readings specified in the monitoring plan: pH, temperature, conductivity, salinity, total dissolved solids, dissolved oxygen, and oxidation-reduction potential for water; and nitrate, phosphate, potassium, pH, moisture, humus content, and temperature for soil. CCAs and CREMA members also submit pressure surveillance observations — recorded cutting events, encroachment incidents, salt pan expansion observations, and livelihood-stress signals — through the platform's community reporting interface. At Dzita, where the Keta Lagoon early action hub is based, CCAs operating the 57-station monitoring network submit records on a quarterly cycle. At Pute in Songor and Sankor in Muni-Pomadze, CREMA members submit observations as the governance structures at those sites develop. The platform's community-level interface provides simplified map overlays of each CCA's or CREMA's designated monitoring zone, so that observations can be geographically anchored at the point of submission rather than requiring manual cross-referencing against maps at the validation stage.

At the district tier, Wildlife Division officers and Municipal and District Assembly (MMDA) officials review community-submitted records, validate observations against their own field knowledge, and flag anomalies for follow-up. Data submitted by CCAs enters a validation queue within the platform; district-level users have the editing and flagging rights necessary to mark records as validated, query them for clarification, or reject and return them for correction. This validation step ensures that measurement errors, transcription mistakes, and implausible values are caught before they propagate into national reporting. The Wildlife Division's district-level role is particularly important for pressure surveillance data, where distinguishing a genuine cutting event from a misidentified seasonal variation requires local ecological knowledge that CCAs in early stages of training may not yet possess.

At the national tier, MLNR and MESTI access national-level reporting dashboards and data summaries rather than individual field records. Their interface presents aggregated spatial views of mangrove extent and condition, carbon stock status, pressure trends by landscape, and governance indicator summaries. MLNR and MESTI can download audit-ready data exports for national climate reporting purposes, including Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) progress summaries and the Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report. Administrative functions — adding new users, managing institutional access levels, and approving data publications — are reserved for system administrators at MLNR in coordination with GAB Climate Smart.

At the independent review tier, accredited carbon market verifiers and external auditors access a restricted but complete export of audit-grade data: the full PSP measurement record with methodology documentation, the satellite-derived extent change record, the pressure surveillance log, and the governance participation archive. This access tier does not permit editing or real-time interaction with the live dataset; it provides read-only export access to verified, validated data packages prepared for each verification cycle, consistent with the data disclosure requirements of Verra/VCS, Gold Standard, and Plan Vivo verification procedures.

The data flow structure linking these tiers runs from community submission through district validation to national database integration and ultimately to platform-mediated annual reporting. All data updates are time-stamped and logged, and the audit trail linking any field observation to the national carbon stock estimate it informs is preserved in the database architecture from the point of original submission.

## 8.6 Reporting Outputs from the Platform

The platform produces four categories of reporting output, each serving a distinct institutional audience and operating on a different temporal cycle.

The Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report is the primary national disclosure instrument. It is produced by MLNR and MESTI on an annual cycle, drawing on the full dataset assembled in the platform over the preceding twelve months. The report presents the national mangrove extent change summary — derived from Sentinel-2 satellite analysis at 10–20 metre resolution — alongside the restoration performance assessment for active sites, the environmental pressure trend analysis across the three Ramsar landscapes, the governance and safeguard performance review, and the carbon risk assessment summary. The platform generates the underlying datasets and visualisations that populate each of these sections; the institutional authors at MLNR and MESTI compile the analytical narrative and approve the final report for publication. The Annual Report serves as the principal reference for government planning under Ghana's WACA ResIP 2 commitments (World Bank Project P175625), for donor review by the World Bank and development partners, and for private sector due diligence by investors and carbon market participants.

Quarterly pressure surveillance summaries are the platform's most frequent reporting output. They are generated from the rolling dataset of community-submitted pressure observations and district-validated records, and present a spatial summary of cutting incidence, encroachment signals, charcoal production activity, and conflict indicators across each landscape. These summaries are circulated to the Wildlife Division and MMDAs for operational response planning. At the Keta Lagoon Complex — where fuelwood harvesting by fish-smoking communities at Dzita, Salo, Galotse, and Agbatsivi represents the most persistent pressure on *Rhizophora racemosa* stands — quarterly summaries provide the early-warning signal that allows the Wildlife Division to mobilise CREMA mediation or enforcement response before pressure events escalate into measurable canopy loss.

Carbon stock update reports are generated on the PSP re-measurement cycle of every three to five years. The platform produces these reports by combining the current PSP measurement dataset against the Year 0 baseline record, calculating stock changes by carbon pool and by landscape stratum, and applying the uncertainty quantification framework established in the national carbon accounting protocol. The output supports carbon market verification by providing a documented, traceable record of stock changes from baseline to the current measurement date, prepared in a format compatible with Verra/VCS and related standard audit requirements.

Governance indicator dashboards provide MLNR and MESTI with a real-time view of CREMA composition statistics, gender and youth representation against the minimum 40% female leadership requirement, grievance redress performance metrics, and conflict mediation outcomes at each landscape. These dashboards draw directly from governance data submitted through the platform and provide the operational basis for the governance and safeguard performance chapter of the Annual MRV Report. They also provide the evidence base for adaptive management decisions — for example, identifying where female participation rates are falling below the approved minimum, or where grievance logs are showing unresolved complaints that require escalation.

## 8.7 Current Development Status and Roadmap

Understanding the current status of the platform requires distinguishing between three categories of work: infrastructure that is provisioned and operational, features that are under active development, and capabilities that are designed but not yet built.

At the infrastructure level, the platform is fully provisioned and operational. The Supabase project (`eavqytqxeaswfbytguxs`) is active and hosts the operational dataset for the National Framework. The Vercel hosting environment is live at `blue-carbon-mrv-green.vercel.app`, with a stable deployment pipeline from the GitHub repository at `GAB-Climate-Smart/blue-carbon-mrv`. The deployment pipeline is functional: code committed to the main branch triggers automated deployment through GitHub Actions, using the Supabase CLI for database migration management and the Vercel CLI for application deployment.

The AI_BLUEPRINT.md development guide further indicates that the platform is one of four active projects maintained by GAB Climate Smart within this technical environment, alongside the GAB OS company dashboard, the GAB Farms ERP, and the GAB Ramsar Map. The Blue Carbon MRV application is designed with the same architectural standards that govern these parallel projects: Next.js App Router, Supabase Pro with PostGIS, role-based authentication, and spatial map views through Mapbox GL JS. The consistency of technical approach across projects indicates that the team has accumulated operational experience with this stack.

What distinguishes the current deployment from earlier versions is the operational build status of its core application features. The platform now natively supports the project-centric architecture, including jurisdictional data submission interfaces, multi-tier validation workflows, and national reporting dashboards. The ingestion of real spatial data for the Keta Pilot — including the 20,717.95-hectare lagoon boundary and the Salo PSP 1 footprint — confirms that the system is now processing real-world evidence. The current version of the platform functions as an operational national framework, delivering the "pilot-story-line" required for the Final Report.

The EOSDA integration represents the most important near-term development milestone. The spatial boundary files required to initiate EOSDA monitoring are already produced and deposited in the repository. The next step — uploading these polygons to EOSDA, configuring recurring observation schedules, and linking EOSDA output feeds to the platform database — requires institutional action alongside technical build work. MLNR should treat EOSDA account configuration as a priority operational task, distinct from the ongoing application development, and should designate a named technical officer to manage the EOSDA account, approve uploaded boundaries, and oversee the first cycle of monitoring output review.

The medium-term development roadmap includes three further capability areas. The community data submission interface — designed to be smartphone-compatible for use by CCAs in the field at sites including the 57-station Keta network and the 23-station Ada network — requires usability testing with field staff before the quarterly reporting cycle can run entirely through the platform. The carbon stock update reporting module, which aggregates PSP re-measurement data against the Year 0 baseline, requires both the data entry interface for field measurement records and the calculation and export functions that generate the re-measurement report outputs. The governance dashboard, which draws on CREMA composition records and grievance logs, requires integration with the governance data submission workflow and the reporting templates approved by MLNR and MESTI.

Beyond these feature-level development priorities, the platform's long-term scalability depends on two institutional commitments. The first is the sustained investment by MLNR and MESTI in the data governance protocols — the user management, access review, backup verification, and data quality audit functions — that keep a living database authoritative over time. The second is the progressive expansion of the platform to cover additional blue carbon landscapes as the MRV system extends beyond the Keta pilot to Songor and Muni-Pomadze. The architecture is designed to accommodate this expansion: new PSP registries, new monitoring station networks, and new CREMA reporting units can be added to the existing database structure without requiring a rebuild. The foundational investment made at Keta — the Year 0 baseline, the 57-station monitoring network, the CREMA governance structure at Dzita — establishes the data model and institutional workflow that the expanded system will replicate.
