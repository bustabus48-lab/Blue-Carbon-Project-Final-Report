# Chapter 11: Keta Pilot Implementation

## 11.1 Why Keta Was Selected as the MRV Pilot Site

The decision to implement the National Blue Carbon MRV System first at the Keta Lagoon Complex — to concentrate the most detailed field infrastructure, the most operationally developed governance engagement, and the most granular monitoring protocols at this single site before extending equivalent depth to Songor and Muni-Pomadze — rests on three converging justifications, each of which independently would be sufficient to make Keta the logical starting point, and whose combination makes it the only defensible one.

The first justification is the scale of national carbon significance concentrated in this single landscape. The 2025 geospatial assessment established that the Keta Lagoon Complex holds 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves — approximately 69% of Ghana's entire coastal mangrove resource of 30,025 hectares. This is not a statistical concentration of modest importance; it means that the fate of Keta's mangroves is, in functional terms, the fate of Ghana's blue carbon strategy. Any significant undetected deforestation event at Keta — whether a rapid intensification of fuelwood harvesting during a poor fishing season, a breach of the coastal sandspit allowing saltwater inundation of intertidal mangrove stands, or an unmonitored land conversion along the lagoon margin — constitutes a national-scale carbon loss event. The 2.15% per year historical deforestation rate documented for the site, if undetected and uninterrupted, would reduce the Keta mangrove estate at a rate that no restoration programme at Songor or Muni-Pomadze could compensate for within any realistic carbon accounting period. The first obligation of a national MRV system is to monitor the national carbon asset with the greatest first — and at Keta, that obligation is unambiguous.

The second justification is the depth and completeness of the baseline package established at Keta. A Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system cannot operate against a blank slate; it requires a Year 0 reference against which subsequent measurements are benchmarked, a socio-economic profile of the communities whose behaviour shapes ecosystem outcomes, a governance readiness assessment that identifies the institutional pathway to sustainable co-management, and an understanding of the environmental pressures the monitoring system must be designed to detect. At Keta, and only at Keta within the three-site national programme, all six baseline workstreams — geospatial extent mapping, carbon inventory, ecosystem health assessment, environmental pressure profiling, governance readiness evaluation, and socio-economic community profiling — have been completed to the depth required for carbon market compliance. The carbon inventory has been conducted. The Permanent Sample Plot has been established at Salo. The participatory socio-economic profiling has been completed across communities including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, Salo, Dzita, Sota, and Galo. The geo-social analysis has spatially correlated the 20,717.95-hectare mangrove estate with the livelihood dependencies and pressure geographies documented in field assessments. No other site in the national programme is at this level of baseline completeness.

The third justification is the operational infrastructure already in place. The 57-station wetland monitoring network described in Chapter 7, distributed across the Keta Lagoon and its associated channels and intertidal zones to capture water quality and soil chemistry dynamics at quarterly resolution, represents a significant field investment that is unique to Keta within the national programme. Ada's 23-station network demonstrates equivalent technical design but covers a smaller and ecologically less nationally critical landscape. The Salo Permanent Sample Plot provides the biomass and soil carbon measurement anchor from which carbon stock changes will be tracked across the re-measurement cycle. As of March 2026, the Keta pilot has moved from an infrastructure plan to an active digital project. The real spatial boundaries for the lagoon and the Dzita restoration zones, alongside the Salo PSP 1 footprint, are now ingested and live within the production MRV platform. The MRV system at Keta is no longer a design framework awaiting implementation; it is a functioning operational infrastructure populated with real-world evidence.

## 11.2 The Landscape and Its Pressures

The Keta Lagoon Complex is the defining feature of the Volta Region's coastal geography: a shallow, brackish, macro-tidal lagoon separated from the Atlantic Ocean by a narrow sandspit and connected to the lower Volta River through a network of channels, creeks, and intertidal flats. The mangrove forests that fringe this system — 20,717.95 hectares in total, dominated by *Rhizophora racemosa* (Red Mangrove) and *Avicennia germinans* (Black Mangrove) — are the largest continuous coastal mangrove stand in Ghana, and among the most ecologically productive blue carbon ecosystems on the West African coastline. Their position at the interface of the freshwater Volta River system and the saline Atlantic creates the hydrological gradient conditions that support dense, structurally complex forest capable of accumulating significant below-ground and soil organic carbon stocks over decadal timescales.

This ecological richness has historically supported, and continues to support, the economic fabric of the communities distributed around the lagoon's margins. Fishing is the primary occupation of men in settlements such as Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, Salo, Dzita, Sota, and Galo. The post-harvest processing industry — dominated by women who smoke and sell fish for local and regional markets — is the economic axis around which female livelihoods in these communities are organised. The link between this industry and the mangrove ecosystem is direct and consequential: the fish-smoking kilns that operate continuously at all major lakeside settlements use mangrove wood as their primary fuel, preferring *Rhizophora racemosa* for its high energy density and sustained burning characteristics. Field assessments conducted in August 2025 confirmed that approximately 70% of households in the Keta study area rely on mangrove-linked activities for their primary income or subsistence — a dependency figure that, when mapped against the density of the fish-smoking industry in settlements like Agbatsivi and Bomigo, reveals that the extraction pressure on the mangrove estate is not incidental but is the direct product of the economic system the estate itself has made possible.

The resulting degradation dynamic has been characterised as a "death by a thousand cuts" — a phrase that captures precisely the challenge this landscape presents to an MRV system. There is no single actor responsible for the decline. There is no industrial permit holder whose operations can be regulated to halt deforestation. There is instead a continuous, distributed pressure driven by thousands of individual livelihoods choices, each rational and survival-driven in isolation, collectively producing a historical deforestation rate of 2.15% per year. Approximately 70% of the natural mangrove cover has been degraded or fragmented, with particularly severe losses documented near Bomigo, Galo, and Sota since 2000. The GIS analysis confirms that the most accessible mangrove stands — those within practical fuelwood collection distance of the largest settlements — show the greatest fragmentation and canopy loss. This spatial gradient is itself an important input to the monitoring system's design: it predicts where the next cutting pressure will concentrate as accessible stands are exhausted and harvesters are forced to travel further into the lagoon system.

Compounding these direct livelihood pressures is a systemic, landscape-level threat that no community action can address and that the MRV system must account for in its risk framing. The construction of the Akosombo Dam in 1964, 60 kilometres upstream on the Volta River, permanently altered the sediment dynamics of the Volta Delta. The dam traps more than 90% of the river's natural sediment load, eliminating the annual replenishment of sand and silt that historically maintained the sandspit separating the lagoon from the Atlantic Ocean. The consequence, now visible and measured in the coastal retreat documented by communities and confirmed in satellite analysis, is the progressive erosion of the sandspit itself. As the barrier between the lagoon and the ocean thins and recedes, the risk of storm-driven breaches increases, and with it the risk of catastrophic saltwater inundation events that could permanently alter the hydrological conditions supporting the mangrove forests. The MRV system at Keta operates within this geomorphologically dynamic landscape: its monitoring infrastructure must be designed not only to detect anthropogenic degradation signals but to flag landscape-level instability that no CREMA governance mechanism can manage alone, and that the institutional tier architecture must be prepared to escalate to national and international levels when its signals indicate threshold-level risk.

## 11.3 The 57-Station Monitoring Network

The wetland ecosystem monitoring network established at Keta consists of 57 sampling stations, distributed across the lagoon system using a supervised geographic information system (GIS) map developed during the baseline assessment phase. The spatial logic of the station network is not uniform coverage — a 57-station grid imposed across 20,717.95 hectares would produce one station per 364 hectares and provide inadequate resolution for detecting localised pressure events or hydrological gradients. Instead, the stations are distributed to reflect the ecological heterogeneity of the lagoon system: stations are concentrated in habitat types and hydrological zones where change signals are most ecologically informative, including the main lagoon margin, the creek and channel fringing zones where fuelwood pressure is most intense, the intertidal transition zones most susceptible to salinity fluctuation associated with reduced Volta freshwater inflow, and the areas immediately adjacent to the most active fishing and fish-processing settlements.

This spatial logic ensures that the network captures the key ecological gradients — the salinity gradient between freshwater-influenced lagoon reaches and the more saline zones near the sandspit, the trophic gradient between nutrient-rich zones near community waste discharge points and the more oligotrophic outer lagoon — that are diagnostic of ecosystem condition change. A station network that sampled only the easiest-to-access lagoon margins would produce a monitoring record biased toward the most pressured areas; the current distribution ensures that reference zones and pressure zones are both represented within the quarterly dataset, enabling the comparative analysis that makes pressure detection meaningful.

The quarterly monitoring schedule, administered by trained Community Change Agents (CCAs) operating from communities near their designated stations, produces four complete data cycles per year. At each station, CCAs record a standardised set of water quality parameters — pH, temperature, conductivity, salinity, total dissolved solids (TDS), dissolved oxygen, oxidation-reduction potential (ORP), and resistivity — using calibrated multi-parameter field instruments. The same station visit records soil chemistry parameters — nitrate, phosphate, potassium, pH, moisture content, humus content, and soil temperature — using soil analysis instruments introduced during the December 2025 field training programme. The monitoring plan specifies that data sheets are completed with the station name, GPS coordinates, date, time, and name of the recording CCA, ensuring that each data record is fully traceable to its source and collection context.

The data flow from field collection to national platform passes through a structured validation pathway. Field data sheets are submitted to the Wildlife Division at the district level, where technical officers review records for internal consistency — flagging readings that fall outside plausible ranges for the station's position in the tidal gradient, cross-checking against the previous quarter's baseline at the same station, and initiating field verification requests for anomalous readings before accepting them into the consolidated district dataset. Validated data is then transmitted to the national digital platform described in Chapter 8, where it is archived under the station's unique identifier and made available for the trend analysis and spatial visualisation functions that the platform hosts. Any drastic or unusual changes in water quality — a sudden drop in dissolved oxygen indicating an organic pollution event, an unexpected salinity spike in a freshwater-influenced station that might indicate a hydrological connectivity change — are flagged by CCAs for immediate reporting to the district Wildlife Division rather than being held until the next quarterly submission, maintaining the network's early-warning function for high-consequence environmental events.

The importance of this monitoring infrastructure for the national MRV system cannot be overstated. The 57-station network at Keta, producing four quarterly datasets per year across water and soil parameters, creates the most detailed, temporally resolved picture of lagoon ecosystem condition in the national programme. It enables the detection of the slow, systemic changes — gradual salinisation, progressive decline in dissolved oxygen levels associated with increased organic loading from urban runoff, incremental changes in soil nutrient chemistry that precede visible vegetation stress — that no satellite-based monitoring system can detect at the temporal and spatial resolution required. The field network and the satellite monitoring layer are complementary systems: the satellite provides the broad spatial coverage and the capacity to detect land use change across the full 20,717.95-hectare estate; the field network provides the in-situ ecological condition data that gives meaning to the spatial changes the satellite detects.

## 11.4 Permanent Sample Plots

The Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) established at Salo represents the carbon measurement anchor for the Keta Lagoon Complex. Salo's selection as the PSP location is consistent with the MRV system's broader station network logic: it places the primary carbon measurement infrastructure in a community context where ecological conditions are representative of the pressured but still-functioning mangrove fringe, where access is practical for the periodic re-measurement teams that will conduct biomass assessments every three to five years, and where the CCA training programme has established a local monitoring capacity capable of supporting the plot maintenance responsibilities that lie between full re-measurement cycles. In the production MRV platform, the **Salo PSP 1 Footprint** is now a live spatial entity, enabling georeferenced tracking of biomass trends and soil carbon pools directly from the field data ledger.

The baseline carbon inventory conducted at Salo PSP 1 established the Year 0 measurements that anchor the entire carbon accounting protocol at Keta. The plot records include a complete species composition baseline — the proportional presence of *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* and any associated species within the plot boundary — providing the species stratification layer that determines which allometric equations are applied in biomass calculations. Diameter at breast height (DBH) measurements were recorded for all trees meeting the minimum diameter inclusion threshold, providing the primary input variable for above-ground biomass (AGB) estimation. Height measurements were taken for a representative sample of trees to parameterise the height-diameter relationship applied in the allometric model. Canopy cover was assessed as a structural indicator of plot density and closure, providing a contextual reference against which subsequent monitoring visits can detect canopy change even in the interval between full re-measurement cycles.

Below the ground surface, the Salo PSP baseline includes soil organic carbon (SOC) measurements taken from cores to 100 centimetres depth, as required by the IPCC Wetlands Supplement methodology that governs the national carbon accounting protocol described in Chapter 9. The soil cores provide the baseline SOC stock estimate for the plot's soil profile, establishing the most important single carbon pool in a mangrove system — one that typically exceeds above-ground biomass as a carbon store and that is disproportionately vulnerable to disturbance events such as drainage, substrate compaction, or the exposure of deep anaerobic layers to oxidation. The soil carbon baseline is archived in the national database under version-controlled records that ensure it is fully reproducible and auditable across the measurement cycle.

The PSP re-measurement schedule at Salo, consistent with the national standard of every three to five years, calibrates the carbon accounting protocol with actual field-measured biomass and soil carbon data. Between full re-measurements, the PSP is not abandoned to the elements; CCA-supported interim monitoring maintains the plot boundary markers, documents any disturbance events — evidence of cutting, grazing, or physical damage to plot trees — and records qualitative canopy condition observations that provide an early signal if the plot's carbon stock trajectory is being disrupted between measurement cycles. Where interim monitoring detects a significant disturbance event, the national MRV protocol provides for an accelerated re-measurement outside the standard cycle, ensuring that the carbon accounting record accurately reflects actual stock conditions rather than interpolating across a disturbance event that has altered the carbon trajectory.

The Salo PSP feeds the carbon accounting protocol described in Chapter 9 through a specific data pathway. Re-measured DBH and height data from Salo are applied to the validated allometric equations for the species composition recorded in the baseline, producing updated AGB estimates for the plot. Below-ground biomass (BGB) is estimated using the standard root-to-shoot ratio applied for *Rhizophora* and *Avicennia* species under IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance. Deadwood assessments, conducted where relevant at the plot boundary, contribute to the deadwood carbon pool estimate. Soil re-sampling at the same core locations used in the baseline produces the updated SOC estimate that, combined with the biomass measurements, allows calculation of the total carbon stock change across the measurement interval. This measured stock change is the evidence base from which any avoided-deforestation or sequestration benefit reported for the Keta project area is derived — making the integrity of the Salo PSP data directly determinative of the credibility of any carbon credit claims generated under the national programme.

## 11.5 The Dzita Early Action Hub

Dzita occupies a specific position within the social geography of the Keta Lagoon Complex. The community sits on the coastal barrier spit — the narrow strip of land separating the lagoon from the Atlantic Ocean whose stability is compromised by the sediment starvation effects of the Akosombo Dam — in direct adjacency to mangrove resources that are both heavily pressured and ecologically significant. This is not a peripheral location in the social landscape of the project. Dzita has been identified across multiple phases of the baseline assessment as a community with exceptional social readiness characteristics: active local groups, documented history of engagement with conservation non-governmental organisations, and internal social structures — including indigenous conservation practices such as the weekly no-fishing day observed on Tuesdays — that demonstrate a cultural predisposition toward collective resource governance. The weekly fishing suspension, maintained through community social norms rather than formal legal enforcement, is the kind of existing customary rule that the CREMA development process is designed to formalise and strengthen within a statutory framework, rather than replace with an externally imposed system from the outset.

The designation of Dzita as an early action hub carries operational meaning that distinguishes it from the general description of community engagement activities. Dzita is the community-level focal point for three convergent functions that together constitute the practical leading edge of MRV system implementation at Keta. First, it is the focal point for CREMA formation at the Keta Lagoon Complex. The baseline governance assessment characterises CREMA development at Keta as fragmented across the landscape — the distributed communities along the lagoon margin have not yet consolidated into the unified governance structure that a single, lagoon-scale CREMA would require — and Dzita represents the most advanced node within that fragmented landscape in terms of social readiness for formalisation. The CREMA formation process at Dzita involves the constitution of a Community Resource Management Committee (CRMC) with the representative membership — and the minimum 40% female executive leadership — required by the national CREMA governance standard, the development of CREMA bylaws that incorporate both statutory requirements and the customary rules already operating in the community, and the submission of the CREMA registration documentation to the Wildlife Division for processing under the Wildlife Resources Management Act.

Second, Dzita is the focal point for initial restoration activities at the Keta pilot site. The community's location on the barrier spit places it adjacent to areas where mangrove loss to fuelwood harvesting and storm damage has left degraded or open substrate zones where restoration interventions can begin under CCA supervision. These initial restoration activities serve two simultaneous functions: they are substantive ecological investments in the recovery of degraded mangrove fringe, and they are the practical learning laboratories through which CCA training in restoration monitoring protocols is applied to real planting and natural regeneration sites. CCAs who conduct the seedling survival surveys and canopy cover assessments at Dzita restoration plots are building the monitoring competency that will be required as restoration activities expand across the lagoon landscape.

Third, Dzita is the community-level data collection node for the monitoring network's social and governance indicators — the Layer III Governance and Safeguard Assurance data that the quarterly reporting cycle requires from each active community monitoring site. CREMA meeting attendance, grievance submissions, benefit-sharing distribution confirmations, and gender and youth composition records for governance bodies are all generated at the community level by CCA monitoring activities, with Dzita serving as the reference community whose governance monitoring data the Wildlife Division will use to calibrate the emerging CREMA system's performance. The **Dzita Restoration Areas** (e.g., Dzita North, South, and West) are now commission with live spatial boundaries in the MRV system, providing a digital audit trail for assisted natural regeneration and replanting efforts documented during the 2025-2026 field campaigns.

The next governance milestones at Dzita require both institutional action within the community and coordination with the district Wildlife Division. The priority action is the formal constitution and registration of the Dzita CRMC, establishing the legal basis for CREMA status and the governance framework within which restoration activities, resource use rules, and benefit-sharing mechanisms will be managed. Once the CRMC is registered, the CREMA boundary demarcation process can proceed, formally establishing the geographic scope of the community's management authority and the spatial extent of the conservation and restoration zone it will steward. The CREMA bylaw development process will then formalise the resource use regulations — including the no-fishing day and any seasonal harvesting restrictions — as legally enforceable rules rather than purely customary conventions. These governance milestones at Dzita are not simply administrative steps; they are the institutional architecture that makes the community's role in the national MRV system legally durable and operationally authoritative.

## 11.6 Restoration Monitoring at Keta

Restoration at the Keta Lagoon Complex begins with an honest assessment of the ecological conditions that determine where active intervention is viable and where the ecosystem retains sufficient structural capacity to recover without full replanting. The preferred approach at Keta is assisted natural regeneration — supporting the re-establishment of mangrove cover in areas where the hydrological conditions, soil chemistry, and proximity to propagule sources indicate that recovery is ecologically feasible with reduced disturbance pressure and, where necessary, supplementary planting to accelerate canopy closure. This preference is not a cost-saving expedient; it is ecologically appropriate. Areas where the freshwater-saline gradient remains functional, where substrate quality has not been severely altered by extended deforestation, and where *Rhizophora racemosa* propagule dispersal pathways from adjacent intact forest are active are the conditions in which assisted natural regeneration consistently outperforms replanting in terms of long-term survival, structural complexity, and carbon accumulation rates. In contrast, areas where hydrological conditions have been significantly altered — by the redirection of drainage channels, the compaction of substrate through repeated human access, or prolonged exposure of intertidal surfaces to direct solar radiation following canopy loss — may require active site preparation and direct planting before natural regeneration can establish.

All restoration sites within the Keta pilot area are georeferenced, assigned unique site identifiers, and registered in the national web-based platform described in Chapter 8, linking each site to the CREMA responsible for its management and to the district Wildlife Division authority overseeing performance. This registration establishes the spatial record against which monitoring visits are validated — a survival survey that cannot be geographically linked to a registered restoration polygon is not an auditable monitoring record — and enables the platform's restoration tracking overlay to visualise the spatial expansion of the restoration programme across the lagoon system over time.

The restoration monitoring protocol at Keta follows the national schedule established in the MRV framework: initial survival and establishment assessments at three months post-planting, six months, and twelve months from establishment, transitioning to annual monitoring thereafter until the site has achieved confirmed establishment status. At each monitoring visit, the protocol records seedling survival rate as a percentage of planted or naturally recruited stems within the plot boundary, canopy development progression assessed through canopy cover measurement and qualitative height class recording, evidence of disturbance — fresh cutting, grazing by livestock, trampling through foot traffic — that might threaten survival rates, and confirmation of hydrological suitability, assessed through direct observation of tidal inundation patterns and any evidence of waterlogging or drought stress in the established plants. The minimum performance threshold for Keta restoration sites is a survival rate of at least 70% after Year 1; sites falling below this threshold trigger a corrective restoration review that assesses whether replanting is required, whether site preparation was adequate, or whether the ecological site classification needs to be reconsidered. Sites meeting or exceeding the threshold are maintained under the annual monitoring schedule until they reach the canopy closure stage at which satellite-based monitoring can confirm their contribution to the annual extent change record.

The question of when restored areas enter the carbon stock calculation is consequential for carbon accounting integrity and is addressed directly in the protocol. Restored areas do not enter the carbon stock calculation on the basis of planting activity alone; they enter when they have achieved confirmed establishment — defined as completion of the first twelve-month monitoring cycle with a survival rate above the 70% threshold and evidence of sustained canopy development — and when the site has been registered in the national platform with a complete monitoring record for the establishment period. For the purposes of the carbon accounting protocol described in Chapter 9, restored areas are initially classified as contributing to the restoration performance record, providing evidence of the ecological trajectory without being credited as confirmed carbon removals until the next five-year PSP re-measurement cycle incorporates the restored areas into the biomass assessment. This conservative approach to crediting restored areas is consistent with the permanence risk management requirements of international carbon market standards and ensures that the carbon accounting record does not overstate removals attributable to restoration activities whose long-term establishment is not yet confirmed.

The ecological context for restoration at Keta makes the assisted natural regeneration approach particularly important in areas adjacent to the coastal barrier spit. The geomorphological instability of the spit, driven by the ongoing sediment starvation of the Volta Delta following the Akosombo Dam construction, means that restoration sites in its immediate vicinity face a structural landscape risk that soil chemistry and hydrology cannot compensate for. Restoration planning at Keta therefore incorporates a spatial risk assessment — identifying the zones where the spit's retreat trajectory creates an unacceptable exposure to future storm inundation or lagoon-ocean connectivity breach — and prioritises restoration activities in the more stable inner lagoon zones where the risk of substrate loss is lower, and where mangrove establishment, once confirmed, is more likely to persist across the carbon accounting period.

## 11.7 Lessons from Keta for National Rollout

The Keta pilot has not only advanced the implementation of Ghana's national MRV system at the country's most critical blue carbon site; it has generated a body of operational experience, design validation, and implementation learning that directly informs the pathway to equivalent MRV maturity at the Songor Ramsar Site and the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site. Four dimensions of this learning are of particular significance for the national rollout.

The station network design logic — 57 stations distributed by habitat type, pressure zone, and hydrological context rather than by uniform spatial coverage — represents a design principle whose application at Songor and Muni-Pomadze will require site-specific adaptation but follows a replicable logic. At Songor, where 465.76 hectares of fragmented mangrove is distributed across a landscape dominated by salt pan operations and subject to the land-use pressures associated with Elavanyo and Obane, the network design challenge is different from Keta's: fewer stations are needed for a smaller mangrove estate, but the network must be specifically designed to detect the salinity intrusion and hydrological alteration effects that salt mining operations produce, in addition to the conventional fuelwood and land conversion signals. Ada's 23-station network — covering a Ramsar site that is ecologically smaller and less nationally strategic than Keta but more complex in its salt mining exposure than Keta's direct pressure profile — provides a transitional reference for this design logic. At Muni-Pomadze, where mangrove cover is ecologically insignificant and the monitoring priority is restoration performance rather than conservation of existing stock, the network design must be oriented toward tracking seedling establishment and canopy development rather than the ecosystem health and hydrological dynamics that justify the large Keta network. The design principle — match station deployment to the ecological questions the network must answer for that site — is portable; the station count and parameter priorities are not.

The CREMA governance approach validated at Dzita carries lessons of immediate relevance for CREMA development at Pute in Songor and Sankor in Muni-Pomadze. The experience at Dzita demonstrates that the most productive entry point for CREMA formation is not the formal constitutional process but the substantive activity that gives the CREMA its operational meaning. Communities engage more effectively with governance formation when it is tied to concrete, locally relevant management activities — restoration planting, CCA monitoring, the formalisation of existing customary rules they already practice — than when it is presented as an administrative process leading to a legal designation. At Pute, where the CREMA formation context is the contested landscape of salt mining conflicts and land tenure disputes that characterise the Songor Ramsar Site, the governance approach will require a more intensive conflict mediation component before CREMA boundary demarcation can proceed — a precondition that Dzita's more favourable starting conditions did not require. At Sankor, where community cohesion is high, women's groups are active, and A Rocha Ghana's school-based environmental clubs have established conservation engagement among younger community members, the CREMA formation pathway may progress more rapidly than at either Keta or Songor, notwithstanding the less nationally significant ecological starting point.

The PSP establishment protocol validated at Salo provides a replicable technical template for the PSP work that will be required at Songor and Muni-Pomadze before those sites can be brought to full carbon accounting readiness. The Salo protocol establishes the minimum data set for Year 0 PSP baseline — species composition, DBH distribution, height sampling, canopy cover, and SOC cores to 100 centimetres depth — and the archive and versioning requirements that ensure the PSP data retains its scientific defensibility across re-measurement cycles. At Songor, where the limited 465.76-hectare mangrove estate may require a different PSP spatial design to achieve adequate statistical representation across the fragmented stand structure, the Salo methodology will need to be adapted rather than directly replicated — but the data standards, the archiving requirements, and the allometric and soil sampling protocols are fully transferable. At Muni-Pomadze, where the PSP system's primary function in the near term will be monitoring restoration performance rather than documenting existing stock changes, the PSP design will need to be appropriate for the early-stage establishment conditions that characterise a restoration landscape.

The data validation workflow — the pathway from community CCA field return, through Wildlife Division consistency checking and cross-validation against satellite analysis, to national platform archiving — is the governance mechanism that maintains the integrity of the monitoring record across the institutional tiers. The Keta experience has validated this workflow under operational conditions: CCAs trained in the December 2025 field programme are submitting standardised data sheets; Wildlife Division technical officers at the district level are conducting the first-line validation; and the data flow to the national platform is following the protocol described in the monitoring plan. The conditions required at Songor and Muni-Pomadze before equivalent data quality can be expected include completed CCA training programmes at Pute and Sankor, the establishment of functioning Wildlife Division data reception procedures at the relevant district offices, and the integration of those district offices into the national platform's data upload and archiving systems. The institutional preconditions are not technical obstacles; they are governance readiness requirements that each site must satisfy before the data validation workflow can produce monitoring records of the quality that the national MRV system depends on.

The broader lesson from the Keta pilot is that MRV system maturity is not achieved by designing a framework; it is achieved by operating one, iterating on what the operational experience reveals, and building the institutional habits — in communities, in district offices, and in national agencies — that sustain monitoring quality when the initial programme investment has moved to subsequent phases. Keta has established those institutional habits at a level of operational depth that Songor and Muni-Pomadze have not yet reached. The pathway to national MRV maturity is therefore, in practical terms, the pathway from the Keta pilot's demonstrated operational depth to equivalent depth at each remaining site — an investment in institutional capacity as much as in technical infrastructure, and one that the Keta experience has now provided a validated and replicable template for.
