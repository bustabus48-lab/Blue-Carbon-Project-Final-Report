## 1. Executive Summary
This report provides the current standalone carbon inventory draft for the Ghana Blue Carbon reporting package. It is designed to function as an extraction-ready markdown source report while preserving a strict distinction between completed evidence, pending inputs, and later verification requirements.

The repository evidence already supports the carbon inventory design logic. The Terms of Reference require a national blue carbon inventory built from well-defined boundaries, ecological stratification, explicit carbon-pool selection, plot design, and an agreed measurement frequency. The MRV draft further confirms that the inventory is expected to cover above-ground biomass, below-ground biomass, deadwood where relevant, and soil organic carbon to 0-100 cm depth through georeferenced Permanent Sample Plots (PSPs), QA/QC procedures, and controlled archiving.

At the same time, the currently uploaded carbon source file does not yet provide the populated carbon stock tables that would be needed to report verified baseline estimates by site or stratum. It refers to four soil depth profiles, five sample trees for above-ground biomass entry, and a baseline deforestation rate of 2.15% per year for additionality framing in the Keta Lagoon basin, but the referenced soil and tree tables are not yet populated in the repository. The safest reporting position is therefore that the carbon inventory design basis is clear, some pilot-linked data-entry cues are present, and the verification-ready carbon evidence package remains incomplete.

## 2. Purpose and Scope of This Standalone Report
The purpose of this standalone report is to:
- summarize the carbon inventory design and reporting logic already evidenced in the repository;
- set out the minimum replication-and-verification chain that the carbon workstream is expected to follow;
- capture the currently available carbon-related inputs without overstating them as finalized stock estimates; and
- provide a markdown source report that can be used in extraction, synthesis, and later final-report authoring.

This report is not presented as a completed national carbon inventory with fully verified stock totals. It is an evidence-bounded drafting report for the Ministry of Environment, Science, and Technology and related national stakeholders, prepared so the wider reporting package can proceed without inventing results that are not yet documented.

## 3. Evidence Base and Reporting Logic
The current repository evidence supports five core propositions.

1. The Terms of Reference require a blue carbon inventory for the identified and mapped ecosystems of Ghana, beginning with well-documented boundaries and ecological stratification.
2. The Terms of Reference also require explicit selection of carbon pools, plot design, and measurement frequency before reporting can proceed.
3. The MRV framework confirms that the long-term carbon system is expected to operate through georeferenced PSPs, approved allometric calculations, soil laboratory analysis, QA/QC, and controlled digital archiving.
4. The final-report blueprint expects the carbon inventory report to provide carbon pools assessed, baseline carbon stock estimates by site or stratum, differences across sites or ecosystem types, and implications for blue carbon project potential.
5. The currently uploaded carbon source file provides partial pilot-linked input cues, but not the completed tables needed for final baseline reporting.

Taken together, these sources justify a report that is strong on method chain, replication logic, and evidence boundaries, but still cautious on final stock values and verification claims.

## 4. Carbon Inventory Design Basis
The Terms of Reference set out the minimum sequence for the carbon inventory.

### 4.1 Boundary definition
Defined boundaries must be documented in line with the land-cover maps because those boundaries become the baseline for estimating total carbon stock or carbon stock within a specified area. This requirement matters for verification because later stock calculations, remeasurement, and avoided-loss claims cannot be reproduced if the inventory boundary is unclear.

### 4.2 Stratification
The Terms of Reference require heterogeneous sites to be divided into smaller, more homogeneous strata according to ecosystem conditions and the purpose of the assessment. The stated stratification drivers include existing and potential land use, soil characteristics, geomorphology, drainage, and proximity to the ocean. This is the basis for representative sampling and landscape-scale extrapolation.

### 4.3 Carbon pools
The required pools cover the main components expected for mangrove and salt marsh accounting. The Terms of Reference and MRV draft together support reporting on:
- above-ground living biomass;
- below-ground living biomass;
- above-ground dead biomass or deadwood where relevant; and
- soil carbon, including the 0-100 cm soil organic carbon reference used in the MRV framework.

### 4.4 Plot design and measurement frequency
The Terms of Reference require the firm to determine plot type, number, and location, with agreement from the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources and the Ministry of Environment, Science, and Technology through progress meetings. The MRV draft then adds an operating expectation that biomass should be remeasured every 3-5 years, with earlier reassessment where disturbance, restoration expansion, or market requirements justify it.

## 6. Permanent Sample Plot, Soil, and Biomass Logic
The MRV draft provides the clearest reporting-safe description of how the carbon inventory is intended to function after baseline setup.

### 6.1 Permanent Sample Plot system
Permanent Sample Plots are intended to serve as the long-term monitoring backbone. The MRV draft states that each PSP should be georeferenced, assigned a unique identification code, stratified, digitally archived, and hosted on the national web platform. Community-level actors can support plot access maintenance, Diameter at Breast Height (DBH) recording, and visual disturbance documentation, while national technical agencies are responsible for recalculation, soil remeasurement, QA/QC, and archiving.

### 6.2 Soil and laboratory pathway
The evidence supports a minimum soil pathway in which soil organic carbon is tracked to 0-100 cm depth, soil core data are linked to PSP identifiers and strata, laboratory analysis is documented, and records are retained for controlled archiving and later remeasurement. The repository does not yet contain the final soil tables or laboratory sheets, so this pathway should be described as designed and partly staged rather than fully evidenced.

### 6.3 Biomass pathway
The MRV draft states that national technical agencies should apply approved allometric equations to biomass measurements. This is important because the same field measurements could otherwise generate inconsistent stock estimates if different equations, conversion factors, or undocumented assumptions were used. The repository presently confirms the need for the equation pathway, but it does not yet provide the final equation register or calculation sheets for annex use.

## 7. Additionality and Baseline-Pressure Context
The currently available evidence allows a cautious baseline-pressure reading, especially for Keta-linked contexts.

The carbon source file records a baseline deforestation input rate of 2.15% per year and attributes the pressure principally to Rhizophora harvesting for commercial fish smoking. The cookstove baseline report separately supports the wider energy-pressure context by documenting continued dependence on biomass fuels and the strong role of mangrove wood in fish smoking economies. The geo-social baseline report further reinforces that additionality in blue carbon interventions depends on a credible business-as-usual baseline for both ecological condition and community pressure.

This does not amount to a completed avoided-emissions calculation. It does, however, support a reporting-safe statement that additionality logic is expected to be grounded in both mapped ecosystem baselines and documented pressure dynamics, rather than assumed qualitatively.

## 8. Replication, Verification, and Auditability
To align this report with the National MRV and Drone Survey / EOSDA standalone reports, the carbon inventory should be read through a common replication-and-verification chain.

1. **Boundary, stratum, plot, and sample identifiers are defined against approved methods.**  
   This includes documented boundaries, ecological stratification, PSP identifiers, soil-depth intervals, biomass measurements, and any field metadata needed to reproduce the observation.

2. **Technical calculations and laboratory outputs are validated before reporting.**  
   The MRV design expects approved allometric equations, soil laboratory analysis, QA/QC checks, outlier review, and methodological consistency with the baseline inventory.

3. **Records are archived in a traceable digital pathway.**  
   The evidence supports controlled storage of PSP records, soil and biomass evidence, recalculation files, and version history so later reviewers can inspect the chain of evidence.

4. **Verification claims are only made when the supporting tables and registers exist in the repository.**  
   Final stock tables, equation references, uncertainty notes, indicator lineage, and protocol-register links should be added before the report is described as verification-ready.

This logic is central to the report package because carbon significance is only credible if another technical team can inspect the same identifiers, methods, and calculations and reproduce the result or document a justified exception.

## 9. Relationship to the MRV and Drone / EOSDA Workstreams
The carbon inventory does not sit outside the other monitoring workstreams. It links directly to them.

| Linked workstream | Carbon-inventory contribution | Reporting-safe implication |
|---|---|---|
| National MRV framework | Provides carbon pools, PSP logic, QA/QC, remeasurement cycles, and reporting architecture | Carbon findings should feed the national system rather than stand alone as a disconnected annex |
| Drone Survey / EOSDA workstream | Can help confirm plot location, site boundary interpretation, disturbance context, and spatial change around inventory areas | Drone and EOSDA evidence strengthens verification only when mapped outputs and upload proof are added |
| Geospatial baseline | Provides boundaries and strata for representative sampling and extrapolation | Carbon claims should remain tied to mapped baseline units |
| Socio-economic and pressure evidence | Supports additionality and risk interpretation | Carbon potential should be read together with pressure and livelihood drivers |

This integrated reading is especially important for Keta. Keta can be described as a pilot implementation context with stronger linked evidence than some other landscapes, but it should not be used as shorthand for national completion.
