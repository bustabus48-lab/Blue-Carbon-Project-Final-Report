# Chapter 8. Drone Survey and Continuous Monitoring

**Draft status:** partial draft  
**Primary evidence files used:** `evidence/drone_monitoring.md`; `annex/figure_register.csv`; `annex/missing_visuals.md`; `qa/chapter_traceability_map.md`  
**Major known gaps:** Drone orthomosaics, mission logs, and finalized survey visuals are not yet packaged as report-ready assets.

## 8.1 Chapter Purpose
This chapter explains how available drone-linked spatial assets and monitoring design elements support the transition from one-time baseline production to repeatable observation workflows.

The same evidence base can be reused if a standalone Drone Survey / EOSDA monitoring report is required.

## 8.2 Current Evidence on Drone-Linked Spatial Assets
Current evidence supports the existence of dated boundary layers for restoration, protection, and sample-plot areas associated with pilot monitoring contexts. These layers provide a usable spatial scaffold for chapter maps and for the preparation of areas of interest to be uploaded into an EOS Data Analytics (EOSDA) Crop Monitoring workspace.

However, boundary assets are not equivalent to full drone deliverable packages. In particular, current files do not yet provide complete orthomosaic outputs, metadata-rich mission documentation, or full before/after visual panels.

The current repository evidence is strongest for named polygons and boundaries associated with Dzita, Salo, Avu, Aborlove, and the Keta Lagoon area. These files show that site-specific monitoring units have already been defined, but they do not by themselves confirm that each unit has been uploaded to EOSDA or linked to a live recurring-monitoring workflow.

## 8.3 Evidence of Monitoring Readiness
The evidence pack and monitoring-plan materials support a cautious statement of readiness rather than a claim of full operational monitoring.

Evidence already available includes:
- a Terms of Reference requirement for baseline maps with drone or aerial shots stored in a database;
- a final-report blueprint that expects orthomosaics, plot imagery, boundary refinement, and condition mapping to feed ongoing monitoring;
- a monitoring plan for Keta and Ada that proposes 57 sampling sites in Keta and 23 in Ada, with quarterly field monitoring; and
- boundary datasets that can function as the initial inventory of areas of interest for platform setup.

Together, these materials support a report statement that the project has the spatial foundations for continuous monitoring. They do not yet support a statement that the EOSDA workflow is already fully operational.

## 8.4 Continuous Monitoring Design Signals
The monitoring-plan evidence indicates map-anchored sampling design and periodic monitoring intent. This supports a defensible statement that continuous monitoring architecture has been defined at the planning level for key sites.

At this stage, the chapter should distinguish between:
- **defined monitoring structure** (supported), and
- **fully evidenced monitoring implementation outputs** (partially supported).

## 8.5 What Is Needed Before EOSDA Monitoring Can Be Reported as Operational
If the consultancy team is opening an EOSDA Crop Monitoring account and uploading the drone-survey areas of interest, the final report package should include the following status table.

The table reflects the current repository evidence snapshot. It should be updated when EOSDA setup records, upload confirmations, screenshots, or monitoring outputs are added to the reporting package.

| Required implementation item | Current status in repository | Reporting implication |
|---|---|---|
| EOSDA account opened and configured | Not yet evidenced in current repository files | Treat as pending until setup details are available |
| Areas of interest uploaded from approved polygons | Not yet evidenced in current repository files | State planned upload workflow, not completed hosting |
| Screenshots or exported views from EOSDA | Not yet evidenced in current repository files | Add as figure evidence before claiming active monitoring |
| Monitoring update cycle and responsible users | Planning-level evidence only | Describe intended workflow and institutional next steps |

This distinction is important for both the Final Report and any companion Drone Survey report. It allows the authoring layer to say clearly what has been prepared, what still needs to be done, and what managers should expect next.

## 8.6 Linkage to MRV and Management Use
Drone-linked boundaries and recurring monitoring plans are intended to feed broader monitoring and reporting systems by improving spatial specificity and tracking readiness. This creates a practical bridge to the MRV chapter.

The chapter does not claim that all components of this bridge are already fully operational across all sites.

## 8.7 Standalone Drone Survey Report Use
If a separate Drone Survey report is required, this chapter can serve as its core narrative, but the standalone report should add:
1. a table of all approved areas of interest and source boundary files;
2. any orthomosaic outputs, plot imagery, or condition-mapping visuals approved for publication;
3. EOSDA account and upload status notes;
4. screenshots or workflow evidence showing how recurring observation will be carried out; and
5. a short action list for handover to managers and development partners.

Until those inputs are available, the standalone report should be drafted as a readiness and setup report rather than as proof of fully functioning continuous monitoring.
