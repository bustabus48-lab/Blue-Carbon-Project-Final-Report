## 6.1 Overview
The socio-economic baseline evidence indicates strong dependence of coastal communities on mangrove-linked and wetland-linked livelihoods across profiled landscapes. This dependency is accompanied by differentiated governance readiness, social inclusion gaps, and varying local risk profiles.

The chapter is drafted as a baseline narrative rather than a policy prescription section.

## 6.2 Livelihood Dependence and Pressure Dynamics
Available evidence indicates high livelihood reliance on activities linked to mangrove and wetland systems, including fish processing and fuelwood use in key areas. This reliance is not uniform across all locations and should be interpreted by site context rather than as a single national behavioral profile.

Pressure dynamics are therefore presented as socio-ecological feedbacks: livelihood needs shape extraction behavior, and ecosystem decline in turn increases vulnerability.

## 6.3 Demographic and Inclusion Context
The baseline evidence highlights demographic pressure, youth livelihood constraints, and recurring inclusion gaps affecting women, migrants, and persons with disabilities in formal decision processes.

These findings are relevant not only for safeguards and equity framing, but also for implementation realism: governance systems that do not include key resource users face higher compliance and sustainability risk.

## 6.4 Governance Readiness and Institutional Variation
Readiness for community-based governance structures is presented as uneven across sites. The chapter therefore avoids “single readiness” language and emphasizes staged implementation logic.

This variation has direct implications for sequencing: areas with stronger cohesion and active local structures may support faster operationalization, while contested landscapes require conflict-sensitive preparatory work.
