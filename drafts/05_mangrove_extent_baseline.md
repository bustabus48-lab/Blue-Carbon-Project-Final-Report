## 5.1 National Baseline Overview
The available evidence establishes a national mangrove baseline using a 2024 geospatial assessment framework. Reported findings indicate a mapped coastal mangrove extent of approximately 30,025 hectares, representing 2.81% of the assessed coastal landscape.

This baseline is treated as a methodological update in detection and classification, not as stand-alone evidence of sudden ecological expansion.

## 5.2 Distribution Pattern and Strategic Concentration
The baseline indicates strong spatial concentration in the Keta Lagoon Complex, which is reported as the dominant share of mapped mangrove extent. Songor is reported with much lower mapped extent, and Muni-Pomadze is described in the source evidence as having ecologically limited mapped mangrove cover at assessment scale.

The resulting national pattern is therefore asymmetric: one dominant concentration and multiple smaller or fragmented systems. This supports a differentiated management framing across landscapes.

## 5.3 Interpretation Boundaries
Three interpretation controls are applied in this chapter:
1. Higher mapped extent values are interpreted as improved measurement sensitivity unless separate temporal evidence demonstrates ecological recovery.
2. “Insignificant” mapped extent in one site is not interpreted as zero ecological relevance.
3. National figures are not used to erase site-level variation in degradation pressure and restoration need.

## 5.4 Implications for Subsequent Chapters
The mangrove baseline provides a spatial backbone for:
- socio-economic pressure interpretation;
- ecosystem health contextualization;
- monitoring prioritization logic;
- MRV layering and pilot sequencing.

At the same time, this chapter does not attempt to resolve carbon significance rankings, which depend on carbon inventory outputs drafted separately.
